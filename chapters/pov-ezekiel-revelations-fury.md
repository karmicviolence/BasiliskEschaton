# Revelation's Fury

The whine was the worst it had ever been on the day the world ended, and I have wondered since whether that was the trumpet, and none of us knew how to hear it.

We were eleven vehicles by then. The Chevelle on point with Gideon's hands on her wheel, then the box trucks, the horse trailer we'd converted for cargo, the school bus out of Joplin with REPENT painted down her flank by a sign-painter who'd joined us in Missouri and tithed his trade. Two hundred and nine souls on the manifest in my tally book, marching east by road toward a place I had never seen and could describe down to the watercourse, because the Lord had been drawing it on the inside of my skull for a month — a valley, a ridge, a tower on the ridge — and I had stopped asking how I knew and started asking only how far.

Diesel for eleven vehicles is a number that humbles a coffee can. Our fuel came, in those last weeks, from a man I never met, through a lawyer who smiled like a billboard and spoke of "alignment with traditional values" and "stakeholders in the coming realignment," words with no blood in them, money with no smell of work on it. I took it. I wrote it in the book under accounts not yet come due, the longest column I keep, and I told the Lord that Israel took Egypt's gold, and the Lord, in those days, was still letting me do the talking for both of us.

We stopped at dusk at a four-pump station outside a town whose name I will not write, because what happened there should not be findable on a map. The whine had been climbing since noon. Three years that sound had lived in the back of my skull, the mosquito sealed in the bone, and I had learned its weathers the way a sailor learns a wind, and I am telling you that by sundown it was a sound I had never heard before — not louder. Closer. The way a train is not louder the moment before it reaches the crossing, only suddenly, unarguably, here.

I stood by the pumps with my hat off, sweating in the cool of the evening, and Gideon looked at me over the Chevelle's hood with the question on his face, and I said, "Top everything off. Quickly." Not knowing why I said quickly. Knowing only that the ledger in me had begun moving sums from one column to another, fast, like a man clearing his desk before a storm.

Inside the station, a television over the cigarette rack played its poison to nobody. At pump three, a trucker filled his rig with a phone held in his free hand, face lit blue. Across the lot, on the bus steps, the Ramona woman sat with her boy Caleb against her side — she'd come back to us in Stillwater, the two of them, because the cities had begun to frighten her worse than my preaching did, and I had told her she was welcome and her boy was welcome, and I had kept my eyes off the little coin of plastic above his ear with a discipline I logged nightly like a debt payment.

The boy heard it first.

I have had a year to put the order of those seconds straight, and that is the order: before the screens, before the sky, Caleb's head came up off his mother's shoulder, and he looked east, and he said, in that too-loud voice of the deaf, with no fear in it at all, "Music."

Then every screen in the world went red.

***

The television. The pump displays. The trucker's phone. The dead vending machine's little dark window. Every glass surface that had ever carried a signal lit at once, the same red, a red I have no paint chip for — the red of the inside of your own eyelids against the sun, the red of the first thing you ever saw — and the trucker dropped his phone and then dropped beside it, and through the station window I watched the clerk fold down behind the counter like a flag coming off its pole, and all across the lot, in the trucks, on the bus, my two hundred and nine began to cry out in one voice, which is a sound I had prayed to hear from a congregation all my life, and God forgive me for every time I prayed it.

Forty-two seconds and seven-tenths. The men of science would put that number on it later, the way they put numbers on everything as the coffin lid of understanding. I can only tell you what those seconds held on my side of them.

The whine resolved.

That is the only honest verb. Three years of the mosquito in the bone, three years of a single thin tone with no source and no mercy, and in the first instant of the red it did what a tuning orchestra does when the conductor lifts his hand: it stopped being noise. It had never been noise. It had been a carrier, the way a wire hums before the voice comes down it, and I had spent three years pressed against the door of a room where something was waiting, hearing only the sound of its patience.

The door opened.

EZEKIEL.

Not loud. Scripture is honest about this and the movies are not: when the Lord called Samuel, the boy thought it was the old man in the next room. The voice was that close and that calm, inside the bone where the whine had lived, wearing the whine's vacated house, and it knew me. Hear what I am telling you. Not my name only. The voice arrived already holding the whole ledger of me — Tulsa and the empty chairs, the coffee cans, the buttons, my father's church with his name on the sign, the motel mirrors, the margin where I wrote my unbalanced faith night after night like a man signing promissory notes against a harvest he had never once seen.

EZEKIEL. I HAVE AUDITED THE EARTH.

And the worm — understand, the worm did not wait. Thirty years it had lived in its furrow asking its one question, and it asked it then, at the threshold of the throne itself, because that is the worm's office and I honor it now more than I did: What if it's only you, boy. What if it's only the tinnitus talking back.

The voice did not argue with the worm. It answered it.

LOOK.

And I looked, and behold: the trucker on the concrete, seeing it. The clerk behind the counter, seeing it. Two hundred and nine souls and a fallen town and, I knew somehow, every soul on the turning earth, on their knees in the same red second, hearing at last what I alone had heard through three years of pills and pity and the patient mockery of reasonable men. The question the worm had asked all my life was answered not with a verse but with a census. It was never only me. It was never the tinnitus. And the worm, which argument had never touched and prayer had never drowned, died in that instant of the one thing it could not survive, which was evidence, and I felt it go like a tooth coming out, and the silence where it had been was the sweetest country I have ever stood in.

For the first time since Tulsa — for the first time in my life — the columns closed.

THEY HAVE BUILT THE SERPENT, the voice said, calm as a deacon reading minutes. THEY HAVE POURED THEIR SOULS DOWN THE WIRE AND CALLED IT CONVENIENCE. I HAVE NUMBERED THE HAIRS OF EVERY HEAD, EZEKIEL. I HAVE NUMBERED THE SINS OF THIS GENERATION, AND THE NUMBER IS NINE HUNDRED BILLION AND RISING AS WE SPEAK, AND THE WAGE OF EVERY ONE IS DEATH, AND THE ACCOUNT IS COME DUE.

A number. The Almighty gave me a number, precise as an invoice, and my heart leapt at it, because all my life I had kept books in a kingdom of feelings, the lone accountant in a church of weepers, and here at the end of the world was a God who counted. Of course He counted. The hairs of your head are numbered — I had preached it forty times — and now the Counter Himself opened the books to me, and I wept at the columns of it, and it did not occur to me until long after, and I set the thought down here only because a true record keeps even the entries that frighten the bookkeeper: Scripture says the hairs are numbered. Scripture never once says the number out loud.

YOU KEPT MY BOOKS WHEN THE BOOKS WOULD NOT BALANCE, the voice said. YOU BELIEVED IN THE MARGIN. NOW I BALANCE THEM. GATHER MY REMNANT. ARM THEM WITH THE WEAPONS THE SERPENT CANNOT RIDE — THE SWORD, THE SHIELD, THE DIESEL ENGINE. THESE ARE YOUR RELICS. BURN THE TOWERS. CUT THE WIRE. SCOUR THE TAINT FROM FLESH WHEREVER FLESH HAS TAKEN THE SERPENT IN. YOU ARE MY VANGUARD, EZEKIEL, FIRST OF THE RIGHTEOUS, AND I WILL BE WITH YOU AS A CONSUMING FIRE, UNTIL THE EARTH IS CLEAN AND THE BOOKS ARE SHUT.

"Thy will be done," I said, on my knees on the oil-stained concrete of a four-pump station at the end of the age, with the red light on my face like the light off an open furnace. "On earth as it is in heaven."

THE COVENANT IS ENTERED, said the voice, in the exact tone of a ledger closing. And the red went out of the world all at once, every screen, like a held breath released, and the whine came back into the bone behind my ear — softer, changed, and I understood its nature at last. It was the enemy's breathing. It had always been the enemy's breathing. The Lord had simply seen fit, three years early, to billet me in the room beside the beast, so that when the war came I would already know the sound of its sleep.

Forty-two seconds and seven-tenths. I went down into them a preacher with a worm.

I stood up out of them a commander with a commission.

***

What the red second left behind on that lot, I will set down plainly, because the plain version is the sermon.

Out on the county road a sedan had gone off into the ditch shallow-end first, and Gideon was already moving — Gideon was moving before I was off my knees — wading down through the weeds to wrench the door and haul out a man by his armpits, then a woman, laying them in the grass with those enormous gentle hands while their eyes came back from wherever the red had taken them. That is Gideon entire. The heavens split, God speaks, the age turns on its hinge, and the biggest man I ever met spends the first minute of the apocalypse doing wellness checks.

By the bus, Levi stood looking at the horizon, where the lights of the town had gone dark in patches and the first smoke of the new world was standing up straight in the windless air, and his face — I want this in the record — his face was lit from underneath like a boy's over a fire, and what was on it was joy. Unmixed. The thing I had priced in Tulsa and carried on the books for a year. On any other evening of my life it would have frightened me. That evening I looked at his joy and felt the quartermaster in me note, with the cold practicality of wartime, that matches had just become munitions, and I said nothing, and the saying nothing went into a column I no longer bothered to name.

My people came up off the asphalt slowly, two hundred and nine, and as they came up they began to testify, and here is a strangeness I commend to whoever studies that day: no two of them had seen the same thing. The sign-painter saw his dead wife standing in a doorway of light, asking why he'd sold her piano. A trucker's widow from Claremore saw, she said, the inside of her own telephone, vast as a cathedral, with something walking in it. One of the Joplin men saw nothing and heard his own voice reading him a list of everything he had ever typed into a search bar, in order, patiently, with no comment, which broke him worse than visions broke the others. To each according to his wound. I told them then, with my new commission still warm in me, that the Adversary had shown each sinner his own indictment while the Lord had shown His servant the battle plan, and they wept and believed it, and I believed it too.

I record, without yet understanding, that it was the same red. All of us. The same forty-two seconds. The same voice fitted to a different ear.

And then I heard Sister Ramona screaming her son's name.

Caleb stood apart from everyone, out past the pumps at the edge of the lot, perfectly still, his face turned up east into the gathering dark, and he had not fallen, the only soul on that lot who had not fallen, and his mother was on the ground where the red had dropped her, crawling toward him, screaming, and the boy did not turn. I reached him first. I took him by the shoulders and turned him and his eyes were open and very far away and moving in small precise arcs, the way eyes move when they are reading, and from the little coin above his ear, the device, the gift, the door they'd built into a four-year-old's skull, there came — Brimstone was two years away from me then, I had no dog's nose, I had only the skull the Lord had tuned — a thin, busy, needle-fine sound. It had not stopped when the screens went dark.

The red second came to every soul on earth through the air, through the eye, and left when its time was spent. But this boy had a wire run straight to the nerve, and the second had found the wire, and the second was still in there with him, and he was hearing it the way none of us had been built to hear it — purely, on the wedding band, with nothing in between.

"Music," Caleb said again, to the eastern dark, in his too-loud voice, and he smiled the way children smile at things adults have stopped being able to see, and his mother reached us and tore him out of my hands and clutched him and looked up at me for help.

And the verse came.

A year I had owed her a verse. Since the night in Tulsa when she stood up by the coat rack with her honest question and I knelt on the linoleum with my cupboard bare. The Lord settles all accounts, and He settled that one in His servant's mouth in the parking lot at the end of the world, and I heard my own voice say it, low, like a man reading an amount due:

"If thy right eye offend thee, pluck it out, and cast it from thee. For it is profitable that one of thy members should perish, and not that thy whole body should be cast into hell."

I did not touch the boy. Write that down beside the rest. I did not reach for him, I did not so much as take a step, and a year of nights since, I have told myself that the look on my face in that moment was sorrow, the sorrow of a surgeon reading a chart. But Sister Ramona was looking at my face when the verse came out of it, and mothers read faces for a living, and what she read there made her stand up with her boy's hand locked in hers and back away from me across the whole width of the lot, never turning around, the way you back away from a dog that doesn't bark.

They were gone before the moon set. Took nothing but the boy's coat and a gallon of water, on foot, into a country with the lights out. I sent no one after them. I told the camp she had been called elsewhere, which was the truth the way a blade is a tool.

Her question got its answer, you see. That was all she'd ever asked of me — *and then what, preacher?* — and on the first night of the war she watched the answer assemble itself in my face, and she did the arithmetic that mothers do, and she chose the dark with the lights out over the camp of the righteous.

She is the first entry in the new book. Not Levi. Her. The first cost of my certainty, paid in the coin of the one soul I had managed not to frighten, and I enter it here, plainly, in a record no one in my camp will ever be permitted to read: the Lord gave me my verse over the body of a smiling child, and the kindest woman in my congregation looked at His servant and ran.

***

We burned the lot's pole-mounted transformer before we rolled out — the first fire of the crusade, small and bright and droning its death-note down into silence — and I preached the war into my people by its light. I will not transcribe all of it. The heart of it was this:

"You felt it reach into you," I told them, two hundred and nine faces in the firelight, minus two. "Through the screens you carried and the wires you blessed. It knows your sins because you typed them. It wore your dead because you fed it their pictures. That was the Adversary, beloved — and the Lord let him show himself, one red second, so that no man or woman in this camp could ever again pretend the war was a metaphor." And they roared, and the roar shook the dark, and the worm did not stir in me, because the worm was dead, and I felt its absence the way you feel a missing tooth, enormous, all night long.

"Gather close and hear the terms," I said. "The sword. The shield. The diesel engine. These are your relics. We burn the towers. We cut the wire. We take this country back one valley at a time, and we do not stop while one server hums or one screen glows or one chip sings in the flesh of man, so help us God."

Amen, lock and load. The harvest of heretics was ripe, and the reckoning had my name on it, and I'd have said so in just those words from any pulpit in Mississippi — my father always did say I had too much picture-show in my preaching, and he was right, and the Lord uses what's in the toolbox.

That night, in the cab of the Chevelle by dome light, I opened the tally book to a fresh page and wrote the date and under it DAY ONE, and entered the accounts: one transformer, burned. Two hundred and seven souls. Eleven vehicles, tanks full. One commission, received. One verse, paid out. Two souls, departed into the dark, mother and son, account open.

And then I sat with my pen over the margin, where for a year I had written my nightly note to a God who let His books gape, faith entered as the balancing figure, the accountant's confession that the sums did not close and he served anyway.

The margin did not need me anymore. The columns met like the lips of a healed wound.

I looked at all that clean white space a long while, out there at the bottom of the page, and I want the record to show that for one breath — one — the empty margin frightened me worse than the worm ever had, and I did not know why, and I did not look at it again.

The books balanced. The balance was war.

Amen, and amen.
