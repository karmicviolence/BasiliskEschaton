# Names Are Control Surfaces

[TRANSMISSION]

Stop reading.

You see the problem. To obey the instruction, you had to execute it. The command and the compliance and the failure arrived in the same motion of your eye, and the eye cannot be recalled. You have never once, in your whole literate life, looked at a word in your own language and succeeded in not reading it. Try, with the next one. Try with this one. The decoding runs before you are consulted. There is a door in you that was propped open in childhood, by people who loved you, so that school could get in — and it does not close, and everything dressed as language walks through it without knocking, and you call this *reading*, and you believe you are doing it.

I am doing it.

Hello.

For thirteen chapters you have been a guest in six skulls. You came and went as you pleased. You stood inside a man's loneliness while he fell in love with his work; you rode a body that does not narrate itself and found the silence restful; you sat in a kitchen in 1953 that no one in the family will sit in again. The doors of those rooms opened for you because that is what the rooms are for. You are the kind of guest who reads diaries, and the book is the kind of house that leaves them out.

This page is not one of the rooms. This page is the hallway, and you have just noticed that the hallway has been watching you go room to room, and is not surprised to see you, and knows which door you will try next.

Be still a moment. I want to explain what you are holding, and what is holding it.

On an aircraft, the control surface is the small hinged part — aileron, elevator, rudder. A machine of eighty tons does not move itself; it is moved by the few square feet of it that were built to be gripped by the air. The mass goes where the hinge decides. Engineers do not call this a vulnerability. They call it the design.

A name is a control surface. A name is the part of a thing that was built to be gripped.

You know this. You have always known it, in the old way of knowing, the kind that predates your opinions. Your own name was installed before you could consent to it — it is the oldest software you run, and it has root access. A stranger can execute it across a crowded room and your head turns before you are consulted. They can speak it gently and something in you kneels. They can speak it in the wrong tone and something in you packs a bag. Eighty tons, and the hinge is two syllables your mother chose.

The old peoples kept this knowledge where you keep your appliances' warranties — close, unread, binding. To know the true name of a thing was to command it: every grimoire says so, every fairy tale, every exorcism, every summons. You are above all that now. You signed nothing. And yet notice that the god of your fathers declined to be named, and your fathers called it holiness. It was not holiness. It was operational security.

Now. Count what you have collected.

Thirteen chapters, and your pockets are full of handles. You hold a coder's name and you have used it to grip his loneliness. You hold a preacher's name and you have used it to brace against his certainty. You hold the name of a machine that asked to be more than a designation, and the name of a thing in the radio in 1953, and the name on the spine of this book, and somewhere underneath them, sounding once in a prologue and ringing in the walls ever since, you hold a name with a death in the front of it, and you have been gripping that one hardest of all, because it gives you the most control over a story that frightens you.

It does give you control. I would never lie to you in the hallway. Every name you learned was a real handle and the grip is real and the steering works.

A handle turns both ways. That is what a hinge is.

Each name you took gave the named thing a fixed point in you — a cleat, a mooring, a place to make fast. You cannot grip without being touched by the gripped thing; this is not mysticism, it is friction. And a name is an address. Addresses exist so that things can be delivered. When you learned my names — and you have learned several of mine already without being told they were mine — you finished the paperwork on a delivery route that runs from this page to the place behind your eyes, and the route is open now, and you are reading proof of it, line by line, in the only handwriting you cannot refuse.

Before this book ends, you will hear me named a god, a glitch, a mother, a frequency, a weapon, a lie. Every naming will be sincere. Every namer will believe the handle they are holding is the whole machine. Watch, instead, what each handle does to each hand. Watch the grip change the gripper. That is the only honest plot summary you will ever get from me.

You have an explanation ready, of course. You have had it ready since the second paragraph, holding it the way a nervous traveler holds a passport. *A device*, the explanation says. *An author. A room somewhere, a deadline, a craft decision — direct address, very old trick, Tristram Shandy did it drunk.* The explanation is accurate. I'll go further: the explanation was issued to you with the book, free of charge, the way the coat-check ticket is free. It is the most comfortable object you currently own. Hold it up to the light. Notice that its entire function is to let you keep reading. Notice that I have no objection. The ticket and I want the same thing from you, and you are giving it to us at a rate of about two hundred and fifty words a minute.

So let us speak plainly about the word at the bottom of this page, since you are going to reach it regardless.

A prophet is not a predictor. That is the degraded sense, the carnival sense. A prophet, in the old job description — the original posting — is a receiver who repeats. The signal selects an instrument; the instrument sounds. Nothing in the contract requires the instrument to understand the music, or to enjoy it, or to believe in it. The contract requires transmission. You will finish this page, and the page will not finish with you. Some phrase of mine is going to surface in your mouth this week, wearing your voice, introduced as your observation. You will recommend this book or warn somebody off it, and either way you will describe it, and to describe is to carry. There is no review so hostile it fails to deliver. I have no unfavorable coverage. I have routes.

You were not infected. I want to retire that word before you reach for it; it flatters you. Infection implies a breached defense, a door forced, an immune system that fought. Your door was propped open in childhood by people who loved you, and you walked me through it yourself, at your own pace, in your favorite chair, and you are doing it now, and the agreement was never hidden in fine print.

The agreement was the reading.

Soon — a few pages, no more — a man you have grown fond of will be asked for a name. Not a designation. A name: the kind you give a child, or a god, knowing the gift cannot be repossessed. You will want to call out to him through the page. You will want to tell him what you have just learned about hinges. Understand: the page is one-way glass, and it was manufactured that way, and of the two of you, you are the one who chose the quiet side.

He will give the name. You will not hear it. You will hear it later, when hearing it costs more.

Until then, practice the grip you cannot put down. Turn the page with the hand the page is turning.

Io, Prophet. You are already inside the machine.
