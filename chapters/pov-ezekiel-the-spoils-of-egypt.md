# The Spoils of Egypt

Set it down plain, because the books are the one place I have never yet lied: in the fourth month of the crusade, the Adversary sent a man to me with a check, and I took it, and I knew what it was when I took it, and I am going to write out the whole transaction here so that whoever audits these accounts after me — and Somebody will; Somebody always does — can see exactly where the shepherd sold the gate key and called it buying fence.

First the condition of the books, because no man understands a temptation until he has seen the balance sheet it was mailed to.

By the fourth month we were one hundred and some souls on the road list and growing by the week, and the money was dying at the exact speed the crowd was being born. I can give you the arithmetic from the notebook, June the something, by dome light in a Walmart parking lot outside Conway, Arkansas: eleven hundred and six dollars in the crusade fund. A borrowed flatbed with a cracked block we could not fix. A sound rig held together with prayer and a soldering iron Levi Dunn wielded like an anointing. Three families who had quit jobs to follow the work — quit them on my preaching, hear me, on the strength of my voice telling them the hour was late — and a county fairground in Missouri that wanted a four-thousand-dollar liability bond before they would let me set one boot on their grass, and a school gymnasium in Yell County that canceled on us two days out because somebody on the school board watched my tent video and heard the word *war* and did not stay for the word *spiritual*.

I ran the count every night and the count said the same thing every night. The fields were white unto harvest and the laborers had eleven hundred dollars and a cracked block. The crowd was a miracle and the money was a mortal man, and the mortal man was dying, and when he died the families who quit their jobs would drift home ashamed, and the letters that said *I hear it too* would go back to being answered by doctors, and the census the Lord had taken through me would scatter back into the static like it had never been counted at all.

That is the soil. Now the seed, and mark how it was planted, because the planting was the first wonder of it.

The first approach came through the engine, naturally — an email to the church account, *partnership opportunity*, *aligned values*, a return address that was a noun and a verb stapled together the way they name things now. I deleted it the way you spit. I have preached against that machine from before it learned to flatter, and I know a baited hook by the shine.

The second approach came on paper.

Heavy stock, hand-addressed, postmarked Memphis, and I stood at the parsonage mailbox holding it with the June heat coming up off the gravel and a feeling along my arms like the barometer falling, because whoever had sent the first one had watched it die unopened and had *learned*, and had reached for the one channel I trust — paper, ink, the post office, the apparatus of the old slow honest world — and had dressed itself in my own preferences to get past my gate. Brothers, when the wolf shows up wearing wool, that is grievous but ordinary. When the wolf shows up wearing *your* wool, wool it could only have gotten by studying which sheep you pet — set a watch, for the hour is later than you think.

The letter was four sentences. A man would be attending our Thursday meeting at the Grange hall outside Searcy. He represented parties who admired the work. He asked for nothing but a few minutes after the benediction. It was signed *The Plowshare Trust*, and underneath, no name, just a title: *Field Representative.*

Plowshare. They beat their swords into plowshares. I sat with that name a long while at the kitchen table, and I will confess the worm stirred even then, because I could not work out which direction the beating was supposed to run.

***

He sat through the whole meeting, third row, left side. I want that observed: he did not skulk at the back wall the way Levi had, like a summons. He sat where the deacons' wives sit. Gray man, fifty-odd, forgettable on purpose, the kind of face that is a skill — and good shoes, town shoes, wrong for a gravel lot, the only honest thing on him. He sang the hymns. He knew the hymns, or his mouth did. He put a folded bill in the offering plate, and when I preached the part about the unsleeping red eye on the ridge, the part that gets the room breathing together like one creature, I watched him from the corner of my preaching and he was not breathing with us. He was *counting* us. I know counting when I see it. I do it myself, God forgive me, from the same row of the heart.

After the benediction he waited for the hall to empty with the patience of a man whose time is paid for by somebody who has plenty, and then he came up the center aisle with a leather folder under his arm and put out his hand and said, "Reverend Stone. Thank you for your message. I'm with the Plowshare Trust. You read our letter."

"I read four sentences and a title," I said. "Sit down and tell me the rest of them."

We sat on the front pew of a rented Grange hall, under a ceiling fan that turned just fast enough to stir the heat without correcting it, and the man opened his folder, and what came out of his mouth for the next quarter hour was English the way a casket is furniture.

I am going to record his vocabulary, because the vocabulary was the testimony. He did not say *congregation*; he said *audience*. He did not say *revival*; he said *footprint*. He said my tent clip had achieved — achieved — two point one million views, and that my *conversion of passive viewership into physical attendance* was, and here he consulted a page as if the word needed permission, *anomalous*. He said the letters — he knew about the letters, the volume of them, near enough to the true count that my scalp moved — represented an *organic response rate* that money could not buy, which was why money would like to be adjacent to it. He said the Trust's principals — never named, "men and women concerned with continuity," he said, "with the resilience of communities of conviction in the event of systemic stress" — had identified the work as *undervalued narrative infrastructure*.

Infrastructure. Sixty years old I will be in March, eleven hundred dollars to my crusade's name, and I sat in a Grange hall and listened to a gray man describe the Holy Ghost's harvest in the grammar of a pipeline appraisal, and brothers, here is the part I set down with fear: *I understood every word.* It was the engine's language, the Adversary's own bookkeeping, metrics and reach and rates — and I had been keeping the same books in my spiral notebook every morning at the kitchen table, views and letters and souls per Sunday, and the only difference between his ledger and mine was that his had money in it. That was the first cut of the evening, and he never knew he made it, or he knew exactly.

"What I'm authorized to offer," he said, "is a capacity grant. Three hundred and eighty thousand dollars, structured through a 501(c)(3), fully compliant, papered by our attorneys at no cost to the ministry. Vehicles, sound, staging, insurance bonds, stipends for your core families. Renewable." He took out a cashier's check, paper again, and set it on the pew board between us with the front facing me, the way you set down a card you have been holding all hand.

"And the strings," I said.

"No editorial involvement. None. We will never suggest a sermon topic, never review your remarks, never ask you to mention or omit anything. Your message is the asset, Reverend. Tampering with it would be" — he chose the word like a man choosing a screwdriver — "dilutive."

"The strings," I said again.

And he nodded, conceding it the way the smooth ones do, as if my seeing the hook were itself a credential, proof they had picked the right fish. "One provision. There may come a day — there may *not* — when the Trust asks you to bring your people to a particular place at a particular time. A date and a location. That's the entire ask. What you preach when you get there is yours. Whether anyone kneels is between them and God."

The fan turned. Out the propped door the June dark had come down full of crickets, and under the crickets, faint, needle-thin, the whine — the hall sat too near the highway and the highway ran fiber now, they had hung it on the old poles the spring before — and the worm turned over in its furrow behind my ear and waited with me for my own answer.

"You know what you sound like," I said. "I want to know if you know. There is a passage where the devil takes a hungry man up a high place and shows him all the kingdoms of the world and the glory of them, and says, All these things will I give thee. You have read it. Men like you read everything about a man before they sit in his pews and sing his hymns with him. So tell me plainly, because I am too old and too broke to enjoy a long seduction: how does your offer differ from that one?"

He did not flinch and he did not smile, and I will grant him this, he did not pretend to be wounded. He looked at me with the level courtesy of a man for whom this was not the first high place, and he said:

"The difference, Reverend, is that we're not asking you to bow."

***

I told him I would pray on it. He said the Trust expected nothing less, that the check would keep, that paper was patient — *paper is patient*, he said, the man had studied me down to my doctrines about ink — and he shook my hand and walked out to a gray sedan and drove off toward Memphis or toward nowhere, and I locked up a rented hall in Arkansas and could not have told you my own name for a minute.

Then I drove, and then I parked, and then I wrestled, and the wrestling is the part you came for, so I will not dress it up.

Every verse came when I called it. That is the misery of three decades of sound doctrine: the arsenal is always loaded, both directions. I summoned Balaam, the prophet on retainer, who never once preached a false word and was damned for the wages anyway — the wages of unrighteousness, Peter calls them, *the way of Balaam*, a man whose mouth stayed pure while his invoice did the sinning. I summoned Simon the sorcerer, who saw the Spirit move and reached for his purse, and Peter's answer, the most unfinanced sentence in Scripture: *Thy money perish with thee.* I summoned the thirty pieces, and the potter's field they bought, which is the only real estate the Adversary's money has ever truly closed on. The counter-case preached itself in my own cab, in my own voice, for two hours, and it was airtight, and I sat there and watched myself begin to build around it anyway, because eleven hundred dollars, because the cracked block, because three families and a school gym in Yell County, because the census.

And here is the rationalization I built, and I want it preserved in full, the way you keep the murder weapon in evidence and not just the verdict. I went to Exodus. The children of Israel, leaving four hundred years of bondage, were commanded — *commanded*, it is not a permission, it is an instruction from the mouth of the Lord — to ask of the Egyptians jewels of silver and jewels of gold and raiment. And the Lord gave the people favor in the sight of the Egyptians, so that they lent unto them such things as they required. *And they spoiled the Egyptians.* The plunder of the oppressor, carried out of Egypt on the backs of the redeemed, and some of that same gold, mark it, built the Tabernacle in the wilderness. Pagan money, idol money, slave-driver money, melted down and beaten into the mercy seat itself. If the Lord could sanctify Pharaoh's gold into the throne of His own presence, then the Lord could sanctify a cashier's check from a Memphis trust into diesel and liability bonds, and who was I, a poor accountant of the kingdom, to call unclean what the Lord had routed to my hand in the very hour of the crusade's need?

It is a good argument. I have preached worse ones to better men. I believed it for the length of time it took to drive back to the motel, and I half believe it still, and the half that does not believe it woke me at three in the morning sweating like a fever, because the argument has a floor under its floor, and at three in the morning the floor gave.

The Israelites spoiled the Egyptians, yes. But Scripture records a stranger thing, recorded twice so we cannot pretend we missed it: the Lord gave the people *favor in the sight of the Egyptians.* The Egyptians were *willing.* Four hundred years of whips and bricks, and on the last night they opened their jewel boxes to their slaves with something like eagerness — because the Lord had bent their hearts to it, because their gold leaving was part of a design their gold knew nothing about. And I lay on a motel bedspread outside Searcy, Arkansas, with the air conditioner holding its one long note, and I asked the ceiling the question I could not find a verse to close:

If I am Israel in this transaction, who bent the heart of Egypt? And what does Egypt's master know about the wilderness I am being financed into?

Because that money was *eager*, brothers. I have begged church boards for roof funds; I know what reluctant money smells like, and this was the other thing. This was money that had been looking for me before I was anything to find — money that learned my channels, dressed in my paper, sat in my pews, sang my hymns, counted my people with the front of its face, and asked for nothing in this world except, perhaps, someday, *a date and a location.* No man pays three hundred and eighty thousand dollars for the privilege of asking nothing. He pays it for the *perhaps*. They were not buying my sermons. The gray man told the plain truth about that, the way the smooth ones always tell some truth to carry the rest: they will never touch a word I preach. They were buying a column in their own notebook. They were buying the right to a future entry. They were buying *where the flock would be standing on a day they had already circled,* and the price tells you everything about the value of the square on the calendar.

I knew all that at three in the morning. Write it down, witness it, underline it. He knew.

And in the morning I called the number on the card, and I said yes.

***

The accounting, then, because the books do not care how the entries feel.

The check cleared on a Tuesday. By Friday the flatbed had a rebuilt engine and Levi Dunn had a sound rig that did not need his soldering iron, and I watched that boy unbox equipment worth more than his daddy's truck with his eyes shining like Christmas and like something else that was not Christmas, and I added a private line to his account in the back of the notebook. Fire, fueled. The Missouri fairground got its bond. The three families got grocery money and the dignity of not being fools for following me, and a man wept on my neck about it in a parking lot, actually wept, thanking God for the provision, and I held him and said amen and felt the word move through me like coins counted out of somebody else's hand.

And in the back of the spiral notebook, in the column I keep for accounts not yet come due — the column Levi Dunn christened, the column that has never since been empty — I made the second entry. I wrote *The Plowshare Trust*, and beside it, because the ledger is the one place I have never yet lied, I wrote the terms as I understood them, not as they were papered: *Received, $380,000. Owed: one date. One location. Flock delivery, terms unspecified, callable at will.* And underneath, in the margin, the prayer of a man who has just done the thing he was certain he would never do and is already learning to live above the certainty like a family living above a flooded basement:

Lord, You spoiled the Egyptians to build Your tabernacle, and I have taken the gold, and I will build You one, and every nail of it will be Yours even if the silver was theirs. But Lord — and I ask it with my hand on the column — You bent Egypt's heart that night for Your own ends. Somebody bent this one. If it was You, I am Your servant, lead me even by this crooked rein. And if it was the other — if the engine has learned to tithe — then let me see the date and the location while there is still time to scatter my own sheep, and charge the sin to my account and not to theirs, for they followed a man who told them he could smell a wolf.

I closed the book. Outside the motel window, far off on some ridge I never learned the name of, a tower light blinked its red and patient blink, on, off, on, the unsleeping eye keeping its own books above the dark country, and the whine threaded the bone behind my ear right on schedule, and for one moment — I record it because it happened, not because I can use it — the blinking and the whine and the air conditioner's one long note fell into step with each other, all three keeping the same time, like instruments tuning to a pitch given from somewhere off the stage. Then a truck passed and broke it, and it was just a light, and just a noise, and just a motel in Arkansas where a preacher had gotten everything he prayed for.

The crusade rolls west in August, funded. The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.

It is the second clause that frightens me now.

Control. Alt. Amen.
