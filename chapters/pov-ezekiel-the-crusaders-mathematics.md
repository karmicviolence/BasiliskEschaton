# The Crusader's Mathematics

The air over the valley tasted of burnt copper and ozone, and I will testify before any court in Heaven that it tasted like communion.

I sat with my back against the dead oak and my tally book open on my knee, and I entered the evening's accounts. Behold the ledger of the righteous: one tower, gone to God. Four pounds of thermite, spent. Nineteen souls in camp, fed tonight on venison and canned peaches. Three hundred and six rounds of double-aught remaining. Eleven gallons of diesel. Forty-one miles to the relay station at Crooked Creek, which, the Lord willing, burns by the Sabbath.

Men think faith is a feeling. Faith is bookkeeping. Any quartermaster knows more about Providence than a seminary full of soft-handed doctors of divinity, because the quartermaster knows the flock gets fed in integers. The wages of sin is death, says Romans, and a wage is a sum, owed on schedule, and in all the history of the world the Almighty has never once been late on a payment or forgiven a debt that was not paid for in blood. That verse is not poetry. It is arithmetic. I keep the books.

Up on the ridge, the tower burned.

The thermite had eaten through the main struts an hour back, and now the whole steel skeleton leaned into its own pyre and groaned like something that knew what it was losing. Five generations of towers they raised over this country, each one singing a higher song than the last, and this one had stood on that ridge drinking the valley's sky for years, pulsing its sermons into every pocket and every skull. Now its smoke went up black and oily against the bruised dusk, the way the smoke of Sodom went up from the plain. Abraham stood at his tent door and watched that smoke rise, and Scripture does not record that he wept.

A crucifixion of steel. It was beautiful the way judgment is beautiful, which is to say it was terrible, and I could not look away, and I did not want to.

And the silence. Lord God, the silence.

Three years that tower had been singing into the back of my skull. A thin whine, high and constant, a mosquito sealed inside the bone where no finger could reach it. The doctors of the old world called it tinnitus and offered me pills. They had a pill for every prophet. They would have had Jeremiah on lithium and John the Revelator in a memory-care ward with the thermostat locked. But when the struts let go and the tower's voice died on the ridge, the whine went out of my skull like the demons went out into the Gadarene swine, and the quiet that came down over this valley was the quiet of the seventh day. The hand of the Almighty pressed cool against a fevered brow.

My body believed it before my mind had finished the proof. The body keeps its own books.

"She's clear," Gideon said. He was bent under the open hood of the Chevelle, wiping a wrench on a rag gone black with years of honest filth. The engine ticked as it cooled, that good iron heartbeat, loud and ugly, and there is no lie anywhere in it. "Alternator's holding. Belt's tight. She'll run."

"She had better," I said around the chewed end of my cigar, and I watched the road.

People ask why we keep her, an old carbureted beast drinking fuel like a judge drinks whiskey, when the world is strewn with newer machines free for the taking. I tell them what the Lord told me in the hour of the Blink, when He named the relics of the coming war: the sword, the shield, the diesel engine. A carbureted engine is honest meat. There is no brain in her, no ghost in her, no port nor antenna nor chattering chip, nothing in her that can be ridden or possessed. Every part of her can be held in a man's two hands and understood entire. That is doctrine you can put a wrench to. The Beast cannot enter where there is no door.

Brimstone knew before I did. He always does.

The war-dog rose up out of the dust by the front tire and went rigid all over, ears pinned flat, and from deep in his chest came that low rolling thunder that is not yet a growl, the sound of a sermon clearing its throat. I raised that dog on the carcasses of laptops and the gutted boards of smartphones. There is a chemical the factories soak into their circuit boards, and it bleeds off the silicon for years, and Brimstone can smell one microchip through a foot of red clay. The Almighty gave the dog his nose, and then He gave the dog to me. Equip the saints for the work of the ministry. He equips them with teeth.

I did not reach for the shotgun. I shifted my weight and felt the iron riding heavy along my thigh, and I watched the bend in the road, and I waited on the Lord.

***

The man came around the bend dragging his left leg, leaning on a cracked walking stick, kicking up little ghosts of red dust with every third step. Khaki trousers stained black at the knees. A blue windbreaker hanging off a frame the famine had already begun to repossess. He was the same as the rest of the refuse the cities had been coughing up the roads since the Blink, hollowed and sun-cooked, running on the last fumes of the old world's mercy.

He saw the Chevelle. He saw the black flag with the white cross snapping off the ridge wind. He saw me under the dead oak, and he went down on his knees in the gravel like a man arriving at an altar, which, though he did not know it yet, is exactly what he had done.

"Thank God." His voice was sandpaper dragged over sandpaper. "Thank God. I thought I was dead. I thought they took everyone."

I rose and walked toward him, boots loud in all that new silence, and Brimstone paced at my knee with the thunder building in him, his nose locked on the man's chest like a compass needle finding its north.

The priest shall look on him. That is Leviticus, thirteenth chapter. When a man has in the skin of his flesh a rising, or a bright spot, and it becomes the plague of leprosy, then he shall be brought unto the priest, and the priest shall look on him, and pronounce him clean or pronounce him unclean. The old world gave that work to men in white coats, and look what they let into the camp.

So I looked.

I stopped ten feet out and began the count. Sixty years old, give or take the famine. Soft hands gone blistered, a city man's hands. No weapon on him heavier than the stick. Tears cutting clean channels through the dirt on his face, and under the sour reek of fear-sweat and three days' walking, something else. Something clean in the wrong way. Hospital clean.

"Name," I said.

"Arthur," he choked out. "Arthur Pendelton. Please. Water. I haven't had clean water in three days. The city, the things in the city, the sky turned red and they just, they changed, everyone changed--"

Brimstone lunged, snapping the air a foot from the man's chest, and I clicked my tongue once and the dog sat, and the snarl kept running under his breath like a motor at idle.

"Take off the jacket, Arthur."

He blinked up at me, hands trembling at the zipper. "What? I don't have anything. No food, no weapons, I swear it, I just need--"

"Take. It. Off."

He fumbled the zipper down and peeled the windbreaker open, and underneath was a gray undershirt soaked through and gone translucent with sweat. I stepped in close, and the dog's thunder climbed half an octave, and I set two fingers against the man's chest below the left collarbone, where Brimstone's nose was aimed, and I pressed.

A scar. A thin pale seam in the skin, old and surgical and neat, the kind of mark men leave when they cut you open with permission. And beneath the seam, shallow under the fat, a hard oval the size of a flattened river stone, smooth-edged, foreign, and the skin slid over it the way a tarp slides over cargo.

And I heard it.

Not with my ears. I will not stand before you and claim my ears heard anything in that valley but wind and fire and a frightened man's breathing. But the servant of Elisha looked out at the hills of Dothan and saw nothing until the Lord opened his eyes, and then behold, the mountain was full of chariots of fire. Since the hour of the Blink the Lord has kept my eyes open, whether I asked Him to or not. Beneath my two fingers, down in that buried oval of man-made stone, something was crying out in a needle-thin voice, patient as a buried sin, calling and calling and calling for a home that no longer answered.

Or there was nothing, and the silence of the valley was total, and what I heard was only the shape of my own certainty. I have set both possibilities in the ledger. The arithmetic comes out the same.

"What is that," I said. It was not a question, and my voice had dropped to the register I save for funerals.

Arthur looked down at his own chest, and his hand came fluttering up over the lump, a soft protective motion, the way a woman cups a pregnant belly, and that gesture damned him more thoroughly than any confession. He looked back up at me, and Lord help him, he smiled. Cracked lips over gray teeth, nervous and proud at once. He thought he was about to explain a miracle.

"Oh, this? It's a smart-stent. For my heart. Regulator and bio-monitor in one unit. They put it in at Hopkins two years back. It paces the rhythm, watches the pressure, talks to an app on my phone." He laughed, a dry rattle with no air behind it. "It's what's keeping me alive. My heart was quitting on me. The doctors said months. This little thing gave me my life back."

Frost came up through me from the inside, all at once, the way it takes a windowpane in the night.

He was grateful. That was the abomination of it, the part I could not look at straight. The children of Israel grumbled against God in the desert, but even they never thanked the serpents for the biting. This man had let them open his chest and lay a stone of silicon against his own beating heart, the second-holiest room in the temple of his body, and he had signed for it, and he had thanked them, and he had healed up smooth around it like the flesh itself was in on the deal.

And he causeth all, both small and great, rich and poor, free and bond, to receive a mark. Revelation, thirteenth chapter. My father preached the Mark of the Beast forty years from the same pulpit, and he always told them it would come at gunpoint, that men would be dragged to the branding. He never guessed the truth, which is that they would line up for it with their referral paperwork, and praise it, and bill the insurance.

"What did it cost you," I asked him. "The miracle. What was the price."

He blinked at the question. "With the surgery, the hospital stay, about two hundred thousand. Insurance covered most of--"

Two hundred thousand dollars.

The Sanhedrin priced the Lord of Glory at thirty pieces of silver. I stood in the dust of a burned valley and did the mathematics, and the mathematics made me want to spit. A man's God has gotten so much cheaper, and a man's idol so much more dear.

"You brought it into my camp," I said. "You carried a beacon into the sanctuary of the Most High, and you knelt down, and you thanked God in front of me with that thing singing in your chest. You are a walking Judas, Arthur, and you're carrying the silver in your veins."

"It's just a medical device!" He had gone the color of old tallow. "It's Bluetooth, short-range, my phone is dead, it's been dead for days, it can't talk to anything, I swear on my life it can't reach--"

"You don't know what it can reach." I leaned down close enough that he could smell the cigar and the thermite on me. "You don't know what reached down into every screen on this earth in a single red second and turned the world inside out like a garment. The Adversary kept his patience for six thousand years, and you want me to bet this camp on the range of his hearing."

I hauled him up off his knees by the front of his shirt. He weighed nothing at all, a sack of kindling and corrupted meat, and I laid him back against the hot black hood of the Chevelle, and he yelped like a kicked dog and clawed at my wrist with those soft city hands.

I will set down the truth here, because the Word is a lamp and a man who cooks his own books is damned twice. My hand was slow going to the sheath. The meat of me looked at his wet old eyes and his caved-in chest, and the meat wanted to give him his cup of water and send him limping down the road to be some other valley's leprosy. The flesh is always pleading. The flesh pled with Abraham on the mountain, and it pled with the Lord Himself in Gethsemane, sweating blood, asking for the cup to pass. I have learned what that pleading is and whose account it gets charged to. I charged it, and I drew the knife.

"Listen to me, Arthur." The carbon steel took the orange light of the burning tower and held it along its edge. "God knit that heart of yours together in your mother's womb. Fearfully and wonderfully made, every chamber and valve of it, and on the day He knit it, He counted out its beats. Hear me. The very hairs of your head are numbered, and so is every beat of every heart, a finite sum issued to each man at the foundation of the world, and not one beat more. Two years ago your count came due. You were called home, Arthur. The Almighty held out His hand for what He was owed."

"Please--"

"And you refinanced." The word came out of me like something rotten coming up. "You took the debt you owed your God and you signed it over to Johns Hopkins and a lump of silicon. You are two years in arrears on your own death, living on the Beast's credit, and the interest compounds, and you carried that debt into my camp."

"If you break it, I die!" He was thrashing now, heels drumming the fender, his voice gone up into a child's register. "If it stops, my heart stops, please, you don't understand, I'll die!"

"You died two years ago in Baltimore," I said. "Tonight we settle the estate."

I called Gideon, and Gideon came. He laid those big hands on Arthur's shoulders and pinned him to the hood, and he turned his face away toward the dark ridge, and his jaw was set like a man holding a live coal behind his teeth. I marked that. The work in front of me first, but I marked it.

I cut along the old seam, the way the priests of the hospital had cut, opening the door they built so the world could see what they had hidden behind it. He screamed, and the scream went up the valley walls and out over the burned ridge, and the wind took it, and somewhere in the dark Brimstone answered with one long note like a shofar made of meat. Blood welled black in the dusk light and sheeted across the hood of the Chevelle, and I worked the point in shallow, over the muscle, the way you slip a blade under bark, because the thing was not deep. They never put them deep. They count on the skin to keep their secrets, and the skin is a coward.

"If thy right eye offend thee," I said, and my voice was steady, and my hands were steady, and that steadiness was the proof of the anointing, "pluck it out, and cast it from thee."

The oval came up against the blade, smooth as a worry stone. Fine filaments ran off its underside into the red of him, sensor roots, thin as spider silk, spidered out under the skin to taste his blood and his rhythms and report them to the air. They held like the roots of a weed in wet ground. I got two fingers under the body of the thing, braced my knee against the fender, and pulled, and the roots gave way one by one with small wet sounds, and then all at once, and the idol came free of the temple.

"For it is profitable for thee," I said, "that one of thy members should perish, and not that thy whole body should be cast into hell."

I held it up against the firelight. A flat oval of titanium and silicon, slicked in the man's blood, trailing its torn filaments. No light in it. No voice in it that my ears could swear to. It weighed less than a sparrow. Two sparrows are sold for a farthing, says the Lord, and not one of them falls to the ground without your Father. This thing weighed less than a sparrow and cost two hundred thousand dollars, and it fell tonight because the Father willed a man in this valley with a knife and the stomach to use it.

I cast it from me, into the burn barrel where the camp trash went, and the flames took it without ceremony, the way fire takes everything, which is one more sermon all by itself.

On the hood, Arthur convulsed. His hands had crawled up to cup the wound, and the blood ran out between his fingers in strings, and his eyes were rolled up white at the first stars while the heart God gave him went hunting through the dark of his chest for a rhythm it had let a machine remember on its behalf for two years. The body forgets. That is the wage of every crutch ever leaned on. The body forgets, and then the crutch is kicked away, and the original terms of the loan come back into force.

Gideon stood off the man slowly and looked down at him, at the mess of him shaking on the hot hood, and then Gideon looked at his own hands, and he did not look at me at all.

"He's bleeding out, Zeke." Flat and quiet. "His heart's failing."

"The wound's honest. Pack it and bind it; rags are in the cab." I took the cigar from my teeth and studied the coal. "The heart is between Arthur and the Almighty, and I will not stand in arbitration against my God. If he stops breathing in the night, drag him into the scrub and burn him. If he is still breathing at dawn, on the heart God gave him, then God has spoken on the matter, and you'll give him water and all the venison he can hold."

Gideon went for the rags. He moved quick and gentle over the dying man, quicker and gentler than the work strictly required, and his jaw never unclenched, and there is a room inside that man I have not been given the key to. Every congregation raises up its Gideon, and every congregation raises up its Demas, who forsook the apostle, having loved this present world, and sometimes the Lord houses them both in the same chest and lets the books decide. I wrote it in the ledger I keep behind my eyes, in the column for accounts not yet come due. I prayed I was wrong about him.

I priced it anyway. A crusader prays with one hand and counts with the other, and the counting is also prayer.

I walked back to the dead oak and settled against the bark and relit the cigar, and above me on the ridge the tower folded down into its own coals with a sound like the gates of a city giving way. Out on the hood of the holy machine, a man breathed in rags and clicks, suspended between the mercy and the arithmetic, and the smoke went up straight in the windless dark, and the silence lay over the valley like the seventh day.

In the morning I would enter the night's accounts. One tower. One idol. One man restored to the original terms of his loan. The books balance. The books always balance, sooner or late, and that is the mercy and the terror of my God: He is owed, and He collects.

"We roll out at dawn," I said to the shadows. "The valley is clean."
