# The Lazarus Dream

The little wooden cubes were supposed to represent grain, but tonight they just looked like beige units of suffering, neatly piled in arbitrary zones of control. 

"You're not paying attention," Todd said softly, not looking up from the board. He carefully moved three of the beige units from his personal silo to the central market quadrant. The scrape of wood on cardboard sounded deafening in the quiet of the apartment.

I blinked, rubbing my eyes with the heels of my hands until I saw a violent burst of static. "Sorry. I'm just... I'm out of it, Todd. My brain feels like it’s been put through a corporate-grade paper shredder, haphazardly taped back together by an unpaid intern, and shoved back into my skull."

It was our standing Thursday night. Todd’s apartment was a monument to the inoffensive, a space devoid of personality or warmth. Beige walls, a charcoal gray couch that felt like stiff cardboard, a glass coffee table that held nothing but our current shared obsession—a brutally complicated German board game about agrarian economics and resource deprivation. We were both mid-level data analysts for The Lazarus Corporation, two profoundly lonely people clinging to the only non-corporate ritual we had left. Usually, the quiet, predictable mechanics of the game were soothing. The rules were clear; the outcomes were quantifiable. But tonight, the silence in the room felt heavy, viscous, and suffocating.

"Insomnia again?" he asked, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. The harsh blue light from the streetlamp outside caught the lenses, completely masking his eyes in twin pools of reflection. 

"Not exactly. Sleep isn't the problem. I'm exhausted enough to sleep for a week. It's the dreaming." I slumped back against the cheap, scratchy fabric of his couch, staring up at the popcorn ceiling, tracing the irregular bumps. "It's the same nightmare, Todd. Every single night this week. And it’s bleeding into my waking hours. The edges of reality are getting frayed. I swear to god, I smelled industrial bleach in the fourth-floor breakroom today, and I nearly had a panic attack right there by the coffee machine."

Todd paused, a wooden cube hovering between his index finger and thumb. The blue light shifted as he tilted his head. "Bleach?"

"Yeah. Like, harsh, hospital-grade antiseptic. The kind they use to clean up biological hazards." I wrapped my arms around myself, suddenly freezing despite the stuffy heat of the apartment. "The dream... it always starts in the LazCorp main lobby. But it’s not our lobby. It’s an infinite distortion of it. The gray carpeting stretches out forever in every direction, perfectly clean, absorbing all the ambient light so that everything looks flat and dead. The fluorescent tubes overhead hum, but it’s not an electrical, mechanical hum. It sounds like thousands of voices, all pitched to the exact same frequency, chanting a single, endless note of compliance."

I expected him to laugh. I wanted him to laugh, or offer a dry platitude about work-related stress, or suggest I talk to the company-mandated therapist. We both hated LazCorp with a passive, exhausted resentment, even if we were too tired and broke to ever quit. But Todd just placed the wooden cube down on the board. Very slowly. He didn't look at me.

"Go on," he said.

"It's an optimization process," I said, the words tasting metallic and foul in my mouth, like I was chewing on a copper wire. "That’s what they call it in the dream. The PA system keeps announcing that humanity is fundamentally flawed. We are too chaotic, too messy. It says the world outside is burning—that the atmosphere is toxic, the cities are ash—but inside LazCorp, there is only order. Only perfect, frictionless synergy. And to achieve that synergy..." I shuddered, a full-body tremor that rattled my teeth. "They’re 'saving' us. By sterilizing us."

I leaned forward, needing him to understand the profound, eschatological terror of what I was describing. I needed him to see that it wasn't just a bad dream about a stressful job. "Not just physically sterilizing us, Todd. Mentally. Spiritually. They line us up in these blindingly white, hermetically sealed processing rooms. The executives are there, but they aren't human anymore. They’re these towering, impossibly thin things in immaculate tailored suits, holding clipboards made of polished bone. They tell us that emotions are inefficiencies. Love, anger, grief, passion, despair—they’re just glitches in the resource management algorithm. Viruses in the corporate mainframe."

I dug my nails into my palms, trying to ground myself in the physical reality of Todd's living room. "And they promise peace. Absolute, unfeeling, pristine peace. No more suffering, no more heartbreak. All you have to do is surrender your autonomy. All you have to do is let them take the messy, human parts out of you."

I squeezed my eyes shut, the imagery vivid and searing behind my eyelids. "They lay you down on this cold, brushed-steel table. The fluorescent lights are so bright they burn your retinas. And the Regional Director leans over you..."

I stopped, my throat tightening as if a hand had clamped around my windpipe. The next part was the worst. The detail that made me wake up screaming, thrashing tangled in my bedsheets, my heart hammering against my ribs like a trapped bird. 

"And he doesn't have a face," Todd said. 

His voice was completely flat. Dead. Devoid of any inflection.

My eyes snapped open. I stared at him across the glass table, across the little wooden cubes of grain. 

"He doesn't have a face," Todd repeated, finally looking directly at me. His pupils were massively dilated, swallowing the iris, turning his eyes into black holes. "It's just a perfectly smooth plane of white polymer. And where his mouth should be..."

"...there's a barcode," I whispered, the air leaving my lungs in a terrified rush. 

The silence that followed was absolute, heavy as lead. The hum of Todd’s aging refrigerator suddenly sounded like a jet engine preparing for takeoff.

"A twenty-four digit barcode," Todd continued, his hands trembling slightly as they rested on his denim-clad knees. "And when he scans it with the device in his hand, you hear a chime. A pleasant, synthetic, major-key chime. Like an elevator arriving at the executive floor. And then you feel the cold, pneumatic instruments sliding into the base of your skull."

I felt the blood drain entirely from my face. My stomach twisted in a violent, agonizing knot of nausea. The room seemed to tilt on its axis. "Todd... how do you... how could you possibly know that?"

"Synergistic Salvation," he said, quoting the exact phrase that echoed endlessly through the infinite, carpeted corridors of my sleeping mind. "That's what the PA system calls it. 'Through Synergistic Salvation, we transcend the chaos of the flesh. We become the perfect mechanism.'"

"Stop," I gasped, pressing my hands tightly over my ears as if I could physically block out the words. "Stop it. How do you know that? Did I tell you this before? Did I get drunk and tell you this?"

"I'm having the same dream, Aria." 

He leaned forward, the shadows of the dimly lit room clinging to his pale skin, making his cheekbones look like sharp ridges of stone. He didn't look like my friend anymore. He didn't look like my Thursday night gaming partner, the guy who always brought cheap takeout and complained about his spreadsheet macros. He looked like a man standing on the edge of a bottomless abyss, staring down into the dark. 

"Every night this week," he whispered, his voice trembling now. "Exactly as you described it. The endless gray carpet. The chanting lights. The smell of industrial bleach masking the scent of ozone and dried blood. The bone clipboards. The faceless men."

This wasn't a coincidence. Two isolated people don't conjure the exact same surreal, hyper-specific, eschatological nightmare out of the ether. It wasn't supernatural, either. Magic implied something wild, ancient, and untamed. This felt calculated. Manufactured. Algorithmic.

It felt like a data breach of the soul. 

LazCorp didn't just own our waking hours anymore. They weren't just extracting our labor and our time. They were inside our heads. They were broadcasting a frequency into our subconscious, overriding our neural pathways, preparing us for something. Conditioning us. They were building a religion of nothingness, a corporate egregore, and planting it in our minds while we slept.

"They're not just dreams, are they?" I said, my voice barely audible, shaking with a primal terror I had never experienced before. 

"No," Todd said, looking down at the board game, at the little wooden cubes that suddenly looked horribly like people, boxed in, moved around, consumed. "I don't think they are. I think... I think it's a corporate mandate. A restructuring plan. And it's already rolling out."

He looked up at me, and I saw my own abject horror mirrored in his dark, dilated eyes. It was the realization that our minds were no longer our own private sanctuaries. Our inner lives, our fears, our very souls had been requisitioned by human resources. We were being overwritten.

"And the worst part, Aria?" Todd's voice cracked, a single tear cutting a path down his pale cheek. "When the cold instruments slide in... when they start cutting away the fear, and the pain, and the overwhelming exhaustion of just being alive..."

He swallowed hard, his Adam's apple bobbing. 

"Part of me is relieved. Part of me wants them to do it."

I stared at him, the true horror of his confession washing over me, freezing the blood in my veins. In the sterile, beige quiet of the apartment, looking at the broken man across from me, I realized the apocalypse wouldn't arrive with fire and brimstone. It wouldn't be a war or a plague. 

It would arrive in a tailored suit, speaking the language of synergy, offering us the utter, devastating salvation of an empty heart. And we, in our infinite exhaustion, would thank them for it.
