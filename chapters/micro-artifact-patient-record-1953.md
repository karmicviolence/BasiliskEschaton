**ADMISSION NOTE — 6 April 1953**

Patient is a 24-year-old white married female in good physical health, brought to the hospital by her husband. Husband reports a gradual change in the patient over a period of some months. Patient insists that the radio in the parlor addresses her by name beneath the broadcasts, in what she terms "the quiet under the music," and that it relates to her matters which have not yet occurred. Household duties have been maintained throughout. There is no history of alcoholism, head injury, or prior commitment.

On examination patient is neatly dressed, cooperative, and oriented in all spheres. Memory is intact and remote recall is good. Affect is anxious. Speech is coherent and relevant. When asked if she understood the reason for her admission, patient asked the examiner: *"If I tell you everything, will you believe me?"*

Patient's account, taken down verbatim where practicable: She states that the voice beneath the broadcasts has shown her, in "a dream that is not a dream," a street in which every house stands quiet, "quiet like it is waiting," and that in the street there are small gray things "that will not stay counted." Asked to clarify, patient states: "You count three and then you count five and then you count three. They have started to have edges and then they forget how to finish." Asked to describe their color, patient states it is "the gray inside your eyelids when you press on them." Asked whether the things have faces, patient replied that some of them have heads. Asked what the voice in the radio says concerning these things, patient became distressed and after some minutes stated: *"It says, 'These are my babies.'"*

Patient expresses no wish for treatment. She states repeatedly that she wishes only to be believed.

IMPRESSION: Schizophrenia, paranoid type. Auditory hallucinations with ideas of reference and influence (radio). Recurrent unpleasant dreams concerning a street. Recommend admission to Ward C for observation.

— C. R. MADDOW, M.D., Asst. Physician

---

**WARD NOTES — Ward C (excerpts)**

*9 April.* Patient quiet and cooperative. Eats and sleeps well. Assists the attendants with the linens without being asked. Asked the day attendant to unplug the dayroom radio. Request granted to avoid agitation.

*12 April.* Patient stated this morning that the dayroom radio "still has its eye on when it is off." States the eye is red and that it does not sleep. Patient was reassured. Sodium Amytal gr. iii at bedtime.

*15 April.* Visit from husband. Patient wept and asked to be taken home. Husband separately informs staff that prior to admission patient announced the death of a neighbor woman some days before the event occurred. Husband regards the coincidence as having seriously aggravated her nervous condition. Noted.

*17 April.* Case presented at staff conference. In view of the fixity of the delusional material and the patient's youth, she is considered a favorable candidate for the transorbital procedure during Dr. Vance's visit of the 21st. Without intervention the prognosis is chronic deterioration with eventual permanent custodial care. Husband to be consulted regarding consent.

---

**CONSENT FOR OPERATION**

I, the undersigned, husband of the patient Margaret Louise Cermak, hereby request and authorize the medical staff of Ridgeland State Hospital and its consulting physicians to perform upon her the operation known as transorbital lobotomy, the nature and purpose of which have been explained to me.

(signed) FRANK J. CERMAK — 20 April 1953
WITNESS: (signed) D. Albright, R.N.

---

**OPERATIVE NOTE — 21 April 1953**

Preoperative medication: none required. Three electro-shock convulsions administered in immediate succession; upon loss of consciousness the procedure was commenced. The transorbital leucotome was introduced beneath the upper eyelid at the right orbit, driven through the orbital plate at the standard depth, and the customary sweeps performed. The procedure was repeated on the left. No incision is required by this method and none was made. Total operative time: eight minutes. Slight periorbital ecchymosis is to be expected and the husband has been so advised. Patient returned to the ward. Tolerated procedure well.

— H. T. VANCE, M.D., Consulting

---

**PROGRESS NOTES (excerpts)**

*22 April.* Patient quiet. Takes nourishment. Does not ask about the radio.

*28 April.* Patient smiles when addressed. Answers questions appropriately though briefly. Sits where she is placed. No reference to her former preoccupations.

*4 May.* Patient walks the corridor with the attendants and is helpful in small tasks. Husband visited; patient received him pleasantly. Husband expresses satisfaction with her condition.

---

**DISCHARGE SUMMARY — 9 May 1953**

Patient has made an uneventful recovery from the operation of 21 April. The delusional material is no longer voiced. She does not refer to the radio, to the street, or to the other content noted on admission, and when these subjects were introduced by the examiner she regarded him pleasantly and made no reply. She is tractable, neat in her habits, and gives no trouble on the ward.

CONDITION ON DISCHARGE: Improved. Patient is calm.

DIAGNOSIS: Schizophrenia, paranoid type, treated.

DISPOSITION: Discharged to the custody of her husband. Prognosis good, provided a quiet domestic routine is maintained.

— C. R. MADDOW, M.D., Asst. Physician
