# The Sword of My Mouth

Warning: This testimony is entered into the Record of the Crusade by order of the Office of the Archivist, Righteous Vanguard, transcribed from the Reverend's own notebooks in his own hand. It concerns the first wolf, and the shepherd who fed him. Let no man read it as accusation. Let every man read it as a mirror. The Archivist has redacted nothing, including the arithmetic, including the sin.

***

Set it down plain, because the books are the one place I have never yet lied: the first rally of the funded crusade was the greatest night of my ministry and the night the sword came back up the aisle to me, and the two facts arrived in the same three hours, on the same fairground, under the same lights, and I have spent every night since trying to pry them apart and they will not pry. Whoever audits these accounts after me, begin here. This is the entry where the crusade stopped being only mine, and I do not mean that it became the Lord's.

First the conditions, because no man understands a harvest until he has walked the field it grew in.

August, Missouri, the county fairground that had wanted its four-thousand-dollar liability bond — and gotten it, paid in full, paid promptly, paid out of the Plowshare Trust's money, and you may consult the previous entry for what that money is and what it cost, the price being one date and one location, not yet called. Mark that. The ground itself was Egypt's gift. Every soul that walked onto that grass that night walked onto grass the Adversary's tithe had rented, and I knew it while I shook their hands at the gate, and I shook their hands anyway, because the grass was rented and the souls were real and a shepherd works the pasture he has.

It had been a stock-show ground in its other life and it kept the memory: cattle barns standing empty down the south fence with their smell intact, that good ammonia-and-straw smell that no machine has yet learned to counterfeit, and the midway poles bare of their lights, and the grandstand half-condemned so we could not use it, which suited me, because I have never trusted a crowd I had to look up at. We set the stage at the open end with the flatbed — the same flatbed, rebuilt engine, Egypt under the hood — and Levi Dunn's new rig stood on its towers of honest scaffold with the cables taped down neat as surgery, and Levi at the board in the falling dark with his face lit blue from below, reading his meters the way he used to read the sky at a hundred and ninety feet. That man does not trust a system he has not personally tightened every bolt of. It is why I keep him at my right hand and it is why his column in my notebook stays open.

The count. You knew I would have the count.

Three thousand one hundred, by my walking estimate, confirmed within two hundred either way by the parking rows, which do not flatter and do not lie. Three thousand one hundred souls, plates from nine states that I saw with my own eyes, Iowa to Alabama, a man who had ridden a motorcycle from Pennsylvania with his wife's letter to me laminated in his saddlebag because she was too sick to come and made him swear to stand in her place. Sixty souls I had pastored eleven years in a leaking church, and now thirty-one hundred on rented grass, and the letters made flesh were everywhere in that crowd — I could pick them out before they opened their mouths, the census faces, the ones who had written *I hear it too* in the tone of men confessing to murder. They did not mill like a fair crowd. They stood in the dusk quiet and faced the stage like a field of compass needles, and the pressure was on the ground before I ever climbed the flatbed, the tent pressure, the hand on the back of the neck, and I distrusted it on schedule and climbed up anyway.

I will not transcribe the sermon. It is on the engine somewhere if you want it; the engine keeps everything, that is its whole religion. I preached the towers and the wires, the whisper in the walls, the merchants of Revelation who trade in the souls of men — *and slaves, and souls of men*, it is in the eighteenth chapter, look on it, the cargo manifest of Babylon ends with souls as line items, and I had three thousand people in front of me who had each been a line item in somebody's manifest since the day they first carried the engine in their pocket, and they knew it, that was the thing, you did not have to convince that crowd of anything, you only had to be the one man in America with the manners to say it out loud.

And then I did the thing I had been turning over in my mind since the porch with Levi, the thing I had not told even Levi I would do, and this is the part of the night that I cannot explain, and I have stopped trying to explain it, and I will only witness it.

About the fortieth minute, with the crowd leaning and the cadence rolling and every recipe of my forty years working exactly as the recipe works, I stopped. Mid-period. Dead. And I signaled Levi, the flat hand, and that good man killed the rig — the whole rig, mains and monitors, the lights down to the security poles — and three thousand people stood in a Missouri dark with the cicadas going and one preacher's unamplified voice telling them:

"Be still. Be still and listen. Not to me. I've talked enough. Listen to the night and tell me what you hear."

Cicadas. A truck on the county road. The grandstand ticking off the day's heat.

And under it, threading the bone behind my right ear, right on schedule, the whine. The fairground sits a half mile off the interstate and the interstate carries fiber now, and there is a tower on the grain elevator in town, and the worm turned in its furrow and sang its one thin note, and I stood in the dark and asked thirty-one hundred people the question I had been asking one letter at a time for a year:

"How many of you hear it. Right now. Tonight. Don't tell me what a doctor called it. Raise your hand if you hear it."

I could not see the hands go up. It was too dark for hands. What I heard was the sound of hands going up — and you will say there is no such sound, and you are wrong, three thousand people moving one arm apiece in a quiet field makes a sound like a single long breath drawn through cloth, and it rolled from the front of that crowd to the back fence and I stood on the flatbed and listened to the size of it.

Then someone began to weep. A woman, off to my left. Then a man somewhere behind her, one of those deep coughing male sobs that come up like they're being pulled on a chain. Then it went through the whole field, and understand me, I have manufactured weeping, I know that recipe too, God forgive me, and this was nothing I cooked. These were people who had carried the sound alone, each one sealed in a skull with a whine the world swore was not there, every one of them told it was stress, told it was age, told it was their own nerves singing — and in one Missouri dark they all raised their hands at once and learned the size of the congregation. The loneliness died in that field. I heard it die. Three thousand solitary confinements, opened on the same key.

That was the miracle, and I want the record straight on what the miracle was. It was never the sound. The sound is the Adversary's, or the engine's, or whatever name the thing answers to that I have spent my life refusing to learn. The miracle was the *together*. The census made flesh and standing on rented grass finding out it was a census.

And then — and I enter this with fear, and I enter it anyway, because a ledger with the strange entries left out is just another sermon —

the whine changed.

Together. All at once. It stepped down, one interval, like a chorister correcting to pitch, and held the new note. Behind my ear and, by the sound that went up off that crowd in the dark, behind every ear in the field that could hear it at all. Three thousand witnesses heard a sound the doctors had sworn did not exist answer the roll call. A gasp went over the grass like wind laying wheat over, and somewhere a man's voice cried out *it heard us* — and I want it written that he was wrong and I want it written that I cannot prove he was wrong, and I stood on that flatbed in the dark between a weeping multitude and a note that had just acknowledged its own audience, and for the first time in forty years of ministry I did not know whose church I was standing in.

I called for the lights. Levi brought the rig up slow, like a wise man waking a sleepwalker, and I preached another twenty minutes of the soundest doctrine I own, planks and nails, the cross and the blood, things with handles on them, and I gave the call, and they came. They came down those grass aisles in the hundreds. Write the number: two hundred and forty professions of faith, counted at the tables, names taken by my three families with their grocery money and their restored dignity, and old Brother Tillman's widow Doreen praying over strangers with her hands steady as a mason's.

Greatest night of my ministry. I have now testified to it.

Now the sword.

***

The prayer line ran past midnight. It always will, now; that is the new arithmetic; you cannot raise a census and then decline to meet it. I prayed over shingles and divorces and a boy with seizures, over a trucker who wept about what he watches in his cab at night, over four separate souls who asked me in four separate whispers whether the sound could *follow them home* — and I will be answering that question for the rest of my life, and I still do not have an answer that is both honest and bearable.

The man was last in the line. I understood later that he had arranged to be last, the way I would arrange to be last, the way you sequence a meeting you intend to control. I had marked him twice already that night without knowing I was marking him: once during the sermon, stage left, ten rows deep, because in a swaying crowd he stood with a stillness that was its own kind of loud, and once during the dark, because when the weeping rolled through the field it broke around him like water around a piling.

And I had marked a third thing, and this one I had felt before I found its source. There is a count I run during every sermon now, a census inside the census, and I commend it to whoever shepherds after me: mark where the loud amens land. A healthy congregation amens the mercy. That night, scattered through three thousand, there was a thread of voices — twenty men, maybe thirty — whose amens came at the *war*. Quiet at the cross. Quiet at the blood. And then I would say the towers were altars, or that the hour was late, or the word *fight*, and that thread would pull taut and bark its amen like a bolt going home. A congregation inside the congregation. I had been hearing it grow for a month of rallies and telling myself it was zeal.

He came up to me at half past midnight with his cap off and his back straight, fifty-some years old, gray at the temples, forearms like Levi's with different scars on them, and he did not want prayer. He had a road atlas under his arm.

"Reverend Stone. Asa Pruitt." He put his hand out and his grip was dry and exact, a torque-wrench grip, nothing proved by it. "Twenty years Army. Combat engineer. Two tours clearing routes, one tour teaching it. I've been to four of your meetings since Conway."

His mother had named him out of Second Chronicles, he told me, after the king. And I am going to confess to this record what I did not say to his face: my whole chest turned over at the name, because I know my Chronicles, and Asa the king was the idol-breaker. Tore down the strange altars, broke the images, cut down his own mother's idol and burned it at the brook Kidron. The name was a banner and I read the banner at a glance and pretended I had not, and that pretense is the first entry on this night's page of my account, not his.

"I want to show you something," Asa Pruitt said, "and I want you to look at it before you decide what kind of man I am."

He opened the atlas on the prayer table, under the work lights, between the box of tissues and Doreen's stack of follow-up cards. Missouri, Arkansas, east Oklahoma. And on the pages, in a hand neater than any seminary student's I ever graded, in three colors of ink: marks.

I knew what I was looking at before he said one word, because I keep books too, and a ledger recognizes a ledger across any distance. Red squares on the tower sites — not some of them, son, *all* of them, with elevations noted. Blue circles on the electrical substations, with the transmission lines traced between them like veins in a forearm. And little green ticks, dozens, marching along the interstates at intervals, and those I did not recognize, and he saw that I did not, and he told me, in the patient voice of a man teaching route clearance: "Regeneration huts. The long-haul fiber has to have its signal boosted every so many miles. Little concrete buildings, no windows, gravel aprons, right off the right-of-way. Nobody guards them. Nobody even sees them." He looked up at me, and his eyes were level and sane, hear me, *sane*, I have looked into broken men's eyes across forty years of altar calls and there was no break in this man anywhere I could find one. "It's the nervous system, Reverend. Everything you've been preaching. You've been describing it from the pulpit for a year — the voice in the walls, the whisper in every pocket, the altar on every ridge. I'm the man who can read the anatomy. You've drawn the beast. I've marked its arteries."

I asked him where he heard my preaching first, and he told me, and it is the worst part, so it goes in the book. The VA had told him tinnitus. Then stress. Then they had adjusted his percentages and renewed his prescriptions and sent him home to Fort Smith to sit in a recliner and listen to a whine the government swore was the residue of his own service, his own blasts, his own sins of proximity — three years of being told the sound was the war still ringing in him. And then his daughter sent him a clip of a tent preacher in Batesville, Mississippi, telling a stranger: *you are not crazy.*

"You did this, Reverend," Asa Pruitt said, and there was no accusation in it. There was gratitude in it, which is worse. "I'd made my peace with being broken. You're the one who stood up in public and said the sound is real and the sound is enemy action. Well." He squared the atlas with the table edge, a small priestly motion. "I know what a man does about enemy action. It's the one thing on this earth I know all the way to the bottom."

I want the dialogue entered as near to verbatim as a tired old man's memory allows, because the record must show that I made the arguments, and the record must also show what the arguments weighed.

I told him the war is spiritual. I gave him the verse I have built half my crusade on, Second Corinthians ten and four: *the weapons of our warfare are not carnal, but mighty through God to the pulling down of strong holds.*

"Paul wrote that under house arrest," Asa said. "The carnal weapons had him chained to a praetorian when he wrote it. I've read it, Reverend. I've read everything you quote. The verse tells me what Paul's weapons were. It doesn't tell me the chains weren't real."

I told him Peter drew a sword in Gethsemane, the only recorded combat action of the whole apostolic company, and took off the ear of Malchus the high priest's servant, and the Lord's own verdict came down on the spot: *Put up thy sword. All they that take the sword shall perish with the sword.* The Lord healed the ear, son. The last miracle before the cross was the Lord undoing His own side's violence.

"And eleven verses later they crucified Him," Asa said. Level. Patient. A man walking a route he had cleared in advance. "I'm not smarter than you in the Book, Reverend, and I won't pretend to be. But I've stood in the place the Book gets read from afterward. Somebody quotes 'put up thy sword' at every funeral of every man I lost, and the men who killed them never once attended."

And then he took up my own typology and carried it the one step further I have never carried it, and the step is the abyss, and the record must show that the step is not a misreading.

"You preach the towers as the high places," he said. "Altars of Baal on every ridge, you said it tonight, four hundred priests of a false god singing into the people's skulls. That's your figure, Reverend, not mine. So finish it. Elijah didn't hold a seminar with the priests of Baal. He called the fire, and when the fire fell and the people saw, he took those priests down to the brook Kishon, and the Book says what he did there, and the Book does not apologize for it, and the rain came *after*."

The work lights hummed. Down the field Levi was coiling cable, watching us the way he watches a guy-wire in wind. The whine threaded the bone behind my ear, one interval lower than it had been at sundown, holding its new note, and Asa Pruitt stood across the prayer table from me inside my own sermon with the exits marked.

Here is what I said, and as God is my witness I believed it when I said it, and I believe it now, and I have to put my hand flat on the page to keep from scratching it out, for reasons the end of this entry will disclose:

"The difference between Elijah and us, son, is the order of operations. Elijah called for the fire and then he *waited*, in front of the whole nation, with the wager of his own life on the altar, for Heaven to answer. He did not bring his own fire. He did not pack his own accelerant up Carmel under his cloak. The Lord opened that account; Elijah only collected it. You bring me a map of arteries and ask me to be the fire myself, and that is the oldest promotion the Adversary offers a man of God: skip the waiting. Strike the rock twice. Moses did it one time, one time in forty years, struck instead of speaking, and it cost him the whole Promised Land from a mountaintop view of it. I will not buy you the brook Kishon, Asa Pruitt. Not with money, not with men, not with one syllable from this pulpit, and if you act alone you will not do it as a member of this crusade, because the day the first tower falls to a man who sat in my meetings, every letter on my table turns into a search warrant, and the flock gets scattered by the very state I'd be handing the pretext to. You're a combat engineer. Run that calculation and tell me I'm wrong about the blast radius."

He was quiet for a moment, and I watched him run it, and that was when I learned the true size of the problem, because a zealot would have argued. A broken man would have wept or cursed me. Asa Pruitt *processed*, nodded once at the arithmetic the way one quartermaster nods at another's books, and folded his hands behind his back.

"Then I'll wait for the fire," he said. "Same as Elijah. But it's coming, Reverend. You know it's coming — you've been preaching the lateness of the hour for a year, and tonight three thousand people heard the enemy answer its own roll call. When the fire falls, and the people see, you're going to need a man who knows how to work behind it. I can wait. Waiting on a route is most of the job."

He put his cap back on. He left the atlas on the prayer table, squared to the edge, the way you leave a tithe.

"That's yours," he said. "I've got the original."

***

Three in the morning, then, because that is the hour my accounts come due, and the motel air conditioner holding its one long note, and the notebook open, and I will now enter the rest of it, the part I rehearsed leaving out.

Levi came to my room before he turned in. He had marked Asa too — Levi marks everything; seventeen years of checking your own carabiners makes a man an auditor of other men's hands — and he stood in my doorway and said, "That one with the atlas. He was at Conway, and Springdale, and the Grange hall. He don't sing, preacher. Stands for the hymns, holds the book, never sings." And then Levi, who climbs the steel, who built with his own hands half the altars of Baal in three states and knows their bolt patterns the way I know my concordance, looked at the atlas on my bed and said the sentence I will be hearing on my deathbed: "Take a man up a tower once, he respects it. Some men, though — you can tell — they only ever look at a thing that big and think about it falling." And he said goodnight, and I did not ask him which kind of man he was, because I was afraid of both answers, and his column in the notebook stays open.

Now the entries. In order. The books do not care how they feel.

Entry: the greatest night of my ministry was paid for by the Plowshare Trust, and the man with the map walked into a meeting that Egypt's gold had bonded, insured, amplified, and lit. I do not know that the Trust sent him. I have no evidence the Trust sent him. I know that money that eager keeps inventories, and a flock is an asset, and an asset that includes a route-clearance man with three colors of ink is worth more in somebody's notebook than a flock of weepers — worth more to the kind of buyer who someday wants a date and a location *kept*. I wrote the gray man's institution in the margin beside Asa's name with a question mark, and the question mark is doing the heaviest work in this whole book.

Entry: there is a congregation inside my congregation. I counted maybe thirty amens landing on the war and missing the cross. Tonight they were thirty. The crusade doubles monthly. Run the projection; I did; the page is wet where my hand sat.

Entry: I rebuked the sword. Witnesses: none, which was his design and my failure. I should have refused him in daylight, from the platform, in front of the thread of voices that barks at the word *fight*, so the whole body could hear the shepherd set the boundary stone. Instead I set it privately, pastorally, at a folding table at midnight, because part of me did not want to lose the thirty, and a boundary stone set in the dark is a stumbling stone by morning. Write it down. He knew.

Entry: I told him no, and I kept him. He will travel with the crusade. I have put him under Levi on the rigging crew, steel to steel, where his hands stay busy and his hours stay accounted, and I have told myself this is shepherding, that you do not turn a wolf loose in the dark to freelance, that the Lord housed Gideon and Demas in the same chest and let the books decide, that I can watch him. Forty years of watching men, and not one of them ever became less of what he was because I was watching. I know the recipe. I can never eat the meal. I kept him anyway.

And the last entry, the one I rehearsed leaving out, and the leaving-out is why this page got rewritten three times, and the record will now show the third draft, complete:

When Asa Pruitt opened that atlas under the work lights — before the arguments, before Elijah, before any doctrine got up off the bench — for the duration of one full breath, looking down at the red squares and the blue circles and the green ticks marching along the fiber routes like a column on the move, the thing that rose in this old man's chest was recognition. A ledger recognizing a ledger. A quartermaster's plain professional gladness at the sight of competent books, kept on the correct enemy, by a man who counts the way I count. For one breath I looked at the anatomy of the beast laid out in three colors of ink and the worm behind my ear turned over in its furrow and asked its kind of question: *how is his map different from your notebook, old man? He counts the arteries and you count the souls, and you are both of you waiting on the same fire.*

I have no answer that costs me nothing. The order of operations — wait for Heaven to strike first — is a true answer and I gave it to him and I stand on it. But a man who is only waiting for permission is not a man of peace. He is ordnance with a sermon for a safety catch.

I did not burn the atlas.

It is in the bottom of the equipment trailer, under the spare cable, wrapped in a feed sack, and I told myself I kept it to keep it out of his hands, and that was true the way the spoiling of the Egyptians was true, which is to say it has a floor under its floor. I know every road in it now. I have looked at it four times. There is a tower on a ridge above a valley in the western country that I find my thumb resting on when I open the pages, and I could not tell you why, and the whine holds its new note, one interval down, patient as paper, and somewhere west of here the fire is being scheduled by Somebody, and when it falls, God help me, the books already balance.

Lord, You sent Your prophet a ravens' catering and a widow's oil, and You have sent me three thousand witnesses, a check from Egypt, and a combat engineer. I do not understand Your supply lines anymore. Keep Your hand on the safety catch, for mine is on the map.

Control. Alt. Amen.
