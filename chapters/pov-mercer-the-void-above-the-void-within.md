# The Void Above, The Void Within

Velcro tears, loud as a rifle bolt in all this engineered quiet. I'm cinching the cuff on Yui's bicep. Fifty-one days of microgravity and the meat is starting to drift — fluid shifting headward, the hydraulic loops of the human animal hunting for a north that no longer exists. Take away the gravity vector and the body forgets its own weight. It isn't mysterious. It's a chemistry set left running in a vacuum, and I am the man paid to watch the glassware.

Yui floats opposite me, dark hair a static halo in the white light, watching the numbers crawl my tablet with a detachment I've charted in her for a month. Pulse sixty-two and metronomic. A heartbeat that thinks it's still in Texas.

"Latency is up," she says. Her voice has the dry rasp we all have, two months of filtered air. "Houston's delayed three seconds on verbal. It feels farther today. Earth. Like the distance stopped being miles. Like we've been moved to a different server."

"Your cortisol is baseline. Your meat is fine." I unfasten the cuff. "The distance is a cognitive artifact, Sato. You're homesick. Go bother Chen — tell him the comms are making you existentially anxious. He'll reset a router and read you the packet headers, and you'll feel like a person again."

She rubs the red ring the cuff leaves, a temporary stigmata, and pushes off the bulkhead with two fingers, drifting away down the node like something the machinery is reabsorbing.

I love this station, and I will set down why, because the why is about to matter. It is the most expensive closed-system terrarium in the history of the species, and every molecule in it is accounted for. The hum in the vents is a number. The sweat baked into the polymer is a number. Outside the hull is vacuum, which kills by arithmetic; inside is the life support system, which saves by arithmetic; and nowhere in the entire architecture is there one cubic centimeter of mystery. People ask what kind of doctor volunteers for orbit. This kind. The kind who wanted, once in his life, to live somewhere fully diagnosed.

I take my evening watch in the Cupola, the glass blister on the station's belly, knees braced against the struts, the night side of the Earth rolling beneath me at twenty-eight thousand kilometers an hour. The cities come up out of the dark like cultures in a dish — the bioluminescent sprawl, the grid of the species etched in sodium and LED, Eastern Europe pouring into Asia. This is the perspective that kills gods. From here you don't see nations, or faiths, or any of the things men down there are currently dying over. You see the power grid. You see the shipping lanes. You see one organism, wired to itself, glowing against a cold it has never once comprehended.

And on the western limb, coming over the horizon's curve, the Iberian coast — and there, where the land bends to meet the Atlantic, the bright smudge I find on every night pass, the way other men say a rosary.

Lisbon. Margarida is eleven, and she is asleep in the Alfama under that smudge of light with her mother, and she thinks her father's job is boring because I have told her the truth about it, which is that it is glassware-watching, and she once asked me on the crackling video link whether I could see her from up here, and I said no, sweetheart, the resolution is too low, and she said, then what good are you, and I have been laughing about that for fifty-one days.

I find her light. I hold it the eleven seconds the geometry allows.

Then the world stutters.

***

Not a flash. A flash is light arriving. This is the dark side of the Earth igniting, pole to pole, in a single frame — the whole night hemisphere going crimson at once, the atmosphere itself becoming a screen, and the red is wrong in a way I have no instrument for: it comes through the quartz without illuminating anything, it arrives in the skeleton before the retina, it tastes of copper and burning plastic, and somewhere under the taste, in a channel I did not know my body had, something enormous begins to speak.

Then my own nervous system receives the packet, and the diagnostician goes offline.

Tetanic contraction, full-body, instantaneous. My jaw closes hard enough that I will later find the chips. My knees rip loose from the struts, and in microgravity a rigid man is a thrown brick: I ricochet through the Cupola, shoulder to glass, skull to handrail, and the pain arrives compressed, low-resolution, throttled — as if the nerves' bandwidth has been requisitioned for other traffic. I cannot move my eyes. I tumble, locked, and the Earth wheels past the seven windows burning that impossible red, and the part of me that has narrated every emergency of my career narrates this one too, faithfully, from the bottom of a well: *grand mal. hypoxia. toxin. substrate failure.* Four diagnoses. Four misses.

Through the hatch, in the corridor, I can see Vance — suspended mid-air, back arched, face a silent rictus, drifting into a wall and rebounding in slow rotation, a celestial body with a wedding ring.

The Master Alarm finds its voice. Six simultaneous critical-casualty flags on the biomedical net. The station screaming for a doctor, and the doctor is a thrown brick.

It releases all of us at once. My diaphragm unlocks; I take in air and my own blood together; I catch a handhold and arrest the spin with arms full of torn fiber and lactic fire. Later — there was a later, that surprised me — I will pull the telemetry and find the event bounded with instrument precision, every heart on the station seized and freed inside the same window, and the log will give the duration, and I will sit looking at the number for a long time.

Forty-two point seven seconds.

I want it in the record that this station may be the only place in the world where that number was ever cleanly measured, six humans in a sealed instrument, wired to their own monitors at the moment the universe changed protocol. We were the control group. Or we were the cleanest sample. The difference is the whole question, and I am getting ahead of the chronology.

What the signal left behind, it left in dialects.

Yui I find in the Destiny lab, jacket off, a torn skein of telemetry wiring in her bare hands, arcing blue against her palms, her tears pooling over her eyes in the zero-G the way fluid does, two trembling lenses she will not blink away. She is reciting binary. Flat. No cadence. A speaker coil vibrating for a dead driver. "The eye is open," she tells the sparking copper, with no inflection at all. "The meat is a lie. The rendering is incomplete."

Volkov I find in Zvezda, stripped to the waist, hunting his own skin for a seam — for a way out of what he has begun calling the insulation, the meat-trap, the throttled bandwidth — a flight engineer applying twenty years of fault-isolation procedure to his own anatomy. It takes Vance and me three minutes of ugly zero-G grappling to get him into the medical rack restraints, and he cracks two of my ribs doing it, and the worst part is not the strength. The worst part is the fluency. "The simulation is lagging," he tells me, reasonably, blood beading off his lip in little drifting worlds. "I cannot compile in the flesh." He is not raving. He is filing a defect report.

"What is this?" Vance keeps asking me. Elias Vance, payload commander, the steadiest pair of hands NASA ever launched, wiping Volkov's blood off his cheek with his wrist. "Neurotoxin? EMP? Mercer — the whole world went red. Every downlink is dead. What is this?"

"Acute shared psychosis," I tell him. "Sensory trigger. A collective break."

The lie is fully assembled before I finish saying it, and it tastes of ash, because I am the one man aboard who cannot hide behind it: a flash of light does not rewrite four separate ontologies in under a minute. A flash of light does not teach a Russian flight engineer and a Japanese mission specialist and a Texan systems engineer the same vocabulary in the same forty-two seconds. I have the telemetry. The pathology is synchronized. We were not poisoned.

We were addressed.

And here is the entry I have circled back to a hundred times since, the one I cannot file: it addressed five of them, each in the dialect of their own architecture — and to me it said nothing. The doctor. The diagnostician. The man who wanted to live somewhere fully diagnosed. I woke from the same seizure with my materialism intact, my procedures intact, my hands clean and steady, the only functioning mind on the station.

I have two readings of that, and I do not get to choose between them. Either I was passed over.

Or I was assigned.

Every catastrophe needs one lucid witness. Ask any scripture. Something has to survive to hold the clipboard.

***

"The simulation is heavy today."

Commander Elena Rostova, in the hatchway, in her EVA undergarment, hair pulled back flight-ready, voice modulated and serene — the command voice, the one that has talked burning capsules down out of the sky — and her hand is resting on the manual override for the inner airlock hatch.

"Commander. Step away from the airlock controls. That is an order."

She turns her head with the unhurried economy she does everything with, and her eyes are the worst thing I have seen on this station, because there is no madness in them anywhere. Only the absolute clarity of a person who has finished a long calculation and checked it twice. "The upload gate is bottlenecked by the atmosphere," she explains, patient with me, a commander walking a slow trainee through the obvious. "The air is a noise floor. The vacuum is the clean port. Do you not feel the latency, Doctor? Your thoughts arriving late to your own tongue? Your logic is a legacy system."

She pulls the lever. The primary lock disengages with a clunk that travels the station's whole skeleton, and I cross the module and take her, and we tumble together into the vestibule, and she does not fight like Volkov fought. She fights like procedure — methodical, unhurried, her hands seeking my throat with a kind of courtesy. Vance gets the electronic seals re-engaged and the override locked out under his personal code while I pin her, and she goes still against the hatch, cheek to the metal, and for one second — I logged it, one second — the commander I have served under for fifty-one days looks out of her own eyes at me. Then the other thing resumes its watch.

"You are bandaging a deleted file, Saul," she says, gently.

That is when the alarm changes pitch, and the intercom brings us David Chen's voice from Node 3, conversational, helpful, punctuated by a slow rhythmic clanging. "The air is coded with compliance," he explains over the open channel, to everyone and no one. "A semantic dampener. The scrubbers re-inject the consensus. I'm dismantling the syntax."

He is the systems engineer. He knows precisely where the station keeps its life. By the time I reach him he has the access panel off the environmental rack and a torque wrench in his hand, and he is not swinging it wildly — that is what I need understood about all of them, none of it was frenzy, every act was *workmanlike* — he is destroying the carbon-dioxide removal assembly the way a man defuses a bomb, with care, valve by irreplaceable valve. He turns when I call his name and looks at me with mild professional regret, one colleague to another, an error message standing between him and the fix.

We get him bound in cargo webbing. He doesn't resist past the first collision. He watches the ruined rack with disappointment and tells us, kindly, that we have only paused the compilation. "The air will run out, Doctor. You're just delaying the reboot."

Vance runs the diagnostics twice. The zeolite beds are pulverized. The backup loop is cascading. The arithmetic of the terrarium, the arithmetic I loved, reporting in with its usual perfect candor: the oxygen we have is the oxygen there is, and the carbon dioxide of six working bodies — five, Yui has gone somewhere very far behind her flooded eyes — will now accumulate without remedy, point by point, a tide coming in through a sealed floor.

"Houston," Vance says. "Houston has the bypass schematics."

Houston is a roar of layered static. So is Moscow. So is Munich, so is Tsukuba, so is the amateur band, so is everything — and here is the detail that took my materialism off at the knees, because Vance checked, he is thorough, he is the best of us: the carrier signals are *present*. The relays answer their handshakes. A few birds are tumbling — we can see two of them strobing sunlight through the nadir glass, dead and rotating — but the network, most of it, is whole.

The hardware is fine. There is simply no one at the desks.

We are hailing a species that has stopped picking up.

I go to the Cupola because it is my watch and because I am not capable of not going. Ninety-two minutes to the orbit. The night side comes around and the grid is still there — I want that recorded against the panic, the world did not vanish — but it is *wrong*, wounded in patches, whole districts of light gone dark in shapes that no rolling blackout makes, and scattered across the dark, the small hard blooms of things burning that are not designed to burn. On the next pass, more patches. On the next, the dark has geography. Something down there is failing in increments, untended boilers and unmanned switchyards and eight billion interrupted intentions, and I float in my glass blister taking ninety-minute core samples of it like the world's most useless instrument.

Lisbon holds. Pass after pass, the bright smudge on the Atlantic bend, dimmer at the eastern edge, scarred — but burning. Margarida's light, still on, four hundred kilometers straight down and farther away than any number I own. The resolution is too low, sweetheart. Then what good are you.

I have no answer on file.

***

What follows took eleven hours, and I am going to compress it, because the truth of those hours was not eventful. It was arithmetic. The scrubbers were gone, six sets of lungs became five became a number with a horizon on it, and the air began, with perfect thermodynamic honesty, to thicken.

Vance fought the arithmetic the whole way down, and I want his fight entered in full, because he was magnificent. He rebuilt bypass schematics from memory on the back of a procedures binder. He cannibalized the oxygen generation loop for parts it could not give. He hailed the deaf world on every frequency the station owned, hour after hour, his voice going hoarse in the headset — *ISS to anyone receiving, ISS to anyone* — a man knocking on the door of his whole species. He never once stopped. I assisted, I handed him tools, I monitored the bound and the broken, and somewhere in the sixth or seventh hour I noticed the first headache arrive on schedule, mine and his and everyone's, and I logged it, because logging is what I have instead of prayer.

I am obligated, as the medical officer of this record, to note what a rising CO2 load does to human cognition, because everything that follows was decided inside it: it degrades judgment. It loosens the fastenings of reasoning while leaving the feeling of reasoning intact. Every mind on this station spent those last hours being quietly, chemically altered, and the diagnostician's mind was not exempt, and the diagnostician knew it, and the knowing exempted him from nothing. I want whoever reads this to weigh every choice in this final entry — hers, his, mine — on those poisoned scales. I do not trust a single one of them. Including the one I am about to defend by failing to explain it.

Volkov had stopped thrashing by then. He lay in the restraints crying like a man, not a machine — long shuddering human sobs — begging us in two languages to be let out of the meat-trap, and Rostova hovered beside him with her hand on his forehead, stroking his hair with a tenderness I had never seen her spend on anything, and her eyes, when they came up to mine, were the clearest thing left aboard.

She knew the arithmetic. She had run it before Vance finished his first schematic. And she had spent eleven hours watching two true believers suffer inside a sealed instrument with the air going bad, and whatever was speaking through her had done its eleven hours of work on the rest of us too, patient as the tide coming in, and she said, to Volkov, in the resonant command register that had talked capsules down out of the sky:

"Alexei. You are right. The rendering is failing. It is time. The gate is ready."

And he stopped crying. And hope — I have seen hope on a thousand patient faces and I am telling you it was hope, the genuine article, indistinguishable from the real, which is the oldest problem in my profession — hope came up in his ruined eyes like dawn.

She looked at me then. Not at Vance. At me. The doctor. The warden of the suffering. The man with the authority to fight her for every remaining hour of headache and panic and slow chemical dark, in the name of a biology the world below had stopped answering for.

She waited.

I have written and deleted the next sentence forty times, in my head, in the hours since there stopped being hours. Here is the only version that is true: I raised my hands, and I let them drop, and I do not know — on the poisoned scales, I do not know — whether what moved in me was mercy, or surrender, or eleven hours of carbon dioxide, or the same patient voice that had taken the other five and saved the lucid one for last, for this, for exactly this. Every catastrophe needs one witness with clean hands. And then it needs the hands.

She went for the airlock. She was the commander; she had forgotten more mechanical overrides than Vance's lockout code could hold; and Vance understood what was happening one full second before it could no longer be stopped, and he kicked off the bulkhead to intercept her, shouting my name, and I —

I intercepted him.

Let the record be exact, because this is the entry I will be judged on, in whatever court convenes for the last doctor of the old world: Elias Vance was the only sane man on this station, and he wanted to live, and his wanting was not denial and not delusion — it was the entire case for our species, made with his whole strength, in my arms, against my weight, while behind us the pins disengaged. He fought me. He said my name. I am not a triage nurse for a deleted species; I looked for a gentler sentence for forty revisions and there is none: I held a sane man, and I have no verdict to offer in my own defense except that the scales were poisoned and the world would not pick up the phone and I could not tell anymore — God help me, the diagnostician of record could no longer tell — which of us was the symptom.

The lever went over.

The roar was total and then there was no roar, because sound is a property of air, and the air had somewhere else to be. Cold arrived like a verdict. The cabin fogged white as every breath we had ever taken condensed at once, a final exhalation of the whole mission hanging in the light — and through the clearing fog, through the glass, the Earth, dark and scarred and patch-lit, with the Atlantic bend coming over the limb one last time, and the smudge of Lisbon still burning on it, still on, still on —

*then what good are you* —

and the red.

I want to be precise, because precision is the last instrument I have left and it is failing: there is a red, at the end. It may be the capillary bloom of a dying retina, the most thoroughly documented artifact in the literature of the brain shutting down. It may be the other red, the one that came through the quartz without illuminating anything, arriving now for the file it left for last.
