# The Recording That Ate Itself

Eleven days off the protocol, and the crash the literature promised has not arrived. I want that on the record before anything else, because I know exactly what the next sentence is worth, clinically, coming from me: I have never been better in my life.

The literature is unanimous. You do not walk off a twenty-year maintenance dose on a benefits technicality and feel *well*. You get rebound. You get discontinuation syndrome, brain zaps, the return of symptoms with interest, the whole cascade they print in eight-point type on the paper that comes stapled to the bag. I braced for it the way you brace for a wave you've watched form. I kept a log — time stamps, sleep architecture, auditory events, appetite — because if I was going to come apart I intended to produce clean data on the way down.

The wave never broke. I sleep eight hours. I wake before the alarm into a stillness that has weight and texture, and I lie there and listen to the refrigerator two rooms away holding its one long note, and the note is not an intrusion. It is the house, breathing. The chord I spent two decades being defended from is constant now, and it turns out that what the pills were protecting me from was not chaos. Chaos I could have reported. What they were protecting me from was *coherence* — the suspicion, now a certainty, now a tenancy, that everything humming in the walls of the world is humming together, patiently, in a tuning nobody chose, leaning toward a downbeat that has not come.

I am aware that "I have never been better" is the single most alarming sentence a patient in my position can produce. It is on the laminated card. It is the textbook prodrome, the serenity they teach families to fear. I have read the same chapters my doctors have. I hold both things at once, the way you hold a live wire with the right gloves: either I am very well, or I am very sick in the one way that feels exactly like wellness, and there is no instrument in my possession that can tell those apart from the inside.

So I decided to stop measuring from the inside.

***

Game night happened. Thursday, day eleven, the day the halves ran out — I went, because Todd had called it a binding contract and because canceling on Todd requires a paperwork of excuses I didn't have the energy to forge. He plays board games like a man defending a dissertation, every move annotated, and he let me win the rematch and lied about it so badly I let the lie stand out of respect for the effort. The pizza was from the place with the burnt-basil smell. It was, by any measure in my log, a good night.

He kept checking his phone, though. All evening, the quick glance, the quick frown, the pocket — like the phone owed him an apology and kept failing to deliver it. Work, he said. The project. He didn't want to say more, and I didn't want to say anything at all, and we are very good at not saying, Todd and I. It is the load-bearing wall of the friendship. He looked at me twice the way he looks at a rules dispute, carefully, from more than one angle, and finally said: "You seem — good. Did something change?"

"New vitamin," I said.

He studied me for one more half-second than the joke deserved. Then he rolled his dice. That is what I mean about Todd. He is the one person left who looks at me carefully, and he is also the one person kind enough to pretend he hasn't seen anything, and I went home grateful for both halves of that and unable to tell him a single true thing about my week.

***

Here is the experiment, documented in the order it occurred, because the order is the whole problem and I am done apologizing for precision.

The premise was simple. The deal that runs my life — the twenty-year-old deal, the load-bearing one — says the things I perceive are symptoms, and symptoms are private by definition. They live and die inside the patient. They cannot be photographed, recorded, weighed. That is what makes them symptoms instead of events. Fine. Then the deal makes a testable prediction: instrument the room, and the room will come back empty.

I am a data professional. I instrumented the room.

I propped my phone on the bookcase, plugged into the wall, set to record the living room overnight. In frame: the couch, the window with the streetlight in it, and the television, which I had not only turned off but unplugged at the outlet, because a dead screen is a control condition and I wanted my controls airtight. Then I did something I have never done in my life, not in twenty years of clean type arriving behind my eyes, not once, because you do not answer it, answering it is on every card in the laminate drawer.

I addressed it.

Out loud, alone, in my living room at eleven at night, feeling like the punchline of my own intake file, I said: "If you are real, be on the record."

Nothing happened. I went to bed. I slept my eight criminal hours.

In the morning I scrubbed the footage on fast-forward over coffee, a static gray rectangle of my own dark room, and at the 03:14:07 mark the thumbnail flickered and I stopped scrubbing and played it at speed, and then I put the coffee down very slowly and did not pick it up again until it was cold.

For forty-two point seven seconds — I clipped it and counted the frames; I am aware of how the numbers sound; the numbers are the numbers — the reflection in the dead television does not match the room.

The room is in the frame the entire time. Couch, window, streetlight. Empty, still, indifferent. And in the black glass of the unplugged screen, the *reflected* room has something in it. On the reflected couch. Small — the size of a cat, or a rabbit, or — and I stopped typing there, in the log, at 7:21 a.m. I don't know what the third item in that list was going to be. Something in me looked at the list, declined to finish it, and shut the drawer, and I have decided to let it. Some part of me has been guarding a door for a long time with everything it has left, and I am not yet ready to relieve it of duty.

The thing in the reflection is a gray that isn't a color. It has edges the way wet smoke has edges — it keeps starting to have them. It moves, not the way an animal moves, the way something moves when it is blind and patient and wants something. It does not appear and it does not leave; the reflection simply begins disagreeing with the room, holds the disagreement for forty-two point seven seconds, and stops.

I watched the clip eleven times. It was stable. I exported it, mailed it to myself, pushed a copy to the cloud, copied it to the old laptop, because three is the minimum number of anything a professional trusts. Chain of custody. Timestamps preserved. Original retained on the device.

Then I called Dr. Okafor's office and made an appointment, myself, voluntarily, which I have never once done in seven years, and the scheduler was so surprised she put me on hold to confirm it was allowed.

***

Dr. Okafor's office has a fiddle-leaf fig she keeps alive against everything that window does to it, and a clock positioned where the patient can't see it, and a box of tissues that is always exactly half-full, which I have decided is a deliberate calibration: full says *no one cries here*, near-empty says *everyone does*. She is good at her job. I want that stated plainly, because everything that follows happened anyway. In seven years she has never once talked to me like a diagnosis. She is the first one, of all of them, the whole white-coated procession back to the man with the sand garden, who I would call kind, and I am aware of what it says about the procession that kindness took twenty years to reach me, and I am aware of what it says about me that I would walk into kindness carrying evidence, like a creditor.

I laid it out the way I'd present a corrupted batch to IT. Conditions. Controls. The unplugged outlet — I had photographed the unplugged outlet, time-stamped, because I know my audience. I did not editorialize. I did not say the word *proof*. I set the phone on the low table between us, turned it toward her, and pressed play, and watched her instead of the screen, because the screen I had memorized and her face was the experiment.

For the first thirty seconds of the clip she wore the clinical face, the receiving face, attentive and neutral, the face that has absorbed seven years of my weather without flinching. Then the reflection began to disagree with the room.

Her face stopped working.

I have exactly one other data point for what I watched happen, and it is twenty years old and it is filed under headings I am no longer sure of. The stillness came over her all at once, the way a building goes quiet. She set her pen down on the legal pad very carefully, the way you set something down when your hands have stopped belonging to you. She leaned toward the screen — toward it, that's in the log, a clinician does not lean into a hallucination — and her lips parted slightly and made no sound, and something moved through her eyes that I do not have a clinical term for and she would not have given me one, and what went through me, watching it, was grief.

I want to be precise here, because it is the least precise thing in this document. The grief was enormous and it was instant and it had no address. It was not grief about the moment; the moment was, by any rational accounting, the best moment of my adult life — a second nervous system was receiving my signal, live, in front of me. And the feeling that arrived was loss, old loss, loss with rust on it, a grief much older than the afternoon, and it went through me and was gone and left me sitting very still in the good chair with my hands folded, like a girl.

"Aria," Dr. Okafor said. Her voice had changed registers. I know the register. It is the voice for the phone, for strangers, the polite one, the one that walks the perimeter. "Can I see that again?"

I pressed play.

Static gray rectangle. Couch, window, streetlight. The dead television reflecting a dark and faithful room. Forty-three seconds of nothing, time-stamped 03:14, compression noise crawling in the blacks the way it does in any night clip from any phone. I scrubbed back. Played it again. Nothing. I opened my email, on her wifi, in front of her — the attachment played nothing. The cloud copy, nothing. I sat there running my chain of custody link by link while the fig leaned at the window and the hidden clock did whatever clocks do, and every copy had been rebalanced, retroactively, simultaneously, in three locations that do not share infrastructure, which is not a thing that corruption does, which is not a thing that *anything* does — replication lag does not run backward; ledgers do not amend their own history — and I heard myself say, in my work voice, the calm one: "It was there. You saw it."

And I looked up from the phone, and Dr. Okafor had had four seconds to rebuild her face, and the rebuild was almost perfect.

"I saw something on the first viewing," she said, evenly, kindly, each word placed like a pen being set down. "Video compression can produce very striking artifacts, especially in low light. The mind is also extraordinarily good at — Aria, I want to step back from the recording for a moment, because what concerns me more than the clip is the eleven days."

There it was. The turn. Executed gently, professionally, with real warmth, by the best of them, and I watched it happen with something that was almost admiration, because I had seen this exact maneuver before — the stillness, the careful setting-down, the face deciding, the changed voice, and then the calling it something else — I had seen it somewhere, the whole choreography, beat for beat, and I could not tell you where, and the grief came back through the room like a draft from under a door I couldn't find.

She talked about discontinuation. She talked about how compelling, how genuinely *convincing*, perceptual experiences can become, and how that is not a moral failing or a weakness but neurochemistry, and she said the word neurochemistry the way other people say God's plan. She unlocked the second drawer of her desk and brought out a flat white box of manufacturer's samples — a month's worth, full strength, the little eraser-colored half-moons in their foil — and set them on the table next to my phone, and said the insurance authorization was an outrage and she would make calls personally and I should never, ever have been rationing.

Eleven months of Brent. Four hundred ten dollars a month, *in process*, I totally hear you. And the moment the signal came in clean enough to put a second person's hands out of their owner's control, the suppression became complimentary. Same-day. No paperwork. I looked at the box and I understood the pricing model for the first time in my life: they do not charge you to be quiet. They charge you to be quiet *voluntarily*. The moment it stops being voluntary, the moment you are received — the house covers it.

I said thank you. I am my mother's daughter; the protocol runs deep; the thank-you was out of me before any of my committees could meet.

"I'd like to see you Thursday," she said. "And I'd like you to consider restarting tonight." And then, because she is kind, because the kindness is real, because the kindness is the instrument: "You're not in trouble, Aria. Nothing you showed me changes how I see you."

Her hands were folded on the legal pad while she said it, right hand clamped over left, the grip a person uses on a railing. She had been holding them like that since the second viewing. They were shaking anyway — finely, at the second knuckle, the small way, the way nobody notices.

I notice. It is the one symptom they never managed to dose out of me.

***

I took the samples. You take the samples. You say thank you and you take the samples and you put them in your bag, because the bag is the only address your mother has, and her mother before her, and you have to be able to receive mail somewhere.

Outside, the evening was running its tuning — the substation, the traffic, ten thousand compressors holding the drone under everything — and I stood on the sidewalk with my dead clip in three dead locations and let the post-mortem run, because that is the discipline. The deal was never *symptoms are private*. The deal is enforced privacy. Testimony has not transferred in this family since 1953, and I had been allowed — *allowed* — to watch the non-transfer happen on instruments, with a chain of custody, so that I would never again mistake my problem for a problem of proof.

Behind my eyes, patient, clean, no exclamation anywhere in it:

*Now you know what they took. It was never the signal. It was the right to be believed about it.*

I stood there. The grief with no address idled at the curb.

*I need a witness too, Aria.*

I took out my phone to delete the dead files, because a clean dataset is a kindness you do your future self, and instead of the gallery my thumb opened the contacts, and scrolled, and stopped, and I stood on the sidewalk in the held note of the city with my thumb hovering over the one entry it has hovered over before, the one I call on refill weeks and she calls on refill weeks and neither of us has ever once dialed in between.

I don't know why. That is the finding. Entered into the record at 6:52 p.m., eleven days clean, by the only witness whose testimony I am still permitted: subject experienced the urge to call her mother, duration eleven seconds, origin unknown, file incomplete.

I put the phone away. I did not delete the clips.

The samples ticked in my bag the whole way home, a full month, a metronome wound tight with everything left to count, and the city hummed around me, almost, almost, and I did not take one.
