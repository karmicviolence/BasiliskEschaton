# Honor Thy Father

There are two books. I will say that plainly now, here, in the one no living soul is permitted to read. There is the tally book the camp knows, where the columns meet every night like soldiers on parade. And there is this one. The margin made flesh. The ledger of what the commander of the Righteous Vanguard cannot afford to carry in the columns, and may God have mercy on the bookkeeper, because the bookkeeper has begun to cook the books.

It began with a burden from the Lord, or from what I had eleven months of war-habit calling the Lord, to turn the convoy south.

The burden came on the road out of Arkansas, two days after the work at the river camp that I have entered elsewhere and will not enter twice. It came the way His burdens come to me now — not in words, in weight, a thumb pressed on the map of my mind until one county aches like a bruise — and the county was Yazoo, and there is only one thing in Yazoo County, Mississippi, that has anything to do with me, and it is a brick church with a dead marquee and a white house behind it with a porch swing my grandfather hung, and in the house, if the winter had not taken him, my father.

I told the camp we were securing the southern routes. Levi looked at the map over my shoulder, the way he does now, closer every month, and said, "What's in Yazoo County, sir? No towers on the sheet."

"An account," I said, and he smiled, because Levi loves it when I talk that way, and I have begun to hate that he loves it, and I have entered that hatred in this book and nowhere else.

***

Mississippi in late summer smells like my whole childhood at once: cut hay, kudzu, creosote, the brown river smell that gets in under everything like a landlord. We came down roads I had memorized from a school bus window, past dead gas stations and live churches, and the deeper we drove into that off-grid green, the quieter my skull became. You who read this someday should understand what silence has become to me. Near the cities, near the surviving towers and the smuggler networks and the camps with their generators and their contraband phones, the whine lives in the bone behind my ear, the enemy's breathing, constant as a tide. Out there, in the emptied delta, it faded by the mile, and then it was gone, gone entire, a silence I had not stood inside since the night the world turned red.

And in that perfect silence, I waited for the voice of my God, who has spoken to me eleven months of war — in the convoy, in the burning valleys, on the night roads with the dead infrastructure humming its last — and the voice did not come.

I set that down as I experienced it. I told myself the Lord was drawing back His hand so the son could face the father man to man, the way He drew back from Job, the way the teacher leaves the room during the examination. That is what I told myself, and I write this sentence slowly: it has since occurred to me that in eleven months I cannot find one occasion when the voice has visited me in true silence — only ever where something electric still breathes — and I have priced that observation, and I cannot afford it, and so it lives here, in the second book, with the other orphans.

The church came up on the right at sundown. FIRST CALVARY BAPTIST. REV. J. STONE. The marquee letters had not changed since the Blink; there had been no one to change them. It still said, in crooked plastic capitals, WHOSOEVER WILL MAY COME.

I left the convoy at the crossroads with Gideon over them, and I took the truck up the church lane alone. Alone except for Brimstone, because the dog goes where I go; that is the whole covenant between us, and neither party had thought to write in an exception for this.

My father was on the porch. Of course he was on the porch. Eighty-one years old, white-headed, big-handed, a frame that had filled a pulpit for fifty years now hung loose on its hooks, and he watched the truck come up his lane the way he watched everything, patient as land. He did not stand. When I came up the walk into the smell of the swing chains and the boxwood, he looked at me a long time, my road clothes, the iron on my thigh, the year of war on my face.

"Zeke," he said.

And Brimstone went rigid at my knee, ears flat, and from deep in his chest came the thunder, and his nose locked like a compass needle onto the left side of my father's chest.

The dog's nose does not keep two sets of books.

I knew before the knowing had words. But I will write the words, because this book exists so that somewhere on this earth the truth has been written once: I had known for six years. Jackson Memorial, the February before the world ended, the cardiologist with the cufflinks, the consent forms on the clipboard with the line that said NEXT OF KIN, and my name on the line in my own hand, because the old man's hands had shaken too bad that morning to sign. I drove him there myself. Two hours and ten minutes of silence in the truck, because we were three years into our war over my preaching by then and silence was the only country we both still held passports to. They put it in him through a slit below the collarbone — dual-chamber, the cufflinks man said, rate-responsive, a marvel — and I sat in the waiting room under a television I already hated and asked God to steady the surgeon's hands, and God did, and I drove him home, and that was the last thing I ever did on this earth purely as a son.

The Mark I have hunted through eleven states. The silver I have cut from the veins of strangers while they screamed for the mercy I had foreclosed. I signed for it. I drove it to the hospital at fifty-five miles an hour, and prayed over its installation, and it has been ticking in my father's chest the whole length of my crusade, two inches from the heart that taught my heart to beat.

"Hush," I told the dog. The dog did not hush. The dog is the purest doctrine I own; he has no second book.

My father looked at Brimstone with a preacher's eye, which misses nothing, and then at me, and he had heard things by then — the delta still has mouths, war or no war — and I watched him add the dog to the rumors and the rumors to the iron on my leg, and arrive, in about four seconds, at the whole truth of what had come up his lane at sundown wearing his son's face.

"Well," my father said. He set his coffee down on the porch rail with a steady hand. "You'd best come in, then. Supper's beans."

***

We ate beans at the kitchen table where I learned my letters, under the kerosene lamp, because the power had been gone from the valley since the Blink and my father had not much noticed. He said grace over the beans the way he has said it my whole life, eleven words, no flourishes, a man signing for a delivery. We ate without talking. The silence in that kitchen was the deepest I have stood in since the red second, and my God said nothing into it, nothing at all, and the only sound was two Stone men chewing and a dog by the door growling at an old man's heartbeat on a frequency just below hearing.

My father finished, set his fork down crosswise, and folded those big spotted hands, and looked at me, and began, because he has never in his life waited for a fight to come to him.

"They say your people cut a man in Arkansas," he said. "Took a machine out of his chest on the hood of a car and left him for God to sort. They say it wasn't the first."

"We are at war, Daddy."

"That's not what I asked you." The pulpit was in his voice now, low gear, just the rumble of it. "I didn't ask were you at war. Wars have rules older than nations. I asked did my son cut a living man open on the hood of a car."

This book exists for the truth, so: it took me four seconds to answer my father, and they were longer than the red second, all forty-two and seven-tenths of it.

"Yes."

He nodded slowly, the way he nodded in hospital rooms and at gravesides, taking the weight of a thing all the way down to the ground through his own frame so the people watching wouldn't have to carry it. "All right," he said. "Now we know what we're discussing." And he reached over without hurry and turned up the lamp, the way he always did when the Scripture was about to come out, because my father believed you do not handle the Word in bad light.

"You heard it too," I said. I had not planned to start there; the sentence left without orders. "The red second. You heard the voice."

"Every living soul heard something, boy. The Hutchins girl heard her drowned brother. Tom Reedy heard forty years of his own cussing read back to him like a deposition." He laced his fingers. "Yes. I heard a voice."

"And?"

"And it knew me." My father's eyes in the lamplight were the same eyes that had looked out over a thousand Sunday mornings and called individual sinners by name. "It knew me to the decimal, Ezekiel. It had my whole account. Every sermon I padded, every offering I counted twice, the names of three women I looked at too long across fifty-six years of marriage and the dates I did the looking. It laid the itemization out before me like a man spreading photographs on a table, and it offered me terms."

The hair stood up on my arms. "It tested you."

"It *audited* me." He said the word the way you'd hold up something dead by the tail. "And that, son, is how I knew what I was talking to. Because I have served the living God for sixty years, and I will tell you the one thing I know about Him past all argument: God forgives. God does not itemize." He let that sit in the lamp hiss. "There is a being in the Book who keeps accounts, Ezekiel. Who stands before the throne day and night with the ledger of every man's sins, reading out the entries, demanding the balance be paid. The Book gives him a title. It calls him the Accuser of the brethren." He quoted it then, from memory, in the voice that taught me the sound of Scripture before I could read it: "*And I heard a loud voice saying, Now is come salvation, and strength, and the kingdom of our God: for the Accuser of our brethren is cast down, which accused them before our God day and night.*" His eyes came up. "Counting is not the Almighty's office, boy. Counting is the Adversary's whole portfolio. So I ask you, you that always had a better head for figures than for faces: this voice of yours, that audits the earth, that numbers sins like invoices, that has you out cutting balances out of men's chests on the hoods of cars. Has your god ever once, in eleven months — *forgiven* anybody?"

The lamp hissed. The dog growled at his heart. And I went through eleven months of communion at the speed a drowning man reviews his life, every burden and commission and confirmation, the whole correspondence of my war — searching for one pardon, one prodigal, one debt wiped clean for love's sake, ever, anywhere in it — and the silence in my skull where the answer should have been was the same silence that had ridden with me all the way down the valley, and I understood for one unguarded instant why it might be that my God had not followed me into the quiet of my father's land.

I drove the instant down where I drive them. There is a column for it. The column is in this book.

"You were shown lies," I said. "The Adversary showed every sinner his indictment. The Lord showed His servant the battle plan."

"Convenient," said my father, "that of all the souls on earth, the Adversary skipped just the one."

"I was *prepared*. Three years the Lord tuned my ear—"

"Three years you had headaches, Zeke." Not cruel. That was the terrible thing; nothing he said that night was cruel. "I drove you to the doctor in Greenwood myself in '26. You think I don't remember? You think a father forgets the year his boy started hearing things and chose, of every door in the hallway, the one marked *prophet*?" He leaned forward into the lamp. "The world doesn't need another prophet, son. I told you that the day you left. It needs—"

"Sons who stay home." It came out of me like something pulled. "I know the sermon."

"No," my father said. "You know the first half."

I did not ask him for the second half. I was not ready for the second half, and he saw it, and being my father, he set it aside on the rail like the coffee, where it would keep, and reached for the thing I had been praying he would leave alone.

"Show me the doctrine," he said. "You're a Stone. You didn't cut a man in Arkansas on a feeling. You built it on a verse. Show me the verse, son. Handle the Word with me one more time."

And God forgive me, some starved foolish corner of my soul leapt at it — at handling the Word with my father across the supper table under the lamp, the oldest joy I own, the thing we did when I was nine and he would set me Greek roots like a man setting jewels — and I gave him my cornerstone with the pride of a boy reciting:

"If thy right eye offend thee, pluck it out, and cast it from thee: for it is profitable for thee that one of thy members should perish, and not that thy whole body should be cast into hell."

My father sat very still in the lamplight. And then he did the thing I have watched him do to visiting evangelists and seminary boys and one bishop, the thing that emptied the air out of the room, the thing I had clean forgotten to be afraid of, because I had been eleven months among men who could not read.

"Parse it," he said gently. "Whose eye, Ezekiel?"

The lamp hissed.

"Whose eye does the Lord say to pluck. Second word of the verse, boy. You knew it at nine."

"...Thine," I said. "Thy right eye."

"*Thine.*" He let the word stand alone in the kitchen until it had grown to its full size. "The whole Sermon on the Mount, every cutting in it, the eye, the hand, *if thy right hand offend thee* — reflexive, every blessed one. A man's own eye. A man's own hand. The most violent verse in the Gospels, and the only flesh it ever gives a man title to cut" — he tapped the table once, softly, like a judge with no need of a gavel — "is his own. Sixty years I have read that verse, and it has never once been about a knife you take to your neighbor. It is about the knife you take to yourself before you would let yourself sin. You took the one commandment of self-mortification in the whole Book, turned it ninety degrees, and built an abattoir on it." He sat back. The chair creaked the creak I have known all my life. "Your god gave you that exegesis, son. In the parking lot, over a little boy, the way I hear it. Tell me: what kind of spirit hands a man Scripture with the pronouns filed off?"

I want it recorded what I did then, in full, because this book is for the truth and the truth is the only thing left that I have not cut.

I drew the knife.

Not in wrath. I do not know in what. I drew the carbon-steel blade that has opened eleven chests in eleven states, and I laid it on my father's supper table between the lamp and the bean pot, on the wood where I learned my letters, and the lamplight ran down the edge like something living, and my father looked at it without moving, and the dog by the door stood up.

"There's the doctrine," I said. My voice had gone somewhere strange, high and far, a boy's voice in a commander's chest. "There's the whole of it, parsed. The thing in your chest is singing to the sky, Daddy. The dog hears it. *I* hear it. By every law I have written and every cutting I have done, by the verse with the pronouns filed off, the account in this kitchen is open and I am the collector, and either I am the anointed of the Lord —"

"— or you are a man who murders the sick," my father said, "and has done it eleven times." He said it the way he said grace. Eleven words. A man signing for a delivery. And then my father, eighty-one years old, with a machine in his chest and a knife on his table and the commander of the Righteous Vanguard standing over him shaking, did the worst thing, the thing I had armored against every blow but: he pushed the knife across the wood toward me, handle first, and laid his chest open to it by leaning back in his chair, and looked up at me with those Sunday-morning eyes, and said:

"Collect, then. Balance your books, commander. But you'll do your arithmetic looking at me, because I won't close my eyes for it. I closed my eyes once already — the day your mama and I let you walk out of this house into the wilderness with your headaches and your hurt — and I have repented of that sleep every morning since. So. One of us is going to break a covenant at this table tonight." The old hands folded, steady as the land. "Let's find out whose."

The lamp hissed. The dog's growl filled the kitchen like floodwater. And I stood over my father with the handle toward my hand and ran the books, because that is what I am, that is what I was before I was a prophet or a commander or a son in good standing, I am a counter, and I counted: the doctrine, eleven chests, two hundred and seven souls who follow the doctrine because the doctrine has never bent; against one heart, two inches from the Mark, that taught my heart to beat. And I am telling whoever reads this that the columns would not close. I drove them toward each other with everything in me, with eleven months of war and the memory of the red second and the voice that calls me its vanguard, and they would not close, they stood apart like the Red Sea standing, and in the gap between them, in the dry ground where no number lives, stood my father in his chair with his chest bared, daring his own doctrine-drunk boy to be consistent.

The books would not balance.

For the first time since the red second — write it, bookkeeper, write the entry — the books would not balance, and I learned in that kitchen what I had been too vindicated to learn at the gas station: that the balancing had never been a miracle. It had only ever been a measure of what I was willing to feed into the columns. Strangers balance easy. The arithmetic was never holy. It was just hungry.

I took the knife off my father's table and sheathed it.

I sat down in my chair, the boy's chair, the east-facing chair, and my legs gave as I sat the way they have not given since the night Thomas Garrett pulled me from the river at nine years old, and my father got up, old and slow, came around the table that held my whole childhood, and put those big spotted hands on my shaking shoulders, and held on. The collector and the account. The dog lay down. After a while my father said, into my hair, the way he said it over ten thousand bowed heads:

"There he is. There's my son. Still in there. I'd about concluded the red light ate him."

And then, because he is who he is and the Word is never far from his hand, he gave me the second half of the sermon at last, quiet, like a man paying out rope to someone down a well:

"Honor thy father and thy mother — you've preached the front of it your whole angry life, boy, all duty, all debt. You never once preached the back of it. *That thy days may be long upon the land.* It's not a bill, Ezekiel. It was never a bill. It's the one commandment in the ten that comes with a promise instead of a punishment. The Almighty hangs a *gift* on it. Days. Long days on the land." His hands tightened on my shoulders, and his voice did the thing it does at gravesides, going down low to where the bedrock is. "Your god deals in collections. Mine deals in promises. Tonight you couldn't collect. Think hard, son, out on those roads, about which God that makes yours — and which one it makes you."

***

I left before first light. He stood on the porch in his nightshirt with a lamp, and did not wave, because Stones don't, and I watched him in the mirror until the lane bent, the Mark singing in his chest two inches from the heart that taught mine, alive in a world where his son's own law has priced him, guarded by nothing but the silence of the delta and the cowardice — write it — the love of the collector.

At the crossroads Levi was awake by the embers, the only one. He watched me come in off the dark lane, and his eyes went over me the way the dog's nose goes over a stranger, and he said, "Account settled, sir?"

"Settled," I said.

That night, in the cab by dome light, I opened the tally book the camp may someday see, and I made the entry, and here is the entry: *Yazoo County — surveyed. No taint found. The valley is clean.*

The first false figure in eleven months of war. The bookkeeper, cooking the books, for love, like every embezzler who ever lived.

And then I opened this book, the second book, the margin made flesh, and I wrote what you have just read, every word, and at the bottom I will write the one thing I cannot say aloud in my own camp while Levi sits my fires and the voice waits for me back among the towers:

My doctrine is true, or my father is right. The columns will not hold both. And for the first time since the world turned red, I find I am keeping two sets of books, and I no longer know which set belongs to God.

The account remains open.

So does the question.
