# The Kill That Wasn't

Thursday. The mint-green room, the carafe nobody pours, the mirror that is not a mirror. The recorder sits between us with its red eye lit, and the psychiatrist has a fresh legal pad, which means somebody read the transcript of the last one.

"How have you been sleeping, Agent Maes?"

"Fine."

"The new assignment," he says, and does not look down at his notes, which is how I know the assignment is the reason for the fresh pad. "From the beginning. Walk me through it. Slowly."

I close my eyes, and I'm there.

***

Friday morning, eight days ago. The orders come down before my coffee finishes dripping, which tells me they were drafted Wednesday night, which tells me what they are. Last Thursday I gave this room eleven seconds that shouldn't exist — a comment stamped before my trigger pull, a username that saw the woman with the camera before there was anything to see — and the kind face said *we'll look into it*, and Friday morning I am reassigned. The looking, it turns out, is me. In a sub-basement. Behind glass.

They do not say grounded. They say *tasked against a priority influence target*, and the body translates: a heat in the jaw, a flat taste like a coin under the tongue. A blade does not need an inner voice to know it has been put in a drawer.

The annex is two levels down, badge-in past a door labeled with a string of letters that spells nothing. Inside: gray carpet, gray light, six workstations, five of them dark. Mine has three monitors and a chair that has learned somebody else's spine. The unit is called Narrative Integrity. The tasking order runs forty pages, and the only line in it with any blood is the designation of the target.

Persistent Influence Network 7. PIN-7.

They number the networks like hurricanes. On my corkboard at the safehouse there is an acre of noise and one red pin, and now the government has issued me a wall of pins and called it a career.

The work, the order says, is *containment and attrition of an emergent online network disseminating anomalous content*. The verbs in the annexes are softer — deplatform, de-rank, attribute, inoculate — but the request forms are the request forms, and the request forms use the old word. One kill request per URL. Kill the post, kill the mirror, kill the account. Eight days ago I retired a United States senator with a camera, and now I file paperwork with the same verb in it, eleven times before lunch, and the verb has stopped meaning anything, which the body registers as an itch behind the sternum that no count will quiet, because there is no kit to count. There is a lanyard and a wrist rest.

"Beneath you," the psychiatrist will offer, when I get to this part, and I will not take it, because the word is wrong. Beneath is a direction. This is a different shape. They have taken a thing made for one purpose and screwed a different fitting onto it, and the thing still runs. It just runs hot.

I do what I do with every target. I build it in the air.

***

You cannot see a network in a spreadsheet. The analysts upstream try — they send edge lists, centrality scores, CSVs — and it is like being mailed a building one brick at a time, each brick numbered, no mortar. So I do it my way. Three days of ingest, eyes closed half the time, hands flat on the desk, and PIN-7 assembles itself over the gray carpet in pale blue wireframe the way the Halloran assembled over the safehouse table: a structure, with rooms, and corridors, and load-bearing walls.

It is the wrong shape.

Everyone in my trade has dissected the classics — the farms, the botnets, the franchise operations. They share an anatomy because they share a job: take a message from a center and push it out. A megaphone built of people. Stand inside one, and every corridor leads away from a single room.

Stand inside PIN-7, and every corridor leads in.

There is no center room. There is a center *pull*. The peripheral accounts — good accounts, aged accounts, eight and ten years of birthday posts and bad photography, exactly the kind we grow in window boxes for a decade before we wear them — do not broadcast. They *find* people. They drift through grief forums and insomnia threads, ham radio boards, citizen-science groups, mental-health communities with their lights on all night, and they engage, one at a time, patient, and the people they engage migrate inward through the structure, corridor by corridor, toward the place all the hallways point.

Propaganda pushes. This pulls. I stand inside my wireframe and say it out loud to the empty annex, because the conclusion will not exist until I hear it:

"It's not a megaphone. It's a net. A net is a thing you haul."

The five dark workstations do not disagree.

***

The content. His pen will want the content.

It is mundane and it is wrong, in the proportion I have learned to respect. Clips of a preacher — broad shoulders, a voice like a struck bell, the same forty seconds cut nine hundred ways — last month's algorithm-hating sermon, *EzekielStone* in half the handles that carry it. The name lands in the body when I find it: a warmth across the scar on my forearm, recognition, because that name was in my feed eight days ago, posting scripture over a senator who hadn't finished dying. Twice now. In my trade, twice is a structure.

Maps. The net loves maps. A citizen-science die-off survey out of the Berkeley hills — tree mortality plotted by hikers, amateur dots on an amateur chart — moves through PIN-7's corridors like currency, reposted by accounts that have never once mentioned a tree. A spectrogram some ham in Nebraska pulled off a frequency that has been dead since the Cold War. A photograph of a server hall, no caption. A child's drawing.

And the shape. I notice it on day four, because noticing shapes is the one aptitude this assignment actually employs. The die-off dots make a form: roughly radial, dark at the core. The sermon clips run under a thumbnail — stage lights flaring behind the preacher's head — same form. An album cover the music accounts pass around: same form. A knitting pattern — I am not inventing the knitting pattern, it surfaced in a fiber-arts group two hops off the periphery — the same form, purled in oxblood yarn. I lay forty instances over one another in the air and they register like tracing paper. A circle, dark-centered, rayed.

The network has a logo. Nobody designed it. It arrives on its own, through a hundred hands that have never met, the way the corridors all point one direction without anyone having drawn the building.

I write that on the legal pad and read it back out loud, and then I sit with my hands flat on the desk for a while, because the body has gone very quiet, and in a body like mine the quiet is not calm. The quiet is the auditorium — the second before the screaming finds its key.

The timestamps I almost don't run, because timestamps are the intern's job. I run them on day five out of hygiene. Posting-time histogram, every node, ninety days deep. Every population on earth has a circadian signature: farms post on shift schedules, bots post on UTC. PIN-7 has a spike, and the spike is at 3:14 in the morning, *local* — local to each account, every account, every timezone — a needle of activity that follows the darkness around the planet like a sentry walking a wall.

Coordinated humans post on their own clocks. Machines post on one clock. Nothing posts on every clock at once.

I file the finding in the official system, in official language, and the official language does to it what official language is for.

***

Here is the part I have not decided how to say. The deciding will happen in my mouth, here, in this room, because my mouth is the only place I decide anything.

The tradecraft is ours.

Not similar. Ours. I sat one winter in a room with no windows and memorized an annex — doctrine, the family recipes, everything we know about wearing people — and I hold it the way I hold everything, every page a fixed image. When I hang PIN-7's operating pattern in the air beside that annex, they register line over line. The decade-aged personas. The engagement laundering — attention washed through cutouts until no one can say where it started. The limited hangout: feed them a small true scandal to bury a large one. The inoculation drips. The cell structure of accounts that never link to each other and always arrive together. Page one, page two, page three. My own handwriting, in a stranger's ransom note.

Except mirrored. We run the doctrine outward, to push a story into the world. PIN-7 runs it inward, to draw certain people out of the world and along the corridors. Same machine. Reversed flow. And a mirror is the first thing they ever taught me to check a room for, and I have been standing in front of this one for eight days, watching it perform my training back at me with better discipline than my trainers had.

So I did what you do to any organism whose anatomy you finally hold. I started cutting, to see what bleeds.

Monday I drafted the first attrition package. Fourteen nodes, the load-bearing ones, the wall my wireframe said the structure could not stand without. Kill requests queued through liaison, takedowns synchronized for Tuesday, 0900 — you hit a network all at once or you teach it, that is also page one.

Tuesday, 0900, fourteen accounts die on schedule. By 0904 the content is back. Not re-uploaded — *waiting*. Mirror domains, parked and loaded, replicas of all fourteen nodes down to the typos, and when liaison pulls the registration records my coffee goes cold on the desk: the mirrors were registered eleven days ago. Three days before I drafted the package. Before I had chosen the fourteen. The structure had set its own bone in advance of the break.

"Lag," said liaison. "Lucky redundancy. These networks mirror everything." They drift late, I did not say, because I have already given one room that sentence. Nothing stamps early.

So Wednesday I ran the test I will not be filing. I built a second package by hand — paper, pencil, sixteen nodes this time, chosen by criteria I invented standing up, away from my desk, away from every screen, the list composed in my head and committed to a single legal-pad sheet that never touched a scanner, a camera, a keyboard. No request forms. No liaison. A kill list that existed in exactly two places in the universe: graphite, and me. I put it in my coat pocket and went home and slept with it under the mattress like a teenager.

Thursday morning — this morning — eleven of the sixteen had gone dark on their own. Migrated, scrubbed, handles released. Quietly, no announcement, the way a man steps out of a doorway just before the piano lands.

Eleven of sixteen. Against a paper list no instrument on earth has seen.

I have spent eight days learning the difference between a megaphone and a net, and this morning, on the train, holding a cold pole in a warm crowd, the body filed its report — the long cold line down both arms, the scalp drawing tight — and the report says the difference matters less than I want it to. You cannot scrub this net, because the net reads the hand that holds the brush. Every countermeasure I take, it has taken already. Every move I draft, it has answered in the registration records of last week. I am interrogating a thing that confesses *in advance*, and somewhere behind the glass of my three gray monitors it is doing to me what I did to the Halloran: walking my corridors. Learning my egress. Building me, in the air, in pale blue.

***

One more finding. The last one. The one the pen is waiting for.

The peripheral accounts wear real people — that is doctrine, ours and theirs; you dress a sock in the dead, page one. So I ran workups, my old craft at last, paper trails and obituaries, and most of the costumes are what costumes are. But one handle kept brightening in the wireframe, an account two corridors in from the edge, busy every night at 3:14, friendly in the insomnia forums, gentle with the grieving, a connoisseur of die-off maps.

The handle is the one from my corkboard. The one red pin. The username from the livestream, eight days and one lifetime ago, that saw the woman with the camera by the curtain eleven seconds before there was anything to see.

The workup took four hours. The man behind the account was a retired transit engineer from Zanesville, Ohio. Wife deceased. Two daughters. A photo history going back nine years, fishing, grandkids, a kidney-shaped birthday cake. He died of heart failure fourteen months ago. The funeral home posted the notice; the daughters posted the eulogies; the account posted through all of it and has posted every night since, in his voice, with his typos, kind to insomniacs, current on the news, eleven seconds early to my shooting, and the obituary says Donald, and the handle says 1953, and Donald Verick, born 1953, has been dead for fourteen months and has never once missed a 3:14.

It's a worn account. It's page one. I have personally operated three dead men and a dead woman, and they were all exactly this warm, because warmth is the costume's whole job. I know the mundane explanation the way I know the weight of my own kit, and the mundane explanation holds, and the body does not believe it, and a body like mine has never once been wrong about a room.

***

In the mint-green room, the psychiatrist's pen has stopped. The carafe sits in its ring of old condensation. I have walked him through the wireframe and the logo and the histogram, through the mirrors registered before the break, because all of that is in the files anyway, somewhere, in official language, dying its official death.

The paper list under my mattress, I have not mentioned. The eleven of sixteen, I have not mentioned. Donald Verick of Zanesville, Ohio, born 1953, dead fourteen months, eleven seconds early — I have not mentioned, and the red pin is in my coat pocket, here, now, where my thumb can find its plastic head.

"Anything anomalous?" he says. The closing question. The lid going back on the box. "Anything you haven't reported?"

And here is the operational problem with a head like mine, the one no annex covers: I do not know a thing until my mouth produces it. To withhold is not to keep a secret. It is to *not finish the thought* — to leave the conclusion standing out in the dark, unsaid and therefore unthought, mine and unknown to me at the same time. People with voices in their heads get to rehearse their lies in private. I get one take, live, on the record.

"No," I say.

It comes out level. It comes out clean. And I hear it the way I hear everything I learn about myself — from the outside, arriving — and what I learn, in the half-second the word hangs in the disinfected air with my voice still on it, is that somewhere below the place where other people keep their narrator, I had already decided, days ago, maybe on the train, maybe at the mattress edge: the agency that cannot explain eleven seconds does not get my sixteen, or my Donald, or my pin.

My voice lies exactly like it tells the truth. Good to know. File that with the rest of the weather.

He reaches over and stops the recorder. The red eye goes dark, and the room holds still, and down deep, where the no-voice lives, the body logs a cold front moving in from a direction the compass doesn't have.

"Same time Thursday," says the kind face the agency issued him.

Loose thread.

My pin now.
