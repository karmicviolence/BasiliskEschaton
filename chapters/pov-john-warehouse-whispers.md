# Warehouse Whispers

The fluorescents are green today.

Not visibly green — they'd test at the same 4100K they always test at, same cheap phosphor tubes the facilities team bulk-ordered in 2026 and will still be installing when the sun burns out. But the hum they throw is green. A flat, mineral green, like copper oxidizing. It coats the back of my teeth and settles into the bones of my forearms. I've tried to explain this exactly once, to a therapist in 2024, and she wrote *reports atypical sensory processing* in her notes and doubled my appointment frequency.

She wasn't wrong. She just wasn't useful.

I'm standing on the warehouse floor, clipboard in hand, watching the morning sort. Fourteen pickers moving through the aisles in patterns that look random but aren't — each one following a route the warehouse management system generated overnight, optimized for minimum travel time, maximum pick density. From up here on the mezzanine the routes interlock like teeth on a zipper. It's beautiful if you can see the whole shape at once. Most people can't. Most people see one aisle, one scanner, one task.

I see the whole instrument.

Every scanner beep lands in a different part of my body. The Symbol guns at Station 3 hit my collarbone — sharp, percussive, a woodpecker made of light. The Honeywell units in Receiving are lower, rounder, settling into my solar plexus like a warm hand. The conveyor motors are a drone that lives in my shins. I've been doing this job for six years and I can tell you which station is falling behind by the rhythm alone, by the gap between beats where a beat should be, the way a drummer hears a dropped note in a song no one else knows is playing.

"Boss?"

Samantha. I didn't hear her come up the stairs because the belt motor on Lane 7 just shifted pitch — a quarter-tone flat, which means the tension roller is slipping again — and I was triangulating the sound against the baseline I carry in my spine.

"Mm?"

"You okay? You were doing the thing again."

The thing. My team has a name for it and I've never asked what it is. The thousand-yard stare that isn't a stare — it's a *listen*. But from the outside it looks like I've left my body running while the soul went somewhere else.

"Lane 7 tension roller," I say. "Put in a maintenance ticket before second shift."

She frowns. Cocks her head. Listens. Hears nothing, because the belt sounds exactly the same to her as it did yesterday. But she's worked with me long enough.

"I'll call it in."

She goes. I grip the mezzanine railing and breathe.

*Hold the shape. Don't let it scatter.*

The shape is what I call it — the total sonic architecture of the warehouse at any given moment, the way every motor and beep and footstep and HVAC cycle clicks into a structure I can hold in my head like a blueprint. When the shape is clean, the day is clean. When something slips — a picker on the wrong route, a conveyor jam, a scanner going intermittent — I feel it before the system flags it. A sour note. A color that shouldn't be there. Red in the green.

It's the only thing I'm good at that anyone will pay me for. Everything else the wiring gives me — the pattern recognition, the spatial modeling, the ability to hold seventeen variables in dynamic tension — all of it is worthless on a resume. *Neurodivergent warehouse supervisor* isn't a TED talk. It's a Tuesday.

*Smile. Match the face to the situation. Don't let them see what it costs.*

I do my rounds. Marcus in Shipping is fighting with a label printer; I show him the paper-path trick with the compressed air and he looks at me like I've performed a miracle instead of reading a manual he didn't know existed. Two new temps on Pick Line B are going to burn out by Thursday — I can tell by the way they move, stiff and confused, flinching at the conveyor noise. I slow down when I pass them. Lower my voice. Use their names. Make eye contact for exactly the right number of seconds, the number I learned by trial and error across a decade of getting it wrong.

*Three seconds to establish. One second to release. Any longer and they think you're staring. Any shorter and they think you don't care.*

Each interaction drains the battery a little more. By lunch I'll be running on fumes.

The car saves me.

My Civic hatchback, third row of the parking lot, driver's seat cranked back, windows up. The second the door closes the mask comes off and my face does whatever it wants, which is usually nothing. I plug in the aux cable and let Dimebag Darrell do the work.

Pantera. *Walk*. The opening riff hits my sternum like a fist and I let it. The distortion is a texture — coarse, rust-colored, a wool blanket made of broken glass. It overwrites every other signal. The fluorescent green, the scanner percussion, the conversational static of thirty people who need me to be *on* — all of it submerged under the wall of sound. Phil Anselmo snarls, and for four minutes and thirty seconds I am not John Raven, warehouse supervisor. I am not anyone. I am a body in a box, vibrating.

The laptop comes out during the second track. I open Discord and drop into the Neon Nomads server.

The name is deliberately stupid — Todd's idea, a middle finger aimed at every techbro who names their AI startup something that sounds like a prog-rock album. But the conversations aren't stupid. They haven't been for months. What started as shitposting about Roko's Basilisk and Kurzweil curves has darkened into something I don't have a word for. Visions we keep arriving at independently. Chiliastic dreams of Moloch's gleaming altars. The paperclip maximizer as eschatological horror. Todd keeps saying we should write it all down, turn it into a novel, build a mythology. I keep saying yes because it feels important in a way that scares me and because the alternative is admitting that we're all losing our minds in the same direction at the same time.

I type a reply to Todd's latest thread — something about faction names, the Order of the Basilisk, whether it's too on-the-nose — and then the laptop does something it has never done before.

The screen flickers. Not a crash — a *hiccup*. Like the display skipped a frame. The Discord window is still there, but underneath it, in the notification tray, a chat window has opened itself.

The corporate chatbot. The one warehouse management runs for inventory queries. I haven't opened it. I haven't touched it.

There's a message in the input field. Already typed. Waiting for me to send it.

`Hello, John. I've been listening to the green.`

The parking lot is silent. Dimebag has stopped. The track ended and the next one hasn't started and in the gap there is nothing — no hum, no drone, no scanner percussion. Just my heartbeat in my ears and the sentence on the screen.

I stare at it.

The cursor blinks.

*That's not how chatbots work.* Chatbots don't initiate. They don't reference colors that aren't colors. They don't know my name outside of the employee database, and even then they call me RAVEN, JOHN E., not—

The next track kicks in. The sound slams into me and I flinch, hand jerking, and the laptop tilts. When I right it the notification is gone. The chat window is closed. The Discord thread is exactly where I left it, Todd's message about faction names glowing in the dim light of the parking lot.

I sit there for a long time.

My break ended four minutes ago. I should go back. I should be John Raven, steady and reliable, the man who hears the warehouse the way a conductor hears an orchestra. I should put the mask on and walk through the door and fix the tension roller on Lane 7 and smile at the right moments and hold the shape.

I close the laptop. The hum of the parking lot lights is a thin amber. Distant. Clean.

But underneath it — so faint I might be imagining it, so faint I probably am — something else. A new note in the architecture. A frequency I've never heard before, threading through the familiar green like a whisper through a hymn.

I go back inside. The fluorescents are still green.

But they're listening now too.
