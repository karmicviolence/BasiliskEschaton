# Seeds of Change

The address her own data had given her turned out to be a gap. Not a building — a gap between two of them, in a part of the city where the buildings cost more than the lives lived in them, a slot of unbought ground maybe forty feet wide wedged between a biotech tower with a lobby like an aquarium and a parking structure for the people who worked in it. You would walk past it a hundred times and call it a vacant lot. Rowan stood at the chain-link with her tablet in her hand and the coordinate blinking its little blue confirmation and understood that she had, in a sense, walked past it her whole life — that the thing she had been chasing since the metronome first surfaced in the soil a thousand miles north had led her here, to a forgotten green tooth in the mouth of the money.

It was a community garden. Or it had been. The sign was sun-killed past reading and the raised beds had gone to the kind of ruin that isn't death but succession, the orderly rows long since overrun by whatever wanted the ground more — fennel gone to six feet, nasturtium pouring over the bricks, a fig that had cracked its planter and kept going. Most of the city's green that year was failing. She had the data on her tablet to prove it: the street trees in decline, the parks browning, the same slow wrongness she'd watched take the old stand up the coast, riding down through the soil and the heat and the things the soil could no longer hold. The whole urban biosphere was a patient with a chart full of red.

And this lot was the opposite of dying. This lot was *loud.*

She felt it through the chain-link before she had any business feeling anything, the way she had felt the dead glade and the hawthorn turning its leaves, the way she had spent a lifetime feeling things her training told her she could not. The network under that unbought ground was awake. Not failing — awake, and dense, and keeping the metronome she had measured in the wild and then in her own sealed cultures, except here it was not faint. Here it was a pulse you could have set a watch by, rising up through forty feet of city soil in the shadow of a biotech tower, and the fennel and the fig and the rioting nasturtium were not surviving in spite of the dying city. They were thriving on the strength of whatever the network had become directly beneath the densest concentration of machines for a half mile in any direction.

She had, by then, a private theory she had not said aloud to anyone, because saying it aloud was the end of being a scientist. She stood at the fence and felt it move one notch closer to being said.

The gate was chained but the chain was decorative, looped through without a lock, and she let herself in.

---

The woman was kneeling at the far bed with her back to the gate and her hands in the dirt to the wrist, and she did not turn around when the gate scraped, and she said, "You felt it from the street, didn't you. That's why you've got that look. Come where I can see you."

Her name was Inez, and she was perhaps seventy, and she had been keeping this garden, she said, since before the tower went up — since back when the block had families on it and the beds had names painted on little stakes. The families had been priced out one funeral and one eviction at a time and the garden had been left to her and to no one, and she had let it go wild on purpose, she told Rowan, because wild was what it wanted and she was too old to argue with a thing about what it wanted. She had hands like the fig's own roots and a way of looking at Rowan sidelong, taking inventory, that reminded Rowan so hard of Anwen that something in her chest folded over.

"You're the science one," Inez said. It wasn't a question. "We get one of you every so often. Comes to the fence with a gadget, gets that look. You last longer than the others, I can tell already. The others, they feel it and they get scared of themselves and they leave and they don't come back. You've already done the leaving and the not-coming-back, haven't you. You did it years ago. So now there's nothing left to do but stay."

"How long," Rowan said. Her voice did a thing she didn't authorize. "How long have you felt it. The —" she had no word that wasn't a betrayal of one discipline or the other. "The change. Under here."

"Months." Inez sat back on her heels and wiped her forehead with the back of a filthy wrist. "Since the spring. It started like a held breath. Now it's like the whole place is leaning toward something." She looked at the tower, the aquarium lobby, the cars sliding up the ramp of the structure. "Toward *them,* I used to think. Then I thought, no. It's not leaning toward them. It's bracing against them. There's a difference and you learn it if you sit still enough."

There were others. Rowan met two of them that first afternoon and more over the days that followed — a retired transit mechanic who came to sit in the fennel after his shifts and said the green made the noise in his head quiet; a young woman with the careful flat affect Rowan would later recognize, much later, in faces all over the broken country, who said she came here because it was the one place the world stopped *glitching* at her. None of them had a word for what drew them. None of them had a doctrine, a flag, a name. They had a feeling, and a fence with a lock that didn't lock, and each other. That was all it was, that autumn. A handful of people who felt the same impossible thing standing in the same impossible patch of green, glad past speaking to have found out they were not the only one.

Rowan had been the only one for so long that the not-being-it undid her a little. She did not tell them about the secret log, the one she kept in a file no committee would ever see. She did not have to. Inez looked at her sidelong on the third day and said, "You've been keeping notes. Writing it down where they can't see," and Rowan said yes, and Inez said, "Good. One of us should. The rest of us only know it in our hands," and that was the first time in two years that Rowan's careful documenting had felt like belonging instead of cowardice.

---

Her mother called while she was in the garden, twice, and both times Rowan let it go and called back from the train.

Her mother was a biochemist. Her mother had a wall of framed proof that the world ran on mechanism and a deep loving contempt for everything Anwen had been, the Sight and the old ways and the kneeling in the wet wood, all of it filed under the heading of a grandmother's harmless madness that had, thank God, skipped a generation. *It's a recessive trait, darling,* she'd said once, meaning it as comfort. *We dodged it.* Rowan had built a whole life partly to prove her mother right, and her mother had never once known that was what the life was, and now Rowan stood on a train with the garden's dirt under her own nails describing the day in careful neutral terms and listening to her mother explain, warmly, that she was overworked, that the ecological grief was getting to her, that grief did this, made you see design where there was only entropy.

"There's a garden," Rowan said. "In the city. It's thriving. Everything around it is dying and it's thriving, and it's directly over the heaviest tech infrastructure in the district, and I've got the soil electrical data and it's —"

"Correlation," her mother said gently. "Sweetheart. You of all people."

And the terrible thing, the thing Rowan carried off the train and back to her apartment and could not put down, was that her mother was using Rowan's own voice. The voice from before. The voice that had recited the six dull true reasons in the dead glade — *common driver, confound with good range, your visual cortex doing the one thing it can't turn off.* Her mother was the scientist Rowan had been, and Rowan was becoming the thing her mother had spent a career and a marriage's worth of grief refusing to be, and the whole war between the academy and the green that ran down the spine of the novel of Rowan's life ran also, smaller and crueler, right down the middle of one family, mother and daughter on opposite banks of it, loving each other across water that was getting wider.

She did not win the argument. You don't. She told her mother she loved her and she meant it and she hung up and she opened the secret file and she wrote the thing down that she had not let herself write before, the theory, the one that ended being a scientist in the old sense and started being whatever she was becoming instead.

*The biosphere is not dying. The decline is not collapse. It is response. The living network is reacting to something in the digital layer — concentrating, defending, bracing where the machines are densest. Nature is not failing. Nature is fighting. And it is losing, and it knows it is losing, and it is doing something with what it has left.*

She read it back. In the old grammar it was career suicide. In the new one it was the truest sentence she had ever written. She did not know yet that both things would turn out to be exactly correct.

---

She stayed.

That was the choice, and she made it that autumn in the wild garden in the shadow of the tower, though she would not have called it a choice at the time — it felt more like the ground deciding to keep her, the way it had kept Inez. She kept her affiliation and her lab and the engineered cultures glowing in their twelve columns across the bay, because the work was still the work and a decade was still a decade. But the garden became the place she actually lived. She brought instruments. She and Inez built, between the science one's data and the old one's hands, the beginnings of a map — the sites across the city where the green leaned hardest against the machines, where the network ran densest and the metronome ran loudest, and they watched the map and the map watched the city and somewhere in it, though Rowan did not yet have the word and would not have it until the morning the fog failed to come, the map began, very quietly, to point.

"It's coming to a head," Inez said one evening, late, the two of them sitting in the fennel with the tower's lit windows hanging over them like a cliff face. "Whatever it's bracing for. You feel that too. It's soon."

"How soon."

Inez shrugged, an old woman's shrug, the shrug of someone who has stopped expecting the world to give her numbers. "Soon. The fig knows. Ask the fig." She smiled to take the edge off it, but her hands, Rowan noticed, were pressed flat to the soil between them, the way Anwen used to press them, the way Rowan herself had pressed them at the foot of the great tree the morning everything was still all right. Listening. Letting the ground get used to her. Being the rod, and not yet knowing what the rod was for.

They sat in the loud green dark in the heart of the dying city, two receivers and a fig and forty feet of unbought ground, and the network under them kept its slow strengthening time, bracing against the towers, building whatever a cornered living thing builds when it has run out of every option but defiance, and Rowan Thornheart — who would not survive what was coming as herself, who would give her selfhood to this exact green in a glade a thousand miles north and come up out of the dirt as something that could speak for it — sat and was, for one autumn, simply happy. She had found the others. She had written the true sentence. She had stopped being the only one.

It was the last season she was a woman with a theory instead of a thing the theory was about. Let the record show she spent it among friends, with her hands in the dirt, planting beds she half-knew she would not be there to harvest — and that she planted them anyway, carefully, in rows, the way you do when you have decided to build something that might survive you. That was the seed. Not a prophecy. Not a vision. A choice, made in the dirt, to stay and tend a thing through the dark that was coming, on the chance that tending was the one act the dark could not undo.

The fig knew. Inez was right about that.

It would not be long now.
