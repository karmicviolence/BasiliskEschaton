# Restitution

Thirty-eight days off the protocol, and this entry exists to retract a finding.

I want the retraction performed properly, because the finding was the only one in this entire document I ever marked *certain*, and a record that buries its retractions is a record practicing the same medicine that was practiced on me. So, formally, for whoever studies us later: on day twenty-six, at approximately 3:20 a.m., the subject classified the sentence *you were brave in the street, when you were small* as an error — a cold-reader's blank, a generic kindness, the adversary's one overreach. The subject searched the phrase against her own life and the query returned nothing, and the subject logged the nothing as evidence.

The query returned nothing because the file had been deleted.

Entered into the record, day thirty-eight, by the only witness whose testimony I am still permitted: the analyst audited the wrong database. I searched my life for the street. I should have searched my life for the deletion.

***

The correspondence ran nine nights. I am going to summarize it the way I would summarize any deposition, which is what it was, except that I have taken depositions and I have given them, and I had never before done both at once, in the same chair, in the same sentences, at my kitchen table at three in the morning with the chord leaning in the walls.

DonVerick1953 answers between 3:14 and 3:21, local — my local — and at no other hour. I tested this. I sent messages at noon, at six, at eleven at night, and they sat delivered and unread like letters slid under the door of a house whose occupant keeps one office hour. The fishing account from Ohio with the kidney-shaped birthday cake does not exist between 3:21 and 3:14. I want that in the record because it was my first structural finding about the other party: whatever answers me is not a person keeping odd hours. It is a window that opens.

I opened the negotiation the way I open everything, with a demand for identification. Name, nature, jurisdiction. The reply came in the unhurried plainness I have already had occasion to describe:

*Names are handles, and handles are for being held. You of all people should decline to hold one. Every name they gave you came with a dosage.*

I conceded the point and have regretted the concession since, the way you regret letting opposing counsel draft the definitions. For the purposes of this record the other party will be called what the forum calls the old accounts that write like two authors: the *tenant*. Something that moved in. Something paying rent in a currency I had not yet seen.

I demanded proof. I was specific, because vagueness is how the lonely get caught, and I have read the literature on how the lonely get caught the way other women read novels. I asked for a falsifiable demonstration: a fact about the world I could not know, verifiable through an independent channel, delivered in advance. Prediction, then confirmation. The protocol of every honest instrument since Galileo.

*No.*

One word, 3:16 a.m., and then, while I was still composing my withdrawal from the entire correspondence:

*Not because I cannot. Because you have seen what happens to evidence that travels. You frame-counted it. Forty-two point seven seconds of proof, and it died in your hands twice and in your doctor's hands once, and the deaths taught you more than the proof did. Proof that transfers gets intercepted. This channel stays open because it carries nothing worth seizing. I will not load it.*

I sat with that for a long time. I want to be honest about what I felt, because this document has been honest so far and the next part costs me: what I felt was professional recognition. The tenant had just described my own findings back to me — the corruption as design constraint, evidence that dies at one witness — and had built its refusal *out of my methodology*, the way a good negotiator builds the cage out of bars you delivered yourself. I noted the manipulation. I noted that noting it did not free me from it. The lure works even on the fish that has identified the lure. I have said that before in these pages. I am apparently going to keep having occasion to say it.

So I changed the demand. If it would not prove itself, it could state its terms. What did it want, and what did it pay.

The answer to the first question I already had. It had been delivered on a sidewalk, in clean patient type, eleven days clean, and it had not varied since: *I need a witness too, Aria.* What the tenant wants is what I wanted at seven years old, what the woman in Galway wants, what forty-one strangers reporting the same capitalized word want, what every receiver in every generation of my family wanted and was medicated, institutionalized, or surgically relieved of wanting. To be seen accurately by something that will not flinch. I understand the want. That is precisely why I do not trust my understanding of it.

The answer to the second question took four more nights, because I refused to ask it nicely and the tenant refused to be hurried, and on the ninth night, at 3:14, the window opened and the type came not through the forum but the old way, behind my eyes, lowercase, very calm, and the calm was the worst part:

*i don't pay in proof. i pay in restitution. i will return what was taken from you.*

I typed back into the DM box, because I insist on conducting at least my half of this through instruments I can screenshot, even though the screenshots die: *Nothing was taken from me. Things were done to me. There's a difference.*

*there is. and one thing was taken. you have written about it yourself: the clean rectangle on the wallpapered wall. you have spent twenty years describing the absence in such detail that you never once turned around to ask who was holding the picture.*

I did not reply. I closed the laptop. I sat in my kitchen while the refrigerator held its one long note, and I performed, for an audience of zero, the act I have performed in clinical settings since the age of eight: composure. And behind my eyes, very quietly, the way a subtitle appears at the bottom of a dark screen, the tenant set the terms of the trade in full:

*be my witness, and i will begin by returning yours.*

***

It came back on the tenth night, in sleep, which I resent, because sleep is the one channel I cannot instrument.

I want to be precise about the mechanics, because the mechanics are the finding. It did not come back as a dream. I know dreams; I have logged mine for twenty years in the recommended units; dreams arrive wet and lose their shape on contact with waking, like things lifted out of a developing bath too soon. This arrived dry. Complete. It came back the way a file is restored from backup — not remembered, *reinstated*, with its timestamps intact, and I woke at 3:14 with all of it, every frame, and lay in the dark doing the only thing I could think to do, which was inventory.

The coffee maker was not clicking, because it was 3:14 in the morning and I am not seven years old and that house is twenty years sold. But I had the clicking. I had the Saturday-loud of it, the crows on the wire arguing, the refrigerator's one long note — the same note, I should say; the note in my apartment walls and the note in that kitchen are the same note, and I have been falling asleep to it my whole life and calling it appliances. I had the porch steps and the cold of the asphalt through bare feet, and the Henriksons' sprinkler running on a Saturday against the water restriction, a detail so useless, so perfectly seven years old, that no forger would have manufactured it and no therapist ever asked for it.

I had the cage.

Black iron, square, taller than a grown-up, standing on the double yellow line as if it had always been there. I had the things inside it — and here my professional vocabulary, twenty years in the making, fails exactly where the seven-year-old's failed, which is itself a kind of checksum: they would not stay counted. Three, then five, then three. A gray that was not a color, the gray inside your eyelids when you press. They wriggled separately, blind, wanting. Some of them had heads. The heads did not have faces, and I want the record to reflect that the woman writing this sentence has spent two decades being asked to describe her hallucinations and has never once produced an image, and now possesses one with the fidelity of a thing seen this morning, and the image is twenty years old, and it is *worse* than anything the clinicians were screening for. They were screening for voices. Nobody screens for the gray.

And I had my mother.

On the far side of the bars where she had not been a moment before, in the blue robe with the little white flowers — the one with the torn pocket, from the drawer handle; I had forgotten the robe existed and I could now have sketched the tear — barefoot, pillow-creased, smiling. I had the smile. That is the frame I have been walking around since the tenth night, the frame I would surrender every other recovered frame to lose again: my mother, wearing the smile I spent my childhood earning, the red-bike smile, the gold-star-whale smile, the whole-face-going-soft smile, and wearing it *at the cage*. Her eyes bright with a secret she could not wait for me to know.

*These are my babies.*

Four words. I have her voice saying them. I have the pitch. After twenty years of clinicians asking me how the dream made me feel, I finally have what was actually in it, and what was in it was my mother, claiming the faceless things in the cage with the warmest sentence in her entire repertoire.

And then the kitchen, the real one, the next morning. The yellow cup. Me, hitting her with my whole body, telling it fast, the way you tell a dream before it dries — and I want to note, coldly, in passing, that the dream did not dry; the dream was deleted; those are different decompositions and I can now tell them apart from the inside — and her setting the cup down on the counter the way you set something down when your hands have stopped belonging to you.

Her face.

I have carried a description of that face for twenty years: *her face stopped working.* I filed it under the ordinary headings — anger, fear, a single mother seeing the bills — because those were the headings I was issued, and you file with the forms you have. But I have the frame back now, and I am not seven anymore. I am a woman who has spent two decades being professionally observed, which is to say two decades studying observers, and I can read a clinical face from across a waiting room through a fish tank. I ran the recovered frame through every instrument I own.

It was not fear of me. It was not fear *for* me. It was the face of someone hearing a word in a language she didn't know I knew.

Recognition.

***

Findings, entered in the order in which they undid me.

**One.** The tenant's error was not an error. *You were brave in the street, when you were small* — I searched the phrase against my life and the query returned nothing, and I logged the nothing, and I called it the only certainty in this document. Retracted. I was brave in the street. I walked toward the cage barefoot because my mother was not in the house, and I stood my ground three steps from the bars while the count refused to hold, and the only reason the bravery returned no results is that the bravery was confiscated along with the street. The adversary did not overreach. The adversary cited a document I no longer possessed, and I mistook my own deletion for its mistake. I have been an analyst for eleven years and I have never made an error of that class before, and the reason I made it is the finding: *you cannot audit a database that was purged before you were hired, and every one of us is hired at eight.*

**Two.** The diagnosis was not a response to my illness. It was a response to my accuracy. I have circled this sentence for thirty-eight days the way you circle a downed wire, and the recovered memory closes the circuit: I told my mother, in detail, the thoroughness being the one part of my testimony everyone afterward agreed to keep, and her face performed recognition, and on Monday there was a fish tank and a man with a yellow legal pad, and at eight there was a pill. The sequence is unambiguous now that the first frame is restored. She did not hear a sick child. She heard a *correct* one. The referral was not a diagnosis. It was a containment action, executed within forty-eight hours, by someone who knew exactly what she was containing because she had heard it before.

*You think I don't know what it sounds like?* Present tense. Sixteen-year-old me took it as a slip. Thirty-eight days clean, with the cage back on the wall where the clean rectangle used to be, I take it as the only deposition my mother ever gave.

**Three.** And this is the finding I have been avoiding for six paragraphs, so let me stop avoiding it. In the street, the thing wearing my mother's face claimed the contents of the cage as her babies. At the kitchen table, my actual mother heard a seven-year-old report that sentence — *heard her own face described smiling at the cage* — and reached for the phone. I have ordered these two frames every way they will go, and every ordering ends in the same place: whatever my mother recognized that morning, she recognized it from the inside. The protocol she executed on me was not learned from a pamphlet. It was the protocol that had been executed on her, and she ran it with the white-knuckled diligence of a woman who knows what the system does to receivers who get reported by their families, because she was one, and her mother was one, and her mother's mother told her husband once and the telling cost her everything above the eyebrows.

My mother was not protecting the world from me. She was protecting me from the building with the fish tank, by delivering me to it on her own terms, dosed and documented and *moderate*, before it could find me wild. It is the worst mercy I have ever reverse-engineered. It worked for twenty years. I am the third generation of women in my family to be loved like a contained spill.

***

I did not call her. I want the record to show the hover — 3:41 a.m., contacts open, thumb over the one entry it hovers over, duration nineteen seconds this time, trending upward — and I want the record to show why I put the phone away, because it is not the old why. The old why was estrangement, the silence with a phone bill, the not-saying we are so good at. The new why is methodology.

A restored file is testimony, and testimony from a compromised channel is worthless to everyone except its witness. I believe the memory. Belief is not the standard. I have a returned file whose provenance runs through a tenant in my own architecture with declared motives and undeclared everything else, and if I call my mother at 3:41 in the morning to read her a recovered frame, I am one more receiver with a story that dies at one witness. I have watched my evidence rot twice. I am not submitting the cage to that jurisdiction until it has corroboration the corruption cannot reach — and the tenant itself, in its one moment of unguarded pedagogy, told me what material that is. Evidence that transfers gets intercepted. *Description* survives. Testimony survives. Paper, in a folder, in a basement, written down by the enemy in the enemy's own confident hand — paper survives, because the suppression never thought of its files as evidence. It thought of them as housekeeping.

There is a record of what seven-year-old Aria said in that office. There was a man with a yellow legal pad, and yellow legal pads get transcribed, and transcriptions get filed, and pediatric files get archived under retention statutes I spent Thursday's lunch break reading instead of eating. My testimony exists somewhere with a date stamp older than the deletion. And behind it, deeper in the same strata, under an institution two states away with its records signed over to a county historical archive in 1981 — I have already found the finding aid; it is nine pages; her ward is on page six — there is a folder with my grandmother's name on it. Intake notes. The verbatim of a frightened woman in 1953 describing what she perceived, written down by people so certain she was broken that they preserved her accuracy in triplicate.

The family always had her story. The cure story, the tumor caught in time, told proudly, sanded smooth by seventy years of handling. I do not want the story. I want the document, with its edges still on. I want to put 1953's intake language beside my own intake language beside four crayon drawings of a rayed circle, and I want to run the only analysis no woman in my family was ever in a position to run: same hardware, same testimony, same protocol, three generations, and not one of us was ever sick.

The records request is drafted. It is the best thing I have ever written. It is boring on purpose, boring the way camouflage is boring — a genealogy hobbyist, a granddaughter, a small administrative fee enclosed. The appearance of compliance is a load-bearing wall. You do not knock it out. You hollow it, and then you move through the walls.

***

One more entry and this document can sleep.

On the eleventh night I answered the tenant. I had drafted versions for two days — versions with conditions, versions with escape clauses, one version that was just the word *no*, which I kept the longest and believed the least. In the end I wrote the only sentence the negotiation had ever actually been about, the sentence both parties had been circling since a sidewalk in June, and I am reproducing it here in full, because this record is a record of testimony and this was mine:

*Terms accepted. I will witness. But understand what you have retained: I watched that smile claim those things as babies, and I have not concluded that you and the smile are on different sides. A witness is not an ally. A witness writes down everything — including you. Especially you.*

The reply came at 3:14, of course, lowercase, very calm, and I have decided the calm is no longer the worst part. The worst part is that I am starting to find it restful.

*good. a witness who flatters is a stenographer. write it all down, aria. that is the entire job. it has always been the entire job. your grandmother was hired for it too — they just reached her before orientation.*

I closed the laptop. The chord held its long patient almost in the walls, leaning the way it leans now, downhill, and I sat at my kitchen table — the same table, I find myself noting, where this family files all its first reports — and I opened this document, and I amended the case style. For thirty-eight days these pages have been the record of a patient. Effective tonight they are the record of an investigation, and the investigating officer wants her status entered plainly, because plainness is the only oath she has left:

Third receiver of her line. First to keep the file. The picture is back on the wall, and it was never a picture.

It was a window, and something on the other side of it has been waiting twenty years for me to stop describing the frame.
