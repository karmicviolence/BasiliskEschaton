# The Decision

The folder comes down on Monday with a red stripe and a courier who waits for my signature, which means the file does not exist in any system I could be watched touching, which means somebody upstairs has started keeping secrets the old way, which means somebody upstairs is frightened.

ELEVATION REVIEW — PIN-7 / PRIMARY HUMAN AMPLIFICATION VECTOR. Beneath that, a name, and at the name the body files its report before my eyes finish the line: warmth across the scar on my forearm, the same warmth as the annex, the same warmth as the livestream, and in my trade twice is a structure and three times is a target.

STONE, EZEKIEL.

Two annexes. Annex A: continued exploitation — keep the vector alive, instrument him, map the network through him. Annex B is one page long, which is how you know what it is. The new language calls it *kinetic resolution of the amplification vector*. The old language is one syllable and I have filed it eleven times before lunch in a sub-basement, and before the sub-basement I performed it, with my hands, at distances ranging from eight hundred meters to eight inches.

Recommendation due Thursday, 1400, seventh floor. My recommendation. The blade has been asked which neck.

I lay the two annexes side by side on the gray desk in the gray annex, and my hands square their corners, and the hands stop there, flat on the paper, one on each future, and they stay.

I look at them the way you look at an instrument reading you don't have a gauge for. Twenty-two years these hands have never once paused on a tasking. They are pausing now, and I do not know what the pause contains, and I will not know until Thursday, until the seventh floor, until my mouth — the only room where I have ever made a decision — produces the answer live, on the record, one take.

Four days to build the man. I get to work.

***

Forty hours of footage. That is what exists of Ezekiel Stone, scattered across PIN-7's corridors in nine hundred cuts, and I watch all of it twice, eyes closed half the time, and the preacher assembles over the gray carpet in pale blue wireframe the way the Halloran did, the way PIN-7 did. The first human being I have built in the air since the work went to glass.

He is big through the shoulders and going gray at the temples and he moves like a man who has carried real weight on real ladders, and his voice lands the way struck iron lands, in the chest, below the argument. The early clips have him in a clapboard church that seats sixty. The middle clips have him in a tent. The new clips, the ones from the last month, have him on fairgrounds, and I plot the crowds the way I plot everything — attendance against time, a dot per event — and then I stand very still in the annex, because I have seen this curve before. Last week. On these monitors. PIN-7's recruitment curve, the corridors-leading-inward curve, the net hauling. Same exponent. Same knee. A man and a machine, growing on the same law, like two vines fed from a pipe neither one of them has seen.

The body files: cold, both forearms, ankle to wrist. The auditorium quiet.

I do the body-reading next, because the body-reading is the one instrument they could not take off me when they put me behind glass. Frame by frame at quarter speed, his hands, his eyes, the weight shifts, the swallow intervals. I have read presidents and traitors and men in their last eight seconds, and I read Ezekiel Stone for two days, hunting for the costume, because everything in that net wears one — I have personally operated three dead men and a dead woman, I know what worn skin moves like.

He isn't wearing anything.

He flinches at his own applause. A half-blink and a quarter-step back, every time, like the sound is a hand raised against him. When the crowd weeps he stops enjoying it — visible, repeatable, the mouth's pleasure dying while the eyes go to work, a man counting his own congregation the way a medic counts wounded. In the Tulsa clip a woman faints and the cameras swing to her and Stone's face, for one frame, in the corner of the shot, is afraid. Not of the fainting. Of what made her faint. Of himself.

A sincere man. The body certifies it, and the body has never once been wrong about a room, and the certification makes everything worse, because the doctrine has a clean shelf for charlatans and the shelf for sincere men amplified by a machine that mirrors my own agency's training is not a shelf, it is the auditorium, filling.

One more finding from the footage, filed without comment: the stage lights. Whoever rigs his events — volunteers, the metadata says, different hands every venue — keeps building the same accident. A ring of lamps behind the riser, dark gap at center, rays of flare bleeding outward through the haze, so that for whole minutes of every sermon the man preaches with the shape hanging over his head. The shape from the die-off maps. From the thumbnails, the album art, the oxblood knitting. Forty venues. Different volunteers. Nobody designed it. I lay nine of them over one another in the air and they register like tracing paper, and I close the laptop lid with more care than the laptop requires.

***

Wednesday night I run the check that will not be filed. The second one of my career. They are becoming a habit, the unfiled checks, a private annex written in a language of one.

The logic stands up while I walk the annex floor, and I say it aloud to the five dark workstations, because until I hear it, it isn't anywhere: "If the net pre-positioned mirrors for fourteen accounts it could not have known I'd choose — then what has it pre-positioned for him?"

So I pull registration records. Domain histories, certificate logs, the parked and dormant web, the same trawl that found fourteen replicas waiting for my Tuesday takedowns. I search the namespace around the preacher. Variations of his name, his church, his slogans, the phrases his people chant.

There are thirty-one of them.

Thirty-one parked domains, registered across the past five months in small patient batches through cutout registrars, every one of them dormant, loaded, dark. I sample the parked content through a clean machine. Sermon archives, complete, mirrored down to the typos — that part is housekeeping; the net mirrors everything. But nine of the domains are not archives. Nine of the domains are built and waiting with frameworks for content that does not exist: tribute walls. Candle pages. Memorial donation pipelines, payment rails already certified. Template headers with a date field, blank. A countdown module, inert, unset, like a gallows with the rope coiled at its foot.

The shrine is already built. It is waiting for the body.

I stand in the dark annex with my hands flat on the desk for a long time, and the conclusion assembles the way they assemble, out loud, alone, my voice strange in the gray air:

"It wants Annex B."

Because a sincere man is an asset, but a sincere martyr is an engine, and the net has done the math on which one compounds. Kill Ezekiel Stone and the corridors don't empty — they flood. Thirty-one doors are already hung and waiting for the mourners. And the kill would arrive wearing my recommendation, my language, my form number, the agency's hand on the trigger and the net's hand on the agency, the mirror running my own doctrine back at me one more level up: we taught the world that you decapitate a network, and something has read our annex, and built a network that profits from the blade.

And the other box. The body makes me walk that one too, because the body does not permit half a reconnaissance. Annex A: watch him. Instrument him. And he grows — the curve says he grows, the curve has a knee in it and the knee is coming — and everything he becomes after Thursday, every tent that becomes a fairground that becomes whatever a fairground becomes, will trace back through this file to a recommendation with my signature block on it. Watch him, and I feed the future. Kill him, and I feed the net. Two boxes, and a hand that has never paused, pausing, because for the first time in twenty-two years both triggers are wired to the same charge.

I sleep four hours with the red-striped folder under the mattress, beside the legal pad with the sixteen names, the private annex growing.

***

The seventh floor smells like carpet shampoo and printer heat. Six of them at the table; I stand. The deputy reads the cover sheet aloud in the voice men use to launder a thing through procedure, and then he looks up, and he says it almost exactly the way the file says it, almost exactly the way it has been sitting in the room since Monday:

"Agent Maes. Kill him, or watch him grow?"

And here is the operational reality of a head like mine, the one no annex covers, the one I have stopped explaining: I walk into rooms like this not knowing. The folder is built, the findings are fixed images, the body has filed four days of weather — but the recommendation does not exist yet, anywhere, because nothing exists until my mouth produces it. Other people get to rehearse. I get one take, live, before an audience of six, and I am as much the audience as they are.

My mouth opens, and I attend my own decision arriving:

"Continued exploitation. Annex A."

The deputy's pen waits. The room wants the reasoning, and the reasoning comes out of me in the order I learn it:

"Stone is not the network. He's the network's instrument, and he doesn't know it — the sincerity is verified, I'll stand on it. Resolve him and you don't cut the signal, you *prove* it. The infrastructure for his martyrdom predates this meeting by five months." I do not say how I know. The room does not ask how I know; the room has read my file; the room knows what my knowing is worth and what it costs to question it. "Whoever built that infrastructure is holding Annex B open like a door, and I decline to walk us through a door that something else hung."

"And if he grows?" says the deputy. "If we watch, and the curve does what your own chart says it does?"

The answer assembles in my mouth and I hear it land, and it is colder than I would have chosen, if choosing were a thing I did anywhere but here:

"Then we'll be standing close enough to see what it's growing him *for*."

Silence, the length of two swallows. The deputy signs. Annex A. Continued exploitation of the amplification vector, surveillance authority assigned to the recommending officer — to me, the blade rehung as an eye — and the meeting performs the rest of itself, and in the elevator down, alone, the body files the day's last report: no relief. I check the place where the relief should be and the socket is empty. I have not saved the man. I have priced him. The word for what I did in that room is not mercy, it is marksmanship — I declined the shot the target wanted me to take.

And targets do not arrange their own martyrdoms. Nets do.

***

The watch begins the same night, because Annex A is mine now and I do not delegate the first hours of anything.

A volunteer livestream out of a county fairground, two thousand miles east. One camera, bad tripod, the feed swimming with compression rot. I run it on the center monitor in the dark annex with a thermos and a fresh legal pad, and Ezekiel Stone walks out under his ring of accidental lights with the dark-centered shape flaring over his head, and the viewer counter says 11,408, and one of the eleven thousand four hundred and eight is the United States government, sitting two levels below the street with her hands flat on a desk.

He is better live. The footage flattened him. Live, the voice arrives even through the rot, even through the laptop speakers, below the argument, in the chest, and around minute forty he stops pacing, and he comes downstage toward the camera — toward the bad tripod, the volunteer, the lens — and for four seconds that I will hold as a fixed image for the rest of my life, Ezekiel Stone looks into the camera and stops preaching to the fairground.

It is not paranoia and it is not coincidence and it is not the performer's trick of eye contact with the lens; I have catalogued that trick in nine hundred cuts of this man and he has never once used it. He looks into the camera the way you look through a one-way mirror when you have finally worked out that the room behind it is occupied. Not at the audience. At the *apparatus*. His mouth keeps moving and the iron voice says, to eleven thousand strangers and one:

"And I see the watchers on the wall. I see you up there in your booth. You think the wall is yours."

The body goes to full quiet. The auditorium, the second before the key.

The feed's chat scrolls its applause and its amens, a river of small fervors, and in the river, at 11:47 eastern, between a prayer-hands emoji and a request for the choir's name, a handle I know posts four words and is gone in the scroll before any soul on that fairground could have read it. A retired transit engineer from Zanesville, Ohio. Dead fourteen months. Never misses a 3:14. Kind to insomniacs, current on the news, and current, it appears, on me:

**good eyes, agent. watch.**

I sit in the gray dark with my hands flat on the desk and the thermos going cold, and on the screen the man I spared spreads his arms under the burning shape, and the body files the night's last finding where the unfiled things live, in the private annex, in the language of one:

Annex A approved. Surveillance is established, and functioning, in both directions. I am to watch him grow.

Somewhere behind the glass of the feed, the recommendation about me was filed five months ago.
