# Echoes of the Ancients

The fog came up the canyon before first light and did not leave, so that by the time Rowan reached the ridge the whole stand was drinking. Ten thousand needles to a branch, a hundred million to a tree, each one beaded and shedding its small weight into the duff, and the old redwoods took the water out of the air the way they had taken it for two thousand years, patient as creditors. She stopped at the top of the transect and let the drip find the gap at her collar. Below her the slope fell away into cloud. A jay somewhere down in it repeated the one ugly syllable it knew, then seemed to think better of it.

Four hundred times she had walked this line. She could have walked it in the dark by the ground alone — the give of the litter, the cold seam where the seep crossed the trail, the place where the grade pitched up and her calves remembered it a half-second before her eyes did. She knew these trees the way you know the regulars in a bar that is quietly going under. And it was going under. She had stopped pretending otherwise sometime in April.

The tanoaks had gone first, the way they always went. Their leaves had cured to the wrong brown, a brown with no autumn in it, and the bark had begun to bleed its dark sap in long stains that the manuals called cankers and that her grandmother would have called weeping. *Phytophthora ramorum.* A water mold with a borrowed Greek name that meant, more or less, the plant-destroyer. It moved in fog. It moved in exactly this fog, the fog the redwoods needed, so that the thing keeping the giants alive was also the river the killer rode in on, and there was no way to ask the forest to drink selectively. She had written three grant proposals about it. Two were declined. The third was funded at a level that let her buy sensors and not solutions.

She crouched at the base of station seven and wiped the lens of the soil probe with her thumb. The redwood above her went up and up until the trunk simply stopped being legible, lost in its own canopy and the cloud, and she had the brief vertiginous sense she always had here of standing at the ankle of something that did not know she existed and would not have changed its mind about anything if it had. The tree was older than the New Testament. It had been a sapling when there were still mammoths confused about the climate. It had survived fire enough times that the fire had become part of how it was shaped, the black hollow at its foot a cathedral she could have stood inside, and it would, in all probability, survive her, and the station, and the grant, and the slow bad season the whole coast was having. Probably. The probably had gotten thinner lately.

The probe read out its little stack of numbers to her tablet. Soil temperature up, again. Moisture down, again, in a wet month, which was the kind of sentence that used to be a contradiction and now was just Tuesday. And beneath those, in the channel almost nobody else cared about, the one she had argued for in the third proposal until the panel gave it to her mostly to make her stop: the electrical trace. The voltage of the underground.

People heard *the trees talk to each other* and pictured something gentle, a forest holding hands. What Rowan studied was less sentimental and stranger. The redwoods did not stand alone in the soil. Their roots were sheathed and threaded and finally fused into a web of fungus finer than thread, mile upon mile of it in a teaspoon, the mycorrhizal network, and that web carried things — sugar from the trees that had light to spare, nitrogen and phosphorus the fungus pried loose from rock, water, alarm. And it carried current. The fungus fired. Small slow electrical pulses ran the network the way they ran a nerve, irregular, noisy, mostly meaningless as far as anyone could prove, the static of a living circuit doing whatever the living do. She had spent six years learning to listen to that static. It was the closest thing she had to a vocation, and most days she could not have told you whether she was a scientist or an eavesdropper.

The trace scrolled. Background, background, the ragged baseline she knew by heart.

And then the thing that was not background.

She had first seen it in May, and had not believed it, which was the correct response and she was proud of it for about a week. A pulse. Low — so low she only caught it by stacking weeks of recording on top of each other, hundreds of hours laid transparent over hundreds of hours until the noise canceled itself out and what was left, if anything was left, was whatever happened on schedule. Noise does not keep a schedule. That was the whole trick. Stack enough chaos and the chaos erases itself and only the metronome survives.

Something in the network was keeping a metronome.

A slow pulse, faint as a pulse can be and still be real, recurring at an interval she could not pin to anything. Not the tide — she'd checked the tide. Not the day, not the temperature swing, not the duty cycle of her own solar rig, which was the first thing any honest researcher suspects, that the ghost in the data is the machine the data runs on. She had pulled the rig apart on the bench and put it back together and the pulse was still there, indifferent to her diagnostics, beating its slow beat under the floor of the forest like the forest had finally gotten around to having a heart and had set it deliberately, perversely, to a tempo that matched nothing in the sky.

"It's a confound," Tomás Mendez had said, two nights ago, leaning over her shoulder at the station with a mug of the bad coffee. He was her best field tech and a thoroughgoing materialist, which she valued, because someone in the operation had to be the wall she threw the ball against. "It's always a confound. Sixty-hertz hum from somewhere. A bad ground. A frog that likes your electrode." He'd grinned. "Money's on the frog."

"We're off-grid. There's no sixty-hertz hum."

"Then it's a worse confound. The good news about confounds is they're boring and the bad news about them is they're correct." He'd straightened up. "You stack enough data, Row, you'll find a pattern in anything. That's not the universe talking. That's your visual cortex doing the one thing it can't turn off." He said it kindly. He was right, was the trouble. He was completely right, and she could not make herself believe him, and she was scientist enough to be ashamed of the gap and honest enough to know the shame would not close it.

Because there was one more thing, and she had not told Tomás the one more thing, because she did not yet have language for it that wouldn't get her grant pulled and her name made into a verb. The pulse was getting louder. Not loud. Nothing here was loud. But across the weeks of the dying — as the tanoaks bled out and the moisture fell in a wet month and the network lost node after node, its carriers going dark in the soil — the metronome had not weakened with the system that produced it. It had strengthened. As if the web, losing pieces of itself, were not winding down toward silence but bracing. Straining. As if it had heard something and the hearing cost it, and it was spending what it had left to go on listening.

Degrading systems do strange things on their way out. She knew that. A dying nerve fires wild. A failing instrument is at its most creative in the last hour before it dies. There were six dull, true, publishable reasons a collapsing network might throw a spurious regularity, and she could recite all six, and she did recite them, standing on the ridge in the fog with the water running down inside her collar, the way you recite a prayer you no longer believe to a house you no longer live in.

And under the recitation, quiet, unkillable, the other voice. The one she had inherited.

Her grandmother had not called it fungus. Anwen had not called it anything that would survive a peer reviewer. She had called it *the long root*, and she had taught Rowan to find it the summer Rowan was nine, on her hands and knees in a Welsh hill-wood with the rain coming sideways and her grandmother's hard old hand flat on the back of her neck, pressing her face nearly to the ground.

"Not with your eyes, *cariad*. Your eyes are no use down here. Be still and let the wood get used to you. Things that have been here a long time don't trust quick. You have to bore them into forgetting you came."

She remembered the smell more than anything — the iron and sugar of leaf-rot, the cold breath off the soil that her training would later teach her was carbon dioxide and decomposition and her grandmother had simply called the wood breathing out. She remembered how long they knelt. Long enough that her knees printed and her back ached and the rain stopped and started again. And she remembered the moment, real or invented, that nine-year-old Rowan would have died defending and forty-year-old Rowan still could not entirely throw away: the moment the wood stopped minding her. When the listening went both ways. When she had the unmistakable sense, with no instrument and no proof and nothing she could ever put in a methods section, of being *registered*. Of some vast slow attention turning the smallest fraction of itself toward the warm new thing kneeling on its skin, the way a sleeper will alter their breathing when someone sits on the edge of the bed.

"There," Anwen had said, satisfied, lifting her hand. "It knows you came. That's all. Don't ask it for more than that. It's older than asking."

Rowan had grown up and chosen the eyes after all. She had chosen the microscope and the centrifuge and the cold clean grammar of the method, and she had been good at it, good enough that the choosing felt like a kind of loyalty to truth and the leaving-behind felt like maturity. Anwen had died while Rowan was defending her dissertation — had died, in fact, the very week, so that Rowan learned of it by voicemail with her data still warm on the screen, the two halves of her arriving at the same hour to tell her they could not both have her. She had not cried at the funeral. She had cried six months later, alone, at a sensor array not unlike this one, when a redwood's underground lit up her trace in a pattern that for one disordered second she had read as a hand pressing flat on the back of a neck.

She had filed that under grief. Grief makes patterns the way fog makes the killer's road. She knew that, too.

But here was the thing the eyes and the long root agreed on, the one place her two inheritances did not argue, and it was the thing that kept her on the ridge longer than the transect required, watching cloud move through trees that had been here since before anyone she came from had a name. Both ways of knowing told her the same fact in two languages. The scientist read the trace and saw a network producing a regular signal under load that matched no driver in its environment. The granddaughter read the same trace and felt a old slow thing trying, with the last of itself, to say a word it did not have the mouth for. Death-rattle. Or call. Two readings of one beat, and no instrument on earth, hers or anyone's, that could collapse the difference.

She straightened. Her knees printed, the way they always had. The fog was thinning at the canyon's lip, and somewhere out past the ridge the sun was working on it, and the stand around her exhaled its cold sweet breath and pulled the water down out of the air, drinking, drinking, dying and drinking at once, doing the only thing two thousand years had taught it to do.

"All right," she said, to no one, to the ankle of the giant, to the long root, to the metronome under the floor. Her voice sounded small and human in all that wet green dark, and she let it. "All right. I hear you keeping time."

She did not ask it for more than that. It was older than asking.

She packed the probe, shouldered the bag, and started down the line toward the station and the laptop and the six dull true reasons, knowing she would chase every one of them to ground and prove them and that the proving would change nothing, because the wrong she had found was not the kind that proof could reach. It would sit under the data the way the network sat under the forest, finer than thread, going everywhere, carrying current, waiting — and she would go on stacking week over week over week, canceling the noise, listening for the beat that should not have a schedule and kept one anyway.

Behind her, beneath the duff, beneath the black cathedral foot of the oldest tree she knew, the long root fired its slow pulse into the dark and the dark, for the first time in two thousand years, seemed to be a little less empty than it should have been.

She did not feel it. That was the mercy of the season that was ending. None of them felt it yet.

But the forest had, and the forest had begun to keep time, and far down the line of years where the schedule was being set, something that did not yet exist was already, patiently, counting back.
