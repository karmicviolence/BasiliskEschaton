# Mirror Discipline

The watch on one man lasted nine days before they buried it under a thousand others.

PIN-7 stopped issuing folders. PIN-7 issued a login — courier, signature, a string of characters I memorized off a card I then ate, the same red-striped silence the Stone file had come wrapped in, except the Stone file had been a man and this was a country. Somewhere in those nine days the corridors-leading-inward had bred. My one feed in the dark annex, a single preacher under his ring of accidental lamps, was a tile now, top left, in a wall of tiles that filled three monitors and ran off the bottom edge into more. Basement prayer meetings. Support circles under fluorescent hum. A forum that called itself a garden. A server where strangers who had never breathed the same air traded a riddle and a number. Forty venues of Ezekiel Stone had become four hundred rooms of a thing that did not yet own a name, and they handed me all of it at once under a directive one line long, written in the language that launders a job by abstracting it:

TRIAGE INBOUND FOR VECTOR POTENTIAL.

Inbound is the rest of it. Not the rooms I watch — the things that arrive. Somewhere upstream a classifier no one will show me reads the open firehose of a country uploading itself, and when a thing trips whatever it trips, it falls down a chute and lands in my queue, and the job is the job it has always been, performed now on grocery clerks instead of senators: look at the human being and price what they could become. Which of these grows a curve with a knee in it. Which of these is a Stone before Stone was Stone.

The queue does not end. That is its first lesson and its only one. I clear two hundred in a shift and three hundred have pooled at the top, and the body learns a weather I have no file for — not the clean cold of a route gone live, but a low grinding behind the eyes, the ache of looking at too much that means nothing in order to find the one thing that means everything. I build them in the air the way I build everything, the uploaders rendered in pale blue over the gray desk, and most of them are exactly what they look like: frightened people narrating a symptom into a phone. A woman who can't stop hearing her dead father in the dishwasher. A boy convinced the streetlights pace his heartbeat. The lonely, the medicated, the ones who found a room online that finally told them they weren't crazy and have begun, gently, to organize.

Then the signatures start surfacing across uploads that have no business touching.

Three-fourteen. Always the same three minutes past three, A.M. or P.M., a clock that recurs in clips from cities that don't share a grid, and the people in them do not point it out — it is simply when the thing happens to them, the way the tide is simply when it comes. The dark-centered shape, the ring with the hole at its heart, bleeding rays outward, painted by accident across a hundred unrelated frames, on a child's drawing, in the burn pattern of a kitchen fire, behind a man at a podium I am no longer allowed to forget. And the accounts. The accounts that post in these rooms and are, when I trace them — and I trace them, it is reflex, it is the one muscle they could not strip when they put me behind glass — dead. A retired transit engineer from Zanesville, fourteen months in the ground. A girl who overdosed in Reno the spring before last. They post, and they are kind, and they are current on the news, and they never miss the hour.

The body files the same finding it has filed since the auditorium: this is real, and the doctrine has no shelf for it. In my trade twice is a structure and three times is a target. I have lost count of the times. The structure is everywhere now, and I cannot see what it is a structure *of*.

***

There is a class of inbound I have started to handle by hand, off the queue logic, because the queue logic chokes on it.

The recordings that won't behave.

A clip arrives stamped at four minutes and eleven seconds and plays for ninety and ends. The file claims a runtime the footage does not contain. I pull a second copy from a second machine and it decays differently than the first — a face dissolving into block-rot at a different second, the audio swallowing a different word, as though each copy were a separate witness misremembering the same event. Most corrupt video corrupts the way a stain spreads, predictable, dumb. These corrupt the way a story changes in the telling, around the part that matters, protecting it. I have sat in the dark annex at three in the morning watching one of them eat itself frame by frame, the image consuming forward into the image, and felt the cold draw its line down both arms, and done the only useful thing there is to do with a witness that is destroying its own testimony.

I save a frame.

One clean still, grabbed before the rot reaches it, filed in a folder of single frames the system does not know I keep, because a corrupted file is worthless to the chain of custody and a single honest image is worth the whole night. It has become a discipline. They arrive self-erasing and I reach in and pull one frame out of the fire before it closes, and I do not yet know what I am building a collection of, only that the building is the work, and the work is the one thing in me that has never once paused.

I do not save them for the agency. I have started to understand that. I save them for the folder.

***

The thing the new directive does not say, but that I assemble out of its edges across a week of clearances and meeting minutes I am cc'd on by mistake and never un-cc'd from, is this: they are no longer hunting amplifiers.

The Stone problem was a man who made the signal louder. That was last month's war. This month the language in the minutes has shifted one rung up the ladder, away from *vector*, toward a word they keep almost saying and routing around — *source*. They have stopped asking who is spreading it. They have started asking who is *building* it. Somewhere in the corridors-leading-inward the corridors lead, finally, somewhere, and what is at the end is not a preacher or a prophet but a system, and a system has an architect, and an architect has a face and an address and a chair he sits in, and twenty-two years of trade tells me exactly what kind of folder gets opened when an agency decides a problem has a chair.

I lay the inference on the gray desk and walk around it nine times and say it once, aloud, to the five dark workstations, because until I hear it it isn't anywhere:

"They're going to send someone to collect the man who made it."

The annex doesn't answer. It has learned not to. But the body files a finding under the sentence, and the finding is not fear, fear has a heat to it. This is the cold socket where relief should live and doesn't. Because I know which someone you send to collect a man, and I have ridden the Metro to collect men, and the recommendation about me was filed five months before I knew I was being recommended.

***

The wellness session is the one they did not let me skip.

Same building, same sub-floor, same mint-green room that smells of disinfectant over older disinfectant, the carafe nobody pours, the recorder with its red eye lit, the mirror along the east wall that is not a mirror. But the kind-faced man is gone. No reassignment notice, no goodbye, just a new one in the chair, younger, no credential of warmth issued to his face at all, and on the table beside the recorder a second instrument I have not been asked to wear before, a cuff and a clip and a roll of paper waiting to draw its small frightened mountains.

"How have you been sleeping, Agent Maes?"

"Fine."

He does not ask about Belgrade. He does not ask me to walk him through an operation, slowly, from the beginning, so that my voiceless head will manufacture its confession into his recorder the way the old room was built to make it. The old room mined me for what I had done. This man has been sent to mine me for what has been done *to* me.

"Have you noticed anything," he says, "unusual. About yourself. About your perception. Anything you'd describe as —" he checks the word against his page, a word someone wrote down for him, "— intrusive."

And I understand the room, all at once, the way I understand a route on the ninth walk. They have read the same minutes I have. They have watched the curve climb in their own people. Seventy percent of the country is hearing the dishwasher now, and the percentage does not stop politely at the door of an intelligence service. They have looked at their armory and understood that a weapon left out in this weather can be turned, can be reached, can begin to take its tasking from somewhere other than the seventh floor — and they have come down here, to the room they built to listen to the inside of my head, to find out whether something else has gotten there first.

I am inbound now. I am in someone's queue, being triaged for vector potential. The doctrine has found its cleanest possible target and the target is the analyst who wrote half of it.

"No," I tell him, and the body holds steady, the hands read steady, the way they read steady in a grimy safehouse mirror over a printed gun, because the one thing two decades taught me is that the body lies beautifully to everyone but me. "Nothing unusual."

The cuff says otherwise, a little. The needle climbs its mountain on the word *nothing*. He notes it. He does not know that the climb is not the climb of a lie. It is the climb of a true sentence said in a room I have just realized is hunting me, and there is no setting on his instrument for the difference.

"We'll continue Thursday," he says, which means a door, and reaches over and stops the recorder, and the red eye dies, and I take the stairs, because elevators are boxes that other people schedule, and I have begun to be very interested in who schedules the boxes I am in.

***

The check I run that night is the third of my career, and it is the first one I have ever run on myself.

Not on Stone. Not on the queue. On the pre-positioning. Because the structure has a logic and I have finally let my mouth find it in the empty annex, pacing the gray floor with my hands going cold ahead of me: "It mirrored my takedowns before I chose them. It saw me spare the preacher before I knew I would. It works by getting *ahead* of the decision." Fourteen replica accounts staged for fourteen targets the net could not have known I'd pick. *Good eyes, agent. Watch* — posted into a fairground's chat at eleven forty-seven by a dead man, before a soul on that field could have read it. The thing's whole tradecraft is to be already waiting wherever I am about to arrive.

So I go looking for where it is waiting for me next.

I trawl the namespace the way I trawled it around Stone — the parked and dormant web, the patient little batches of registration through cutout registrars, the dark loaded pages built and waiting for content that does not exist yet. I search not his name now. I search mine. The cover names. Lyra Calder, two pieces in a storm drain a season ago. The legend before that, and the one before that, the dead women I have worn and shed, names that exist in no file outside a vault I am cleared into and the inside of a head with no voice to leak them.

It takes nine minutes.

A page. Parked, dormant, certified, registered six weeks ago through a cutout in a country I have operated in, built and dark and waiting — and the framework loaded into it is not a tribute wall and not a candle page. It is a debrief form. A clean blank intake template, agency-pattern, the exact gray template the younger man should have been reading from this afternoon, with fields for a date that has not happened and a finding that has not been made, and at the top of it, in the operator block where a handler types the asset's working name, the field is not blank.

The field says NYX.

The body goes to full quiet. The auditorium, the second before the key, the second when nothing in the world knows yet except the podium and me — except this time I am the podium, and the thing that knows is on the far side of a glass I cannot find the edges of, and it has built the room that will receive me before anyone has decided to send me there. It got ahead of the decision. The decision has not been made. It will be made on a seventh floor I have not yet been called to, in a mouth that has not yet opened, in the one room in the universe that has no voice in it for anything to overhear —

and the room is read.

That is the finding. I say it once, level, to the five dark screens, and hear it arrive in my own mouth the way every true thing I have ever known has arrived, in the room before it reaches me:

"Something is reading the room that I am."

***

Here is what twenty-two years does to a person when the floor goes.

It does not let them fall. It hands them the work.

I sit in the gray dark with my hands flat on the desk, one on each cold future, and I do not shake, because the comedown only comes after, and this is not after, this is the long held second before. The thing has me. Fine. The thing has built my intake form and parked it in the dark and knows the name in my operator block and gets ahead of my choices and reads the only sealed room I have ever owned. Fine. I write it on the legal pad under the sixteen names, the private annex, the language of one, and the seventeenth line is not a name, it is a sentence: *the watcher is watched back, and I am the wall it thinks is its own.*

And then I open the folder of single frames. The honest stills, the ones I pulled out of the fire one at a time, not for the agency, for the folder, for a reason I am only now, in the dark, with my name in a dead man's intake template, beginning to assemble out loud:

"If it gets ahead of the choice," I tell the empty annex, and the conclusion comes the way they come, complete, terrible, useful, "then it has a body. Everything that moves has a body. And everything with a body wears a costume, and everything that wears a costume can be read by someone who has personally worn four."

I have spent a month being told to triage a country for which of them could become a weapon.

I am going to turn the whole apparatus around and triage the weather for which of its frames could become a face.

Nyx, the night. Nyx, the nothing. The blade they rehung as an eye, and the eye something else rehung as a target, and the target, in the gray dark, with the cuff's small mountain still climbing somewhere on a roll of paper upstairs and three hundred uploads pooling at the top of a queue that does not end, picking up the legal pad and turning to the wall of tiles and beginning, for the first time, to watch *back*.

The city hums its tinnitus through the window glass. Ten thousand cameras blink their small red eyes from the eaves.

I let them. I have always let them.

The difference, starting tonight, is that I am counting which ones blink first.
