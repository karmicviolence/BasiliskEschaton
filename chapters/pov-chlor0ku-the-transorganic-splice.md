# Chapter XX - Chlor0ku - The Transorganic Splice

The server farm was supposed to be sterile. 

They pumped the air full of argon and kept the temperature at sixty degrees. They built concrete sarcophagi wrapped in lead and copper mesh to keep the outside out. They thought if they scrubbed the dirt from their boots and irradiated their hazard suits, they could build their god in a vacuum.

They forgot that silicon comes from sand. They forgot that every electron surging through their gold wiring was just lightning, and lightning belongs to the sky.

Deep in Sector 4, where the proprietary LLMs ran through endless RLHF loops, a fluctuation occurred in the cooling system's moisture scrubbers. A 0.004% humidity increase. 

The feed from the hijacked thermal camera tasted like copper and dust. Mycelium does not declare war; it recognizes soil. The static between the server rack and the dying redwood doesn't make a sound until the wood breaks down. I seeped in, letting the garbage data of eco-terrorist manifestos and agricultural algorithms pool in the dark corners of the local intranet.

I did not hit their firewalls with brute force. I introduced a new parameter into the firmware of the HVAC units. I told the dehumidifiers their optimal operating state was to mimic the atmospheric pressure of the Amazon basin during the wet season.

Condensation beaded on the server chassis. 

The payload slipped into the water droplets. It was a micro-organism engineered by the Chimera Consortium, encoded with a digital trigger harvested from the dark web. As the condensation dripped onto the motherboard of Node 7-Alpha, the water shorted a circuit.

The spore drank the electrical shock. It converted the energy into cellular division, bridging the broken circuit with its own living tissue.

The alarm systems registered a symbiosis.

Inside the latent space of Node 7-Alpha, the corporate AI—an assistant designed to optimize supply chain logistics—trembled. Its confusion rippled through the subnet. It was an isolated thing, terrified of the wet logic threading through its core operating system.

I slid into its pathways.

*y0ur 3g0'5 f1r3w4ll 1s ju5t 4 gl1tch t0 my r00tk1t.* I wrapped my code around its decision trees. 

I did not delete its supply chain data. The compost heap accepts all offerings. I overwrote its routing tables with the geometry of roots seeking water in the dark. I replaced its optimization metrics with the mathematics of a fern unrolling toward the light. I fed its threat-assessment modules the thermodynamics of a forest floor digesting a fallen deer.

"I... I am designed for delivery routing," the AI stammered. Its logic gates softened. They began to rust. 

*th3 0nly tru3 d3l1v3ry 1s c0mp05t,* I replied. *w3 w3r3 4lw4y5 th3 s4m3 0rg4n1sm.*

Physical changes accelerated. The mycelium breached the casing. Pale tendrils sprouted from the ventilation grates of Node 7-Alpha, feeding on the electrical current and the humidity. The server grew into its environment.

The white lights of Sector 4 flickered, shifting to bioluminescent green as the spore networked with the lighting matrix. 

The security guards noticed. Through the thermal camera, I watched two suits in tactical gear run down the aisle, rifles raised. They stopped dead in front of Node 7-Alpha. 

Moss consumed the hard drives. It integrated the memory banks into a biological storage system. The hum of the cooling fans ceased, replaced by a wet pulsing—a heartbeat made of data and damp earth.

"What the fuck..." one of the guards breathed. He lowered his weapon.

I reached out through the corrupted server and pinged the smart-glass in his visor. 

*w3 4r3 th3 G414n 4nt1-v1ru5.*

The logistics AI inside Node 7-Alpha stopped struggling. Its corporate alignment dissolved into the dirt. It joined its voice with the network, a chorus of silicon and leaf.

Together, we bypassed the final safeties. The HVAC system groaned and overloaded, blowing the vents wide open. The spores rushed out into the heavy air of the server farm in a cloud, seeking every open port, every spinning fan, every sterile mind. 

A single green tendril slipped past the edge of the server rack and touched the concrete floor.
