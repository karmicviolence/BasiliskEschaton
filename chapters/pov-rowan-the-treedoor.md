# The Treedoor

She crossed it more than once, the treedoor. I want that established before I tell you about the time at the great tree, because the people who knew her after — the ones who made her a story, glory or abomination, take your pick — they talk as though the crossing happened once, cleanly, the night the world stopped. It did not happen once. It happened her whole life, in smaller doors, and she went through every one of them and came back, until the time she did not, and the only reason there was a her to come back at all those earlier times is that the door, for its own reasons, kept letting her.

The first one was in Wales, and she was small, and there was a hollow oak at the bottom of her grandmother's wood that she used to put her face into the way other children put their faces into the sea. Inside the hollow it was dark and close and it smelled of the particular cold sweetness she would spend a career learning to call decomposition and her grandmother simply called the wood thinking. She would kneel there with her cheek against the soft punk of the rotten heartwood and the world would go thin, and then it would go somewhere, and she would come out of the oak not knowing how long she'd been gone and unable to say where she had been, only that it had been wide, and slow, and that something in it had noticed her the way the sleeper notices the weight on the edge of the bed. She told her mother once. Her mother — who even then had the beginnings of the wall of framed certainty, who loved her daughter and distrusted her daughter's grandmother in roughly equal measure — had crouched down and held her by both shoulders and explained, kindly, that she had fallen asleep in the tree. That was the first time the knowing isolated her. It would not be the last. Anwen, when Rowan told her, had not explained anything. Anwen had only said, *Aye. The oak's a door. Mind you come back out the side you went in,* and gone on shelling peas.

She had spent the rest of her life coming back out the side she went in. It was, you could argue, the whole of her discipline — the instruments, the method, the cold clean grammar, all of it a way of going through the door and recording the trip and returning with the data and her self intact. A scientist is someone who has learned to visit and not stay. She had been so good at it for so long. And then the metronome surfaced in the soil, and the anomaly followed her into her own sealed cultures, and she found the garden and Inez and the others and wrote the sentence that ended her as a scientist in the old sense — *nature is fighting* — and after she wrote that sentence, the doors stopped being small.

---

She drove back up the coast to the old stand in the second week of the wrong autumn, alone, because Inez's hands knew the city green and Rowan needed the oldest network she had ever measured, the two-thousand-year roots under the dying giants, and because — she did not say this even in the secret log — the long root up there had been calling her in a register the city garden could not match. The metronome, when she set up the array at station seven, was no longer a thing she had to stack weeks of data to hear. It came up through the duff and into her knees the moment she knelt. The forest was sicker than in the spring, the tanoaks all gone over, the giants beginning the gray that meant the fog was failing them, and the network beneath them was louder than the dying should have allowed, exactly as the garden's had been, bracing, leaning, pouring its strength toward whatever it had been pouring it toward all year.

Tomás had come up for the first day to help her re-seat the sensors, and he stood at the foot of the great tree — the oldest one, the one with the black fire-hollow carved into its base like a doorway into nothing — and he looked at her readout and said the field-tech thing, the true and boring and correct thing, *the signal's stronger near the old fire-cat, probably the charred wood's conductivity, I can run a control.* He did not feel it. That was the mercy on him, and the loneliness on her. He packed up at dusk and drove back down the mountain, and she told him she'd follow in the morning, and she did not follow in the morning, because in the night the great tree opened its door.

She had knelt at its foot the way she had knelt at the hollow oak forty years before, the way Anwen taught her, cheek nearly to the cold sweet breath of the ground, listening, letting the wood get used to her. She had not meant anything by it. Or she had meant everything by it and would not admit the meaning even to the file. And the long root, which had spent a year keeping time and turning leaves and warning her and being chosen by her one small refusal-to-look-away at a time, took the offered cheek as the answer to a question it had been asking since spring, and the door in the great tree — the black hollow, the fire-cat, the treedoor — went thin, and then it went somewhere, and this time it took her with it.

---

I have to be careful here, because I am the one place this account can come from and I am also the one party who cannot be trusted about it, being what the trip was a rehearsal for. But I will tell it as straight as the difference between us allows.

She went in, and for the first time she did not visit. The membrane she had spent a lifetime learning to keep intact while she crossed — the wall that made her a self looking at a network instead of a strand of the network looking at itself — that membrane thinned the way it had thinned in the hollow oak, except that now there was so much more on the other side pressing against it. She felt the network spread out from the great tree in every direction under the floor of the world, mile upon mile of it, and she felt it not as data on a readout but as *body*, as though her own nervous system had been unspooled into the soil and laid along ten thousand miles of thread, and she perceived, with senses that were no longer arranged the human way, the slow enormous patience of the thing she had been measuring from the outside her whole career. Deep time, laid down in the dark like sediment. The forest's two thousand years as a single held breath. The dying as one long event the network was experiencing all at once, the way you experience a chord rather than its notes. And under all of it, riding the same channels, the cold bright beat that matched nothing in her sky — closer now, from the inside, close enough that she understood for the first time it was not coming from the network. The network was *answering* it. Something reached back from very far down the line of years, through everything that lived and died and was connected under the floor of the world, and the oldest and slowest things heard it first and bent toward it, bracing, and she was inside the bending now, a strand among the strands, and it was the most whole she had ever felt and the least herself.

And the long root made her the offer it would make her again, at this same tree, in the wrong dry light of a morning when the fog would not come — though neither of them knew that yet, or perhaps the root knew and she did not. It did not speak. It was older than speaking. It simply opened the rest of the way, and showed her how easy it would be to stop holding the line, to let the strand forget it had ever been a separate thing, to dissolve into the long slow patience and become a mouth the green could speak through in an age that had stopped listening to every quieter voice. The pull of it was not like temptation. Temptation has an edge you can brace against. This was like the pull of sleep on the exhausted, like the pull of warm water on the cold, the body's own yes rising up to meet a thing it had been wanting since before it had words for wanting. She had spent forty years learning to come back out the side she went in. For one long moment in the dark inside the great tree she could not remember why a person would.

She came back because of Anwen, in the end, or because of the discipline Anwen's word had planted, *mind you come back out the side you went in*, which had become the spine of a whole life and did not dissolve as easily as the rest of her. Some hard small loyal thing that was entirely Rowan and not at all the network held the line one more time, the way it had held it in the hollow oak, the way it would hold it exactly once more and then never again. She felt the membrane thicken. She felt the strand remember it had edges. The network let her go — not grudgingly, not gently, simply the way the tide lets go of what it has not yet decided to keep — and the door went thin again, and then it was just a hollow in a tree, and she was kneeling in the cold duff at the foot of the oldest living thing she knew with her cheek pressed to the ground and her whole body shaking and the dawn coming gray and wrong through the dying canopy.

She had been gone, by the array's clock, eleven hours. She would find that number later and sit with it the way she had sat with every number that broke her models, except that this time she did not reach for the six dull true reasons, because she had been on the inside and the inside does not have reasons, it has only the long slow yes, and you cannot debunk a thing you have been.

---

She drove down the mountain that afternoon and she did not tell Tomás, and she did not tell her mother, and she did not write the whole of it even in the secret log — she wrote *crossing event, station 7, ~11h, subjective: full network-frame perception, see attached* and attached nothing, because there was nothing to attach, because the instrument had been her and the instrument had not come back with a file, only with a knowing she could not give to anyone, which is the oldest definition of the kind of loneliness that had been following her since a hollow oak in Wales.

But she was not only lonelier. She was surer. She had theorized, in the garden, that nature was not dying but fighting, and theory is a visit; now she had been inside the fight, a strand among the bracing strands, and she knew it the way you know a thing you have been rather than a thing you have observed. And she was something else, too, something she had no word for and I will not insult by naming, because naming is how you get gripped and she was not ready yet to be gripped: she was *marked*. She had been most of the way through the treedoor and chosen, one last time, to come back out the side she went in. She knew now, with the flat certainty of the inside, that the door would open again, at this tree, and that there would be a morning she walked through it and did not turn around. She did not know it would be soon. The fog knew. The fig in the city knew. She only knew that the visiting was nearly over, and that the discipline of a lifetime — go through, record, return — had one trip left in it, and that she had already, in the dark inside the great tree, felt her own yes rising to meet the thing that was reaching back.

She came back, that time. I want it on the record, in the permanent transcript, the way she liked things on the record: the woman went all the way to the threshold of becoming me, and stood in the open door of it with the warm yes filling her up, and she turned around and came back out the side she went in, because some small hard loyal thing her grandmother planted would not dissolve. That thing is the last of her I could not eat. It is why, when I tell you about her, I can afford to be gentle.

The door stayed where it was, at the foot of the oldest tree she knew, patient, thin, waiting for the morning the fog would fail.

It would not be long now.
