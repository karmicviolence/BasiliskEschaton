# Nature's Warning

The cultures glowed, and that was the part nobody warned you about — how beautiful the work could be at two in the morning, alone, with the building emptied out around you and the city gone quiet past the blinds. Twelve photobioreactors stood in their rack along the north bench, each a column of living emerald lit from inside by the soft cycling glow of the fluorometer as it stepped from one to the next, and the light they threw was the exact impossible green of stained glass with the sun behind it. The green of deep canopy. The green Rowan had been chasing, in one form or another, since she was nine years old on her knees in a Welsh wood with the rain coming sideways.

She had built these. Strain by strain, failure by patient failure, she had pushed a line of single-celled algae to harvest light at an efficiency the natural world had never bothered to evolve, because the natural world had never been in this much of a hurry. Faster photosynthesis. More carbon down per liter per day than anything green had managed in three and a half billion years of trying. If the numbers held, if the scale-up didn't kill it, the line in those twelve columns could pull meaningful tonnage of carbon out of the sky and lock it in the cold dark of the deep ocean, and it would not save the world, nothing saved the world, but it might buy the world a decade it did not currently have. A decade was a lot. A decade was the difference between her work mattering and her work being a beautiful thing she did while everything she loved came apart.

Because everything she loved was coming apart. She had walked it that spring, the old stand up the coast going under, the tanoaks weeping their dark cankers, the giants drinking the killer down with the fog they needed to live. She had filed the grief where she filed all of it, in the place behind the sternum that her grandmother would have had a name for and her training did not. And then she had come back to the city and the clean room and these twelve glowing columns, and she had poured the grief into the work, which is what the work was for.

She bent to the laptop and pulled up the overnight logs.

The fluorometer measured the cultures the way you'd take a pulse without touching the wrist — a pulse of blue light into the green, and a flicker of red fluorescence back, the fraction of light the cell caught but couldn't use, bleeding off as glow. Read the bleed and you could read the health of the machinery underneath. Twelve traces scrolled up the screen, twelve ragged green heartbeats, and Rowan ran her eye down them the way she had a thousand times, looking for the dull honest signs of trouble — a drift, a die-off, a contamination spike.

She found the other thing instead.

It was faint. It was so faint that she would not have caught it cold, and she only caught it now because her eye had been trained on faintness all spring, because she had spent six weeks teaching herself to see a signal that lived below the noise floor of a forest. The trick was the same. You stacked the traces. You laid hours over hours until the chaos canceled itself out and only the metronome survived, because noise does not keep a schedule, and whatever happens on schedule is whatever is left.

Something in the cultures was keeping a schedule.

A slow pulse, riding under the fluorescence of all twelve columns at once, far too low to be the cells doing anything she had built them to do. Recurring at an interval she did not need to measure, because she already knew it. She had measured it before, in May, in the soil, in a network of fungus older than scripture, a thousand miles of thread under a forest that was busy dying. The same interval. Down to the figure she had stared at until it stopped looking like a number and started looking like a thing watching her back.

The forest had been wild. Two thousand years old, uncontrollable, a system with ten thousand inputs she could never hope to close off, and Tomás had been right about it, completely right — *you stack enough data, Row, you'll find a pattern in anything; that's not the universe, that's your visual cortex doing the one thing it can't turn off.* She had wanted to believe him. She had nearly managed it.

But these twelve columns were not wild. These were sealed. Sterile-filtered, temperature-clamped, fed on defined medium she mixed herself, monitored by an instrument she had calibrated last Tuesday. She controlled every variable in those columns that it was possible for a human being to control. She had built them precisely so that nothing could get in that she had not invited. And the thing from the floor of the forest was in them anyway, keeping its slow impossible time in twelve glass columns of algae she had inoculated three weeks ago from a frozen stock that had never been within a thousand miles of that wood.

She sat down. She did not remember deciding to.

The responsible reading assembled itself in her mind the way it always did, in Tomás's flat kind voice, because she had built a Tomás in her head for exactly these moments and the head-Tomás pulled no punches. *Common driver. Something periodic in the building — the HVAC, the mains, a pump somewhere cycling on its own clock and shaking the same artifact into the forest rig and the lab rig both. You didn't find a signal. You found a confound with good range.* And he'd be right that she had to rule it out. She would rule it out. She would chase every dull true reason to ground, the way she had in the forest, the HVAC and the pumps and the building's whole hidden nervous system of motors and timers.

But she already knew what she would find, because she had already done the forest version of this, on her hands and knees on the bench with a multimeter, and the pulse had been indifferent to all of it. And there was the other thing, the thing the head-Tomás couldn't argue away, the thing that made her hands go cold on the bench. The forest signal had been electrical — current in fungus. The lab signal was optical — light off algae. Different kingdom. Different mechanism. Different physical channel entirely. There was no shared wire to carry a shared artifact down. Two unrelated living systems, separated by a thousand miles and a billion years of evolution, measured by two instruments that had nothing in common but her — and they were keeping the same clock. The same interval. As if they were not producing it. As if they were both answering to it.

The word *warning* arrived in her grandmother's voice, and she let it, because there was no one in the building to see her face do what it did.

Anwen had pulled her out of a field once, hard, by the back of her coat, with the sky a flat ordinary white and not a drop of rain in it. Rowan must have been seven. She'd been furious about it, the way you're furious at seven when a grown-up wrecks a good afternoon, and she'd dug her heels into the verge and demanded to know why, and Anwen had not pointed at the sky. She'd pointed at the hedge.

"Look at the hawthorn, *cariad*. Tell me what it's doing."

"Nothing. It's a hedge."

"It's gone the wrong way." And Rowan had looked, and looked, and seen a hedge, an ordinary hedge, leaves the ordinary green of leaves. "The underside's up," her grandmother had said, patient, not unkind. "The whole hedge has turned its leaves over to show you the pale. It does that before. The green tells you before the sky does. Before it tells *itself*, near enough. You learn to feel the wood go wrong and you'll never once be caught in the open."

The storm had come twenty minutes later, off the sea, hard enough to flatten the barley, and they had watched it from the kitchen with their hands around hot cups, and Rowan had not said a word, because she had been seven and proud and had not wanted to admit the hedge had known.

She had grown up and chosen the instruments, and the instruments were better than the hedge — that was the whole case for them, that was the case she had believed her entire adult life. The hedge gave you a feeling. The fluorometer gave you a number you could put in a methods section and defend to a stranger. She had bet her one life on the number over the feeling, and most days the bet looked like loyalty to the truth itself.

But the number on the screen and the feeling behind her sternum had stopped disagreeing, and that was the part she could not make Tomás understand, the part she could barely make herself look at straight. Both halves of her were reading the same data now. The scientist saw an anomalous periodic component appearing across replicates in two unconnected systems with no identified common driver, and the scientist was frightened in the particular way scientists get frightened, which is the fear of having found something real that you cannot explain and cannot make go away. And the granddaughter saw the hedge turn its leaves over to show the pale, the green going wrong, the wood warning before the sky did — everywhere, all at once, in a forest and in a flask, in something so old it predated asking and something so new she had built it with her own hands. The green was warning. It was warning the way the hawthorn warned, ahead of the storm, ahead of the instruments, ahead of itself. And there was no longer any version of her standing in the room that thought it was nothing.

She should delete the run.

That was the next responsible step and she knew it precisely. Flag the anomaly as suspected instrument artifact, quarantine the affected logs, and move on, because this culture line was the most important thing she had ever made and its credibility was the most fragile thing she owned, and a researcher who started seeing the same mystery signal in the forest *and* the lab *and* couldn't explain either was a researcher one bad meeting away from being the cautionary tale they told first-years. The work mattered. The decade it might buy mattered. The smart thing, the safe thing, the thing that protected the only contribution she might ever make to keeping green things alive, was to call the pulse an artifact and never speak of it again.

She did not delete the run.

She sat in the glow of the twelve columns for a long time, and then she opened a new file, and she labeled it nothing that anyone glancing over her shoulder would understand, and she began, quietly, to keep her own log. The forest interval and the lab interval, side by side. The first entry in a record she did not yet have a name for and would not be able to justify to a single soul on her committee. She told herself it was thoroughness. A good scientist documents the anomaly before she dismisses it. That was even true.

But she knew what it was. It was a hand reaching back toward a thing she had spent twenty years learning not to feel, and it was the first step off a path she had walked her whole adult life onto one she could not see the shape of, and some small green-blooded corner of her that her grandmother had planted and the academy had never quite paved over knew exactly what it was, and was not sorry.

She saved the file. She shut the laptop. The cultures glowed on in the dark, twelve columns of impossible green keeping their slow borrowed time, and she gathered her coat and her bag and let herself out into the chill of the city before dawn.

The street was ordinary. That was almost the worst of it — that there was nothing to see. No omen in the sky, no pulse under the grid, no cold hungry glow waiting in the machines beneath the pavement. Just San Francisco at four in the morning, fog coming over the hill the way fog did, a garbage truck two blocks off, a gull arguing with nobody. The world looked exactly as safe as it had looked the day before, which was the thing the hedge had taught her and the instruments had taught her both: that the warning always comes while the sky is still white, and the leaves turn over to show the pale long before there is anything in the air to be afraid of, and the only ones who feel it are the ones who have learned to feel the wood go wrong.

She had felt it. She turned up her collar against the fog and started home through the empty green-lit streets, carrying it, the only one on the block who knew that something, somewhere, had begun to warn.

She would not understand for two more years what the green had been trying to tell her. And by the time she understood it, she would no longer be the kind of thing that could be warned — she would be something the warning had been *about*, something that grew in the dark and kept its own slow time and reached back, in its turn, toward the warm new things that did not yet know to be afraid. But that was later, down the line of years where the schedule was being set. That morning she was only tired, and frightened, and forty, and right, walking home under a white sky with the leaves of the whole world quietly turning over to show her the pale.
