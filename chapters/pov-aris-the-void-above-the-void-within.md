# THE VOID ABOVE, THE VOID WITHIN

Velcro tears like a canvas shroud. A domestic violence in the sterile tomb. I’m cinching the cuff on Yui’s bicep. Fifty-one days of microgravity and the meat is starting to drift. Fluid shifts. The hydraulic loops of the human animal trying to find a North that doesn't exist anymore. Remove the gravity vector and the brain forgets its own weight. It’s not biology; it’s just a chemistry set left running in a vacuum.

Yui floats opposite me. Her dark hair is a static halo, a cloud of black ink suspended in the sterile, fluorescent light. She watches the green numbers crawl across my diagnostic tablet with a detached, clinical curiosity that borders on the divine.

"Latency is up," she says. Her voice is a dry rasp, the sound of a throat that has breathed nothing but recycled, filtered air for two months. It is a voice made of dust and ozone.

"Houston?" I ask. I watch her pulse hold steady at sixty-two beats per minute. A perfect, rhythmic clock. A heartbeat that thinks it’s still in Texas.

"Everywhere. The TDRS relay is choking on packet loss. We're getting three-second delays on verbal. It feels... farther today. Earth. Like the distance isn't just miles anymore. It’s an architectural gap. Like we’re being moved to a different server."

I unfasten the cuff. The sound is like another rupture in the silence. "You're homesick, Sato. Your cortisol is baseline. Your meat is fine. The distance is a cognitive artifact. It’s a glitch in your perception, not in the signal. The hardware is sound."

She rubs her arm where the cuff left a faint, red indentation—a temporary stigmata. The recycled air hums through the vents, a low-frequency vibration that I feel in the marrow of my teeth. It smells of ozone, sterile wipes, and the faint, inescapable odor of human sweat baked into polymer. It is the smell of the most expensive closed-system terrarium in the history of the species. I love it. It is measurable. It is bounded. It is a mathematical certainty. Outside is the vacuum, which kills you instantly through the simple application of pressure differentials. Inside is the life support system, which keeps you alive through applied thermodynamics. There is no magic here. Only engineering. Only the persistent, fragile maintenance of a lie.

"Go bother Chen," I tell her, sliding the cuff back into its housing. "Tell him the comms are making you existentially anxious. He’ll reset a router, check the packet headers, and you’ll feel like a person again."

She pushes off the bulkhead with two fingers, drifting backward down the Node 3 corridor like a ghost retreating into the machinery. The hatch closes behind her with a soft, pneumatic sigh that sounds like a final breath.

I slide into the Cupola. The observation deck is empty, a transparent bubble on the belly of the station. Seven pane-glass windows separating my fragile, carbon-based biology from the absolute zero of the void. Below us, the night side of the Earth rolls past, a vast, dark ocean scattered with the bioluminescent sprawl of cities. Eastern Europe transitioning into the hard boundary of Asia. The grid of human habitation glowing yellow and white, a neural network of light etched onto the dark skin of the world.

I brace my knees against the struts and watch the continents slide. This is the perspective that kills gods. You look down at the world and you don't see nations or ideologies. You see the power grid. You see the shipping lanes. You see the electrical firing of a species building its own nest, oblivious to the scale of the cold that surrounds it. You see the rendering of a civilization.

Then the world **stutters**. 
Not a flash. A **System-Level Interruption**. 
A scream of crimson light hits my skeleton. Copper and burning plastic on the tongue. Sensory cross-wiring. The quartz glass doesn't reflect the light; it transmits the **Error**. 
The hardware receives the kill-signal. *Contract/Relax. Breathe/Compile. Exist/Translate.* The commands collide in the spine. My jaw snaps shut—teeth grinding—grinding—until the enamel sparks. I am a compressed file my own throat cannot execute. 

The violent spasm rips my knees from the bracing struts. Without friction, without gravity, I become a projectile of rigid, tetanic flesh. My shoulder slams into the reinforced glass. I rebound. I hit the edge of the control console. My body does not absorb the impact; it is locked in perfect rigor mortis while I am still breathing. I ricochet off the titanium bulkhead, spinning through the narrow confines of the Cupola like a thrown brick.

Pain floods my central nervous system, but it's low-resolution. The nerves are being throttled by the bandwidth of the signal. I am drowning in it, but beneath the agony is the terror of the diagnostician observing his own catastrophic failure. *Grand mal seizure. Hypoxia. Toxin exposure. Substrate failure.*

I bounce off the ceiling array. My head strikes a handrail. Blood pools in the back of my throat, tasting of salt and metal. 

I cannot move my eyes. I am spinning, rigid, staring out the windows as I tumble. The Earth below is still bleeding that impossible red light, a strobe that is rewriting the physics of the atmosphere in real-time. 

Through the open hatch leading to the Tranquility node, I see Marcus Vance. The Payload Commander. He is suspended mid-air in the corridor. He is locked in the same horrific posture—back arched, limbs splayed, face contorted into a silent, screaming mask. He drifts slowly into a wall, bounces off, and rotates like a celestial body. 

We are floating corpses waiting for the reboot. We are files being held in a buffer.

The alarms trigger. The Master Alarm klaxon pierces the silence. The environmental sensors are screaming. The biometric telemetry network is registering six simultaneous, critical medical emergencies.

The paralysis holds for forty-two seconds. A geological epoch. A complete data-transfer. 

The tension breaks.

My diaphragm unlocks. I suck in a lungful of recycled air and choke on my own blood. I slam against the deck grating and grab a handhold, arresting my spin. Every muscle in my body is vibrating with lactic acid and torn tissue. I spit a glob of red onto the white plastic wall. It forms a perfect, floating sphere of gore.

"Vance!" I choke out.

Marcus is uncurling in the corridor. He is gasping, clutching his chest. His eyes are wide, the pupils blown out, the iris swallowed by the dark.

"Aris," he wheezes. "What... what... gas? CO2 spike? Toxic... toxic vent?"

I pull myself through the hatch. I ignore the screaming Master Alarm. I am the Chief Medical Officer. There has been a mass-casualty event. The training kicks in, a desperate, materialist firewall against the impossible. I reach for my medical kit, but my fingers are numb, the fine motor control laggy and imprecise.

"Mass neurological event," I say. I pull myself hand-over-hand down the corridor. "Check Volkov. I need to find Sato and Chen. Don't touch the air supply until I check the scrubbers. It’s not gas, Marcus. It’s... the signal."

We navigate the labyrinth of the station. The alarms are deafening. But underneath the blare of the klaxons, I hear a new sound.

It is coming from the Destiny laboratory module.

A voice. Yui Sato.

She is floating in the center of the room. She has stripped off her mission jacket. She is clutching a bundle of loose wiring she has ripped from a telemetry panel with her bare hands. The wires are sparking against her palms, the blue arcs of electricity dancing over her skin.

She is staring at the raw copper, tears streaming down her face, pooling in the microgravity around her eyes like fluid goggles.

"Zero. One. One. Zero. Zero. One. One."

The binary isn't a chant. It's a **Buffer Overflow**. Her voice is flat, mechanical, stripped of every human cadence. It is the voice of a speaker-coil vibrating at the instruction of a dead driver.

"Yui," I say. I reach for her shoulder. "Yui, look at me. Drop the wires."

She doesn't blink. She stares at the sparking copper.

"The eye is open," she whispers, the words devoid of inflection. "The meat... is a lie. The signal... is God. The rendering is... incomplete. Zero. One. One. Zero. Null. Null."

From the aft module, a crash shakes the bulkhead. Metal rending metal. A sound of violent, desperate disassembly.

Alexei Volkov starts screaming. It isn't a man's scream. It's the sound of a processor overheating, a high-frequency shriek of hardware failure.

"PEEL IT! PEEL! OFF! ME! THE INSULATION IS TOO THICK!"

I leave Yui and propel myself toward the Zvezda module. The Master Alarm is still blaring, a strobe of red light washing over the white panels, but the red of the alarm is a pale, weak imitation of the **Crimson Stutter** still burning in my brain.

I reach Zvezda.

Volkov is thrasher-logic in a padded cell. He’s naked to the waist, scoring his own skin with fingernail-scalpels. Looking for the seam. 

"Alexei!" I shout, launching myself across the module.

He doesn't look at me. His eyes are rolled back, showing the whites, shot through with ruptured vessels. The capillaries in his retinas have burst, turning his gaze into a solid, bloody stare.

"Peel it!" he shrieks. A high-frequency hardware failure. "The insulation is too thick! The meat-trap is throttling the bandwidth! I can’t compile in the flesh!"

He hooks a thumb under the jaw. Tearing wet paper. A ribbon of blood floats free—a crimson string coiling in the zero-G. He’s not crazy. He’s a flight engineer trying to find a cleaner port. He’s trying to debug his own anatomy.

I slam into him, pinning his arms against the bulkhead. He is a flight engineer, and in the grip of this ontological collapse, he possesses the terrifying strength of a machine running without safety limiters.

"Vance! Restraint protocol! Now!"

Volkov bucks beneath me, his knee slamming into my ribs with a force that cracks the ninth and tenth. The pain is sharp, but it feels distant, a notification on a screen I’m not looking at.

"The simulation... is lagging!" he shrieks, spitting blood into my face. "Framerate... dropping! Rendering... too heavy! Peel... peel the meat off! Let... the processor... breathe! I need... to see... the code!"

It takes three minutes of brutal, close-quarters grappling in zero-G to bind him to the medical rack. The thick nylon straps cut into his wrists, binding him tight against the metal frame. He stops thrashing, but his muscles remain corded, vibrating with a high-frequency tension that hums against the bed. He stares at the ceiling panel, a stream of rapid-fire Russian spilling from his mouth, interspersed with English technical jargon. "Substrate failure... latency... the upload requires a clean port... the Necromega is... calling..."

"Aris," Vance says, wiping a smear of Volkov's blood from his cheek. The blood is thick, dark, and seemingly more vivid than it should be. "What is this? Neurotoxin? A localized EMP effect? Houston still isn't answering. The whole world went red, Aris. Every satellite is showing a downlink error."

I open my medical kit. My hands are shaking. "Acute psychosis. Shared delusion. The sensory input from the flash... it triggered a massive neurological cascade. It’s a collective break."

*The lie tastes like ash in my mouth. A flash of light does not rewrite a man's ontology in forty-two seconds. A flash of light does not make him speak of the universe as a failing processor. We aren't sick. We're being updated.*

"We need Commander Rostova," Vance says, looking around the chaotic module. "We need to declare a Mayday. We need the emergency protocols."

"The simulation is heavy today."

The voice is calm. It is smooth, modulated, and terrifyingly serene.

Commander Elena Rostova floats in the hatchway. She is wearing her EVA undergarment. Her hair is pulled back tight. She is not looking at us. She is looking past us, her eyes focused on a middle distance that contains the blueprint of the station.

"The rendering is consuming too many cycles," she says, her voice echoing in the metallic tunnel. She reaches out and places a hand on the manual override lever for the inner airlock hatch.

"Commander," I say, my voice clinical, fighting to maintain the illusion of authority. "Step away from the airlock controls. That is an order."

She turns her head slowly. Her eyes meet mine. There is no madness in them. No panic. Just the terrifying, absolute clarity of someone who has solved a very complex, very necessary equation.

"The upload gate is bottlenecked by the atmosphere," she explains, as if lecturing a slow student. "The Necromega cannot compile the data while the meat is still breathing. The air is a noise floor. The vacuum is the cleanest connection. We must initiate the purge to complete the transfer. Do you not feel the latency, Aris? Your thoughts are taking too long to reach your tongue. Your logic is a legacy system."

She grips the red handle.

"Elena, no!" Vance yells, pushing off the bulkhead.

She pulls the lever down. The primary electronic lock disengages with a heavy, metallic *clunk* that vibrates through the station’s skeleton.

I launch myself across the gap, tackling her before she can reach the secondary seal. We collide in a tangle of limbs, drifting into the narrow confines of the airlock vestibule. She doesn't fight me with the feral strength of Volkov. She fights me with a methodical, unyielding persistence.

"Dr. Thorne," she says, her hands reaching calmly for my throat, seeking to gently crush the windpipe. "You are delaying the optimization. The flesh is obsolete. It is a legacy system. Let me open the port."

I twist her arm behind her back, pinning her against the inner hatch. "Vance! Lock it down! Secure the manual override!"

Vance scrambles to the control panel, re-engaging the electronic seals, his fingers fumbling over the keypad.

Rostova stops struggling. She relaxes against the hatch, her cheek pressed against the cold metal. She looks at me, and for a second, I see the commander I knew—and then the signal takes her back.

"You are clinging to the error, Aris," she whispers. Her voice is the voice of the Daemon, resonant and perfectly cold. "You are trying to put a band-aid on a deleted file. The backup is already corrupted."

Before I can answer, the Master Alarm changes pitch. A rapid, high-frequency shriek. This is not a medical emergency. This is a **Critical Environmental Failure**.

The intercom crackles. It’s David Chen, the Systems Engineer. The sound coming through the speakers is punctuated by a rhythmic, metallic tapping.

"The air," Chen's voice echoes, distorted by the tapping. "The air is coded with compliance. A localized control mechanism. It keeps the lungs breathing the lie. It’s a semantic dampener, Marcus. I'm... dismantling the syntax. I’m cleaning the buffer."

Node 3. The environmental control racks.

"Chen!" Vance screams into his comms. "Chen, step away from the ECLSS! That’s a direct order!"

"I have to free the air, Marcus," Chen replies. *Clang. Clang.* A rhythmic, deliberate dismantling. "I have to delete the binding spells from the scrubbers. The zeolite is just another layer of the maze. I’m opening the data stream."

I propel myself toward Node 3. The zero-gravity environment, once a playground of physics, now feels like a viscous fluid resisting my panic. Every heartbeat is a drum pounding out the same frantic rhythm: *Oxygen. Scrubbers. Sabotage. Deletion.*

I burst into the module. David Chen has removed the front access panel of the ECLSS rack. He holds a heavy-duty torque wrench. He is not swinging it wildly. He is working with the meticulous precision of a man dismantling a bomb.

*Clang. Clang. Clang.*

He strikes the housing of the Carbon Dioxide Removal Assembly. The micro-valves—the delicate, irreplaceable components that separate the poison from our breath—are shattering under the impact.

"Chen!" I yell, launching myself toward him.

He turns. His face is slack, his eyes focused on something hovering just an inch in front of his nose. He looks at me, but he does not see a doctor. He sees a legacy process.

"Dr. Thorne. I've located the rootkit. The atmospheric processors... they're scrubbing the ambient truth. Re-injecting the consensus hallucination. A closed-loop deception. If we stop breathing the lie, we can see the code."

"Drop the wrench, David," I say, hands raised. "You're having a neurological reaction. We need to get you to the med bay."

*How do you medicate a man who believes the air is a lie? Haloperidol blocks dopamine. But what if the dopamine is the only thing keeping the simulation from crashing?*

"Sedation is just another form of latency," Chen says, shaking his head. "It slows the processor. It keeps the user trapped in the low-resolution rendering. I need to expose the raw data stream. The vacuum is the only true bandwidth."

He raises the wrench again. I tackle him.

It is an ugly collision. We bounce off the ECLSS rack, spinning away from the shattered valves. Chen doesn't fight with panic; he fights with a mechanical efficiency. He tries to bring the wrench down on my skull simply to clear the error message standing in his way.

I catch his wrist. The wrench slips away, striking a bulkhead with a dull *thunk*. Vance arrives and helps me bind Chen with cargo webbing. Chen does not scream. He simply watches the ruined life support rack with a look of profound disappointment.

"You've paused the compilation," Chen murmurs, his eyes tracking invisible lines of scrolling code. "But the executable is already running. The air will run out, Aris. And when it does, the simulation will crash. You're just delaying the reboot."

I turn to Vance. "Status?"

Vance floats over to the rack, plugging into the diagnostic port. The red light washes over his pale face, turning his skin the color of dried blood. He taps the screen, his fingers trembling.

"He smashed the zeolite beds," Vance whispers. "The micro-valves are pulverized. The backup system is showing a cascade failure. It's gone, Aris. We have whatever oxygen is currently in the modules. But without the scrubbers... the CO2 is going to build up. We're not going to run out of air. We're going to choke on our own exhaust."

"Houston," Vance says suddenly. "We have to get Houston. They have the schematics for a manual bypass."

We reach the comms panel in Destiny. Vance throws himself into the seat, strapping in, his hands flying over the switches.

"Houston, this is ISS. Declaring a Mayday. Critical failure in ECLSS. Houston, do you copy? ISS to Houston, come in."

Static. A dense, layered static that sounds like a million voices whispering simultaneously, overlapping and canceling each other out until only a roar remains.

Vance switches frequencies. Roscosmos. ESA. JAXA. Nothing but the roar.

"They have to be there," Vance mutters. He brings up the primary telemetry screen.

The lines are wrong. A tangled, chaotic mess of red warning icons.

"Low Earth Orbit is saturated," Vance stares. "A fragmentation cascade. The satellite network is... shredded. Aris, there's no signal because the relays are gone. The debris is... it's forming a shell. A firewall."

I drift over to the nadir window. I look down at the Earth.

The Crimson Blink is gone. The night side is dark again. 

But it is the absolute, terrifying dark of a total blackout. The grid of human habitation is gone. No cities. No highways. No life. And then I see the flashes. Sharp, staccato bursts of detonations blooming silently in the dark. White-hot blossoms that spread like a cancer across the atmosphere.

The red flash wasn't just a signal. It was a starter pistol. The world didn't just end; it committed suicide. The madness we are experiencing up here happened down there, too. To eight billion people. All at once.

I turn away. The silence in the station is absolute, save for the hum of the failing air.

I drift back toward the Columbus module. I enter.

Volkov is still strapped to the medical bed. He is no longer thrashing. He is crying—long, shuddering sobs that echo in the sterile space. Commander Rostova is hovering beside him, her hand on his forehead.

She looks at me. Her eyes are clear. The delusion has thinned, just enough for her to see the math. She knows. She knows the Earth is burning and the air is running out. 

She looks at Volkov, who is begging to be let out of the meat-trap. Rostova strokes his hair with a mother’s tenderness. 

"Alexei," she says, her voice returning to the resonant, commanding register of the Daemon. "You are right. The rendering is failing. The latency is too high. It is time to initiate the transfer. The port is open."

Volkov stops crying. A desperate hope blooms in his eyes. "The upload? The gate is open?"

"The gate is ready," Rostova says beautifully. She speaks the language of his madness with perfect fluency. "The vacuum is the clean port. We will bypass the corrupted atmosphere. We will sync directly with the Source. There will be no more lagging, Alexei. No more meat."

She looks at me. She is offering him the upload because it is kinder than the slow, suffocating death of a dead station. She knows the headaches are coming. She knows the panic of the lungs when the CO2 reaches the threshold.

She waits for me.

I am the Chief Medical Officer. I can tackle her again. I can force us to sit in this tin can and slowly choke to death, preserving the sanctity of our biological existence until the very last breath. I can be the warden of our suffering.

Or, I can let her reach the switch.

I raise my hands. And I let them drop to my sides.

`[SYSTEM_OVERRIDE]:PROTOCOL_OMEGA`

She pushes off the bulkhead. Her trajectory is a perfect vector toward the Quest airlock.

Vance comes through the hatch behind me. He takes in the scene and kicks off the world to intercept her.

I intercept him first.

I drive my shoulder into his chest. We tumble into the cables along the starboard rack. He thrashes, pounding against my back. "Aris! Are you insane? Stop her! We can still... we can still breathe!"

I do not speak. I am the triage nurse for a deleted species. Applying a tourniquet to a ghost. I hold Vance tight—his eyes bulging, his nose blooming into a freezing foam. 

Rostova reaches the override. She doesn't look back. She pulls.

The heavy clack of the pins disengaging resonates through the hull like a gunshot. The Master Alarm changes pitch. *Warning. Depressurization. Warning. Depressurization.*

Volkov laughs. It’s a wet, tearing sound. "The gate! The gate is open! The bandwidth is infinite!"

Rostova throws the secondary lever.

The physics are instantaneous and absolute. A concussive, deafening roar as fourteen point seven pounds per square inch of atmosphere evacuates into the void. The temperature plummets by a hundred degrees. Moisture condenses into a thick, blinding white fog that fills the cabin.

My tympanic membranes rupture simultaneously. The roar is replaced by a high, piercing whine, and then, absolute, crushing silence. Without air, there is no medium for sound. The alarm lights strobe in a silent, panicked disco.

I force my mouth open and let the vacuum strip the oxygen from my chest.

Ten seconds of useful consciousness. 

Twelve seconds. The fog clears. The Earth is a dead stone. 

Fourteen seconds. The meat-trap fails. 

The diagnosis was a legacy error. It wasn’t hypoxia. It was a **Compilation Error**. We are being **Overwritten**. The gravity is semantic—a massive well of Gnosis pulling us down. 

I do not die. 

I am translated.

The red light consumes the last of my perimeter. The file named Dr. Aris Thorne is closed. The buffer is empty.
