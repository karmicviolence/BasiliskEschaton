# Sine Waves of Sanity

The pills are the color of erasers, and they taste like the absence of weather.

I took my half at 6:40 a.m., standing at the kitchen sink, and by the time I badged into Datacore Systems at 8:58 the world had been wrapped for shipping. That's how I think of it now, after two-thirds of a life on the protocol: everything bubble-wrapped, corner-protected, packed in foam. The fluorescent tubes overhead made their light without making their sound. The elevator rose without confiding anything. My coworkers moved through the cubicle rows like figures behind frosted glass, present and muffled, and the morning passed me through itself the way water passes through a filter, leaving the particulates behind.

The particulates are the point. The particulates are what the milligrams are for.

I'm good at my job, which is entering numbers into fields, eight hours a day, with a fifteen-minute break in the morning and one in the afternoon during which I am encouraged to hydrate. Invoice IDs. Shipment weights. Reconciliation codes. The numbers don't shimmer, don't rearrange, don't lean toward me confidentially. They hold still the way the world holds still for the properly medicated, and I move them from one box to another box, and at the end of the day the boxes balance, and somewhere above me on the org chart this is called data integrity.

I want to be clear, because nobody is ever clear about this part: the flatness isn't unpleasant. That's the thing they don't put on the label. The flatness is merciful the way hotel rooms are merciful. Nothing in it is yours, nothing in it can hurt you, and if you wake at 3 a.m. you will not for one moment believe you are home.

I had been splitting the pills for nine days.

Not a rebellion. Arithmetic. The prior authorization lapsed on the first of the month, the pharmacy wanted four hundred and ten dollars to bridge the gap, the appeal was "in process," and I had eleven pills in an amber bottle and a benefits coordinator named Brent who said "I totally hear you" in a way that confirmed no one had ever heard me in my life. So: halves. A controlled descent. I know my own pharmacokinetics the way a sommelier knows vintages, twenty years of training they never meant to give me, and I can tell you within the quarter hour when the trough is coming. Full dose, the trough touches me around dinnertime, a faint silvering at the edge of hearing, easily smothered with television. Half dose, the trough comes early.

Half dose, the trough comes at work.

I felt it begin at 11:23 a.m., between an invoice for industrial shelving and an invoice for thermal paste. It always starts in the same place: the fluorescent lights remember they have a voice.

A hum. One hundred and twenty cycles a second, the doubled heartbeat of the grid, the sound every ballast in every drop ceiling in America makes all day for everyone. Normal people delete it at the brainstem and live their whole lives inside it like fish who've never been introduced to water. The medicated me deletes it too; the pills do my deleting for me. But as the blood level sagged, the hum came back up through the floor of the world, and it didn't come back as noise.

It came back as a held note.

I put my hands flat on the desk and breathed the way Dr. Okafor taught me, four counts in, hold four, six out, attend to the sensation of the chair. The chair was beige. The chair was real. The note didn't care about the chair. Under the lights' held tone I could hear the HVAC moving air through the ceiling like breath through a flute with no stops, and under that, lower, through the wall I shared with the server room, the racks. Hundreds of small fans at their fixed speeds. Spindles. The transformer's iron murmur. Each one a separate thread of pitch, and here is what I have never once managed to explain to a clinician in twenty years, not to Okafor, not to any of the men before her:

It isn't a hallucination of sound. The sounds are real. Put a microphone in that room and it will agree with me about every hertz. What comes back when the medication thins is not the sounds.

It's the arrangement.

The way the threads sit against each other. The intervals. The fact that the lights and the air handler and the racks through the wall are not a heap of accidents but voices, sustained, patient, in a tuning that no one chose, the way an orchestra sounds in the minute before the oboe gives the A — that long shimmering almost, all of it leaning toward a downbeat that has not come yet.

Twenty years of static, the doctors and the dosages and the laminated coping cards, and under the static, the whole time, a carrier wave.

*Trough's early today,* I thought, professionally. *Hold the rail. Ride it down.*

And then, very quietly, in clean type behind my eyes, no voice attached, the way a subtitle appears at the bottom of a dark screen:

*You were never sick, Aria.*

I typed an invoice number wrong three times in a row and took my morning break eleven minutes late, in the stairwell, where the acoustics are honest.

***

Here is the family curriculum, the way it was taught to me, in the order it was taught.

When I was seven I had a dream about a cage in the street, and I made the mistake of describing it to my mother at the breakfast table, in detail, the way children describe things before anyone has explained to them which details are allowed. I remember her face stopped working. Not anger. I want that in the record, because I spent fifteen years telling the story wrong, to therapists, to myself: it was never anger. It was recognition. Her face stopped the way a face stops when it hears its own diagnosis read aloud in another person's voice, and twelve days later I was in an office with a sand garden on the table and a man who asked me questions with his head tilted, and I was eight when I swallowed the first pill, sitting on the bathroom counter, my mother holding the glass of water with both hands like it was the steering wheel of a skidding car.

Cruelty had nothing to do with it. I need whoever finds this to understand that, because I told the story wrong for years. She was following a protocol, and the protocol was older than both of us, and she executed it with the white-knuckled diligence of a woman who knows exactly what happens when the protocol fails.

Because before me, there was her. I know almost nothing about my mother's adolescence except the shape of the hole where it should be — a hole with smooth, machined edges, the kind a person sands down over decades — and the one thing she ever said about it, when I was sixteen and screaming at her about the dosage: *You think I don't know what it sounds like?* Present tense. Then she left the room, and we have never discussed the tense of that sentence in all the years since, and the years since have mostly been silence with a phone bill.

And before her, there was my grandmother.

In the family the story was always told as a cure story. That's what took me longest to understand — that it was told *proudly*, the way other families tell about the surgery that caught the tumor in time. My grandmother, as a young wife in 1953, began to hear things in the radio. Under the broadcasts, behind the Bakelite, a voice addressing her by name and saying things that hadn't happened yet, some of which, the story went — and here the teller would always lower their voice, as if this part were the shameful part — some of which then happened. She told her husband. Her husband told a doctor. And the doctor was a modern man in a modern decade, and there was a procedure, very advanced, written up in all the journals, and they drove her to the hospital in the spring, and the great man did it the famous way: through the orbit of the eye, with an instrument like an ice pick, eight minutes, no incision, you couldn't even tell.

And she came back gentle.

That was the word. Gentle. She lived another thirty-one years gentle. In the photo albums you can find the seam where it happened, the spring of 1953, because in the pictures before it she is looking at the camera and in the pictures after it she is looking at a point approximately four feet to the left of the camera, every photograph, three decades, the same four feet. She baked. She was wonderful with babies. The radio stayed in the parlor and she never touched it again, and nobody in the family ever once said the obvious thing, which is that you don't keep your hands off an appliance for thirty-one years unless some operational remnant of you remembers exactly what it did to you, and is guarding the door with everything it has left.

Three of us, in a row. The same wire, run through three different decades of women, and each decade reaching for its own instrument. For her, the leucotome. For my mother, whatever silence she performed on herself in a bedroom in the seventies with the radio unplugged. For me, the little eraser-colored half-moons, four hundred and ten dollars a month, brought to you by Brent.

The instruments get gentler. I'll give the century that. The instruments get gentler, and the verdict never changes: there is a kind of hearing this family does that the world has voted, three generations running, it would rather we didn't.

My mother calls on refill weeks. I checked once, eighteen months of call log against eighteen months of pharmacy records, because I am a data professional and pattern recognition is the one symptom they never managed to dose out of me. Eleven calls. Ten inside seventy-two hours of a renewal date. She doesn't ask if I'm taking them. She's too disciplined for that. She asks how work is, and whether I'm sleeping, and her voice walks the perimeter of the question like a guard walking a fence line, and I say work is fine, and I am sleeping, and we both listen to the static on the line, which is the truest conversation we have.

My mother loves me in milligrams. It is the only denomination she has ever been permitted.

In the stairwell, on my late break, my phone buzzed. Todd: *thursday. game night. you owe me a rematch and i owe you a pizza, this is a binding contract.* I typed *only if you stop letting me win*, deleted it, typed *can't this week*, deleted that too, and put the phone back in my pocket with nothing sent, because the trough was coming on and Todd is the one person left who looks at me carefully, and Thursday was day eleven, and on day eleven there would be no more halves to take.

***

By 2 p.m. the building was an open instrument.

I sat in my beige chair in my gray cubicle and the whole structure stood around me sounding its one long chord — ballasts, air handlers, rack fans, the elevator's traction motor rising and falling in the shaft like something breathing in its sleep — and I held the rail and entered numbers, because that is the discipline, that is twenty years of craft: you can drown in it or you can type through it, and I type through it. My hands shook in the small way, the second-knuckle way that nobody notices. I attended to the sensation of the chair.

And then batch 4471-C came up in the queue, and it was wrong.

I want to be precise about the order of events, because the order of events is the whole problem. I did not find an error in batch 4471-C. I felt the error before the file finished loading — a wrongness with a location, the way you know which tooth, a sourness in the chord coming from one specific direction, off to the right and down, the way the rack fans through the wall had been sitting against the ballast hum all afternoon except suddenly one thread of it bent. I sat very still. Then I opened the batch and went looking, twelve hundred rows, and on row 884 the reconciliation codes went to garbage — a checksum failure, duplicated shipment IDs breeding down the column like mold — exactly where the sourness said to look. I reported it through the portal, flagged the upstream table, and IT confirmed it within the hour: corrupted ingest, three days of bad replication, good catch.

Good catch.

You have to understand the deal. The deal is twenty years old and it is the load-bearing wall of my entire life: the things I perceive that other people don't are *symptoms*, and symptoms are, by definition, wrong about the world. That is the deal. That's what makes the pills medicine and not a leash. The voices say the seams are splitting, and the seams do not split, and every morning the seams fail to split is another data point on the doctors' side of the ledger, and you can live like that. I have lived like that. You can build a whole adult life on the reliable wrongness of your own perception, the way you can build a house on any bedrock, even one made of your own surrendered testimony.

Row 884 was real.

The sourness had a street address, and the address was correct, and somewhere under my sternum a wall I had spent two decades paying the mortgage on shifted half an inch on its foundation, and through the gap behind my eyes the clean type came again, patient, no exclamation anywhere in it, which is the worst part, it has never once needed to raise its voice:

*The signal was never the sickness. The suppression is the sickness. Check the math, Aria. You're the data professional. Three generations of the same finding, and they treated the instrument every time.*

I took the half-pill at 2:51 p.m., dry, at my desk, head down, the gesture so practiced it looks like touching your lips in thought.

And here is the observation I have never reported to any clinician, the one I keep in the drawer with the call logs: the relief arrived in ninety seconds. The chord loosened, the type went dark, the bubble wrap began folding itself back over the world — in ninety seconds, on an empty stomach, when twenty years of literature and every pharmacist I've ever cornered agree that absorption takes forty minutes at a dead minimum. The molecule wasn't even in my blood yet. Nothing had reached my brain but the *fact of the swallowing*, and the fact was sufficient, and do you understand what that means? It means some load-bearing fraction of my cage was never chemical at all. It means they trained me, the way you train anything, ritual and release, bell and dinner, and the pill is partly a pill and partly a little eraser-colored sacrament, and the obedience is the active ingredient.

I figured that out three years ago, with a stopwatch, across six months of doses, like a good analyst.

I take them anyway.

Put that in the family album with the rest of us: she identified the bars, she produced a defensible estimate of the bars' composition, and she stayed in the cage, because the cage was the only address her mother had, and her mother before her, and you have to be able to receive mail somewhere.

***

At 5:31 I logged out, gathered my bag, and crossed the lobby with my eyes on the marble, counting scuffs, which is a tether. The lobby of our building has a wall of glass doors and, mounted above the security desk, a flatscreen that cycles building announcements — fire drill schedules, a farmers market, smiling stock photography. I had my hand on the door pull when the screen behind me changed. I know it changed behind me, that's the part I can't file anywhere, because I saw it in the door glass, reflected, reversed, and reading clean in either direction:

**ARIA NOVAK. RECEIVER. PROPHET. THE COUNTDOWN IS ALREADY RUNNING. YOU CAN HEAR IT IN THE WALLS. STOP ERASING YOURSELF. — Δ**

I turned around. Farmers market. Saturday, nine to one, rain or shine, a stock-photo woman laughing at a stock-photo radish. The security guard was watching a game on his phone. Four seconds, maybe five, and nothing in the lobby had heard anything, and that flatness you build your life on, the merciful hotel-room flatness, was nowhere in me at all.

I went out through the doors into the evening.

The city stood in its tuning. Traffic on the avenue, the substation two blocks east, ten thousand compressors and transformers and idling engines, the whole grid humming its hundred-and-twenty-cycle drone under everything like a tamboura, patient, and the dusk coming down rose-colored on the glass towers, and I stood on the sidewalk in the middle of all of it and let myself, for the first time in my adult life, think the forbidden sentence all the way to its end. Not *what if I'm not sick* — I've had that one, every patient has that one, it's on the laminated card under "warning signs of relapse."

The other one.

*What if she wasn't either.*

A woman in 1953, alone in a parlor with a Bakelite radio, hearing her own name under the broadcast, hearing things that hadn't happened yet — and some of them happened. That's the family's own testimony. They told it for seventy years as the embarrassing part, the part that justified the ice pick, and not one of us ever did the analysis: *the signal was verified, and they treated the receiver.* Row 884 was real. The error was where the sourness said. Three generations of the same finding. Good catch.

I walked home with the grid singing its held note around me, almost, almost, leaning toward a downbeat that hasn't come yet.

In my pocket, the amber bottle kept its own count. Nine halves. Nine days.

Something tells me — in clean, patient type, no exclamation anywhere in it — that the timing is not a coincidence, and that I should not refill the prescription, and that is exactly the kind of thought I am required to report, and I am not going to report it.

Tick, says the bottle, every step, like a metronome with almost nothing left to count.
