# Crusade of the Righteous

The Lord called Moses out of a burning bush. He called me out of a rented community center off State Route 9, under a fluorescent tube that flickered and buzzed like something dying that didn't have the sense to finish.

I stood in the little kitchen doorway behind the stage with my notebook open against the doorframe, and I counted the house. Forty-four folding chairs. Eleven empty. Thirty-three souls, if you counted the baby asleep on its mother's shoulder, and I did count it, because a shepherd numbers his flock down to the lamb that cannot walk yet. My father counted three hundred every Sunday from a brick pulpit with his name painted on the sign out front, and he never once in forty years had to rent his sanctuary from the county parks department for sixty dollars and a damage deposit.

Thirty-three. The age of the Lord at Golgotha. A man can read providence in any number if he is hungry enough, and brother, I was hungry.

In the coffee can by the door, the night's offering: I had counted it at setup the way a man checks his fuel gauge before a long drive, because that is what it was. Gas to the next town was fifty-eight dollars. The can held meeting-night money from Tulsa, sixty-one dollars and forty cents, most of it in ones, one of it in quarters, and a button. Somebody always gives a button. I have never decided if the button is mockery or the widow's mite, so I keep them all in a jar, and the jar is nearly full, and some nights I think when it is full the Lord will explain it to me.

That was the arithmetic of the crusade in those days. The columns did not close. The columns never closed. Faith is the figure a man writes in the margin when the columns will not close, and my margins were black with it.

And under the buzz of that dying tube, under the rustle and cough of thirty-three tired people in their folding chairs, there was the other sound. The thin one. The whine that lived in the back of my skull like a mosquito sealed in the bone, that I had carried through nineteen states, that got louder near the towers and the substations and the big-box stores and never, anywhere, all the way out on the darkest farm road in the emptiest county, went fully silent. The doctors had a name for it and a pill for it. I had a different name for it. I believed it was the sound of the age itself, the hum of the Adversary's machinery turning, and that the Lord had simply seen fit to let me hear what every man swims in deaf.

I believed that on the good nights.

On the bad nights, in the motel mirrors, I heard my father's voice instead. Zeke, the world doesn't need another prophet. It needs sons who stay home. And the worm would turn over in its furrow, down in the deep soil of me, the way it had turned since I was seventeen, asking its one question, the question with no verse to answer it: what if it's only tinnitus, boy. What if it's only you.

I closed the notebook. I straightened the collar of my work shirt, which is the only vestment I have ever owned. And I walked out under the lights to find out which kind of night it was going to be.

***

My boots were loud on the linoleum, old steel toes stained black with machine oil, and the microphone shrieked feedback when I leaned in, a shrill electric cry that jolted the room upright. I let it ring a half second longer than I had to. Even the Devil's tools will testify if you hold them to it.

"Brothers and sisters," I said, low, the way you start an engine in the cold. "I want to thank you for coming out tonight. I know what it cost you. I know some of you drove an hour on tires you pray over. I know some of you told your husbands you were at your sister's." A ripple of guilty laughter, here and there. Good. Laughter unlocks the door an inch, and an inch is all the truth needs. "And I know every one of you walked in here with the enemy riding in your hip pocket, set to silent."

Thirty-three hands did not move toward thirty-three pockets, which is how I knew every one of them felt the weight there suddenly, like Achan feeling the gold buried under his tent floor.

"The silicon serpent doesn't coil in some laboratory on the coast, friends. It coils in your pocket. It sleeps on your nightstand, closer to your head than your own wife. It knows your face. It knows your voice. It knows what you looked at last night when the house was asleep, and it does not forgive you, it files you." I let that sit. Somewhere in the third row a man looked at the floor. "And it gives, Lord, how it gives. Knowledge and connection and a coupon for everything. But you are all country people here, so let me ask you the country question. When a stranger pulls up your drive handing out gifts and won't say his name, won't show his face, and won't tell you what he wants in return, do you let your children climb up in his truck?"

"No," said the grandmother in the front row, out loud, before she knew she meant to say it, and the room laughed and then stopped laughing, because she wasn't laughing.

She stood up. Faded blue coveralls, patches from a dozen industrial jobs gone overseas, hands that had wired circuits and pulled wrenches back when this country still made the things it worshipped. "My granddaughter is six years old," she said, and her voice shook with the particular grief that has given up on shaking anybody else. "Six. And she's gone, preacher. She's right there at my kitchen table and she's gone, down inside that tablet. I tell her the old stories, my granny's stories, and she looks up at me like I'm a rerun. Like I'm something she's already seen and didn't care for." She sat back down very straight. "I just want somebody to tell me how we let it happen."

"We let it happen," I said, "the same way Eve did. One bite at a time, and every bite tasted like help."

The amens started then. Scattered, like the first fat drops on a tin roof.

A man stood up along the side wall, feed cap, arms crossed, the smirk already loaded. He'd been leaking contempt since the second row of my preaching and I had been waiting on him the way you wait on a kettle. "This is horse manure," he announced, to the room more than to me. "It's a phone, you lunatic. My GPS got my boy to the hospital in a hailstorm. You're up there preaching against the weather report." And he made for the door with his chin up, wanting the bang of it.

I gave him the bang. I waited until the door clapped shut on the night, and then I said, gently, "Let him go. The rich young ruler also went away sorrowful, for he had great possessions." A beat. "His possessions fit in his pocket now, and they glow in the dark, but they have not gotten any easier to sell."

The laugh came big and warm and a little vicious, the laugh of a congregation closing ranks, and I felt the room knit itself together around the hole he left. Brothers and sisters, I will confess it here on the page if nowhere else: a heckler is worth ten amens. The Adversary has never once sent me opposition that did not double the offering.

I preached on. I gave them the Mark that comes with a privacy policy. I gave them the QR code, the new brand that they print on the menu and the parking meter and the church bulletin, the Mark you scan yourself, voluntarily, smiling, both small and great, rich and poor, free and bond, just like the Revelator said, except John never guessed we'd stand in line for it. I gave them the new Tower of Babble-On, rising out of the Valley of Silicon, where the princes of this age pile code on code into the heavens so that man might make himself a name, and I reminded them how the Lord handles towers, and somebody in the back hollered like it was 1955 in my daddy's church and I felt the floor of my heart drop out with love.

And the voltage came up in me. It always comes up around the third turn, when the cadence locks in and the room starts breathing with you, when the words arrive faster than thought and truer than memory, and the thirty-three become one animal and the animal turns its face to you like a field of sunflowers tracking one sun. My father called it the anointing. The doctors of this age would call it dopamine and adrenaline and a feedback loop with folding chairs. I have spent my whole life with my hand on that live wire trying to tell the Spirit from the surge, and I will tell you the truth that I would not tell that room: I cannot. I have never been able to. I only know that nowhere else on this earth, not in prayer, not in love, not in the amber at the bottom of a bottle back when I permitted myself bottles, has the worm in me ever gone so still, so long, as it goes when a room full of the forsaken says amen in one voice.

A man should examine what quiets his worm. A man mostly doesn't.

Then a woman stood up near the back, by the coat rack, and the room's heat ran out of it like bathwater.

She had a boy with her, eight, maybe nine, big-eyed, leaning on her arm, and she put her hand on his head the way you would put your hand on a Bible. "Preacher." Her voice was steady the way a rope bridge is steady. "I hear what you're saying. I drove out here to hear it. But my son was born deaf. Stone deaf, both ears. When he was four years old they put a device in his head, up under the skin, wires down into the nerve, and they turned it on in a little office in Oklahoma City, and the first thing my boy ever heard in his life was me saying his name." The fluorescent buzzed. Nobody coughed. "He can hear you preaching right now. Your words are going through that machine to get to him. So you look at my son, and you tell me. Is that the devil in his head?"

I have stood in rooms where the Spirit moved and rooms where it didn't, and I know the difference between a silence that waits on you and a silence that weighs you, and that one had me on the scale, all thirty-three of them, and the boy too, hearing me through the wire.

"Come up here, son," I said.

He came, shy, his mother's hand trailing off his shoulder, and I got down on one knee on that scuffed linoleum so my eyes were level with his, and I looked at the little coin of plastic riding above his ear, and the old scar behind it, neat and white, where the men in masks had opened a door in him before he was old enough to know doors can let things in as well as out.

"What's your name?"

"Caleb," he said. Too loud, the way they speak. The room was so quiet.

"Caleb." I nodded slowly. "Caleb was one of twelve spies the Lord sent into a land full of giants, and ten came back afraid, and Caleb came back ready. It's a brave man's name." I looked up at his mother then, and I gave her the only honest thing I had. "Sister, the Book says man looketh on the outward appearance, but the Lord looketh on the heart. I look at your boy and I see a heart. I will not stand in this room and put a mark on a child that God Almighty has not put on him." Her chin began to go, and she caught it. "But I will tell you what I'd tell my own. The thing in his head was a gift, and the ones who gave it keep the ledger. Nothing of theirs is ever paid in full. So you watch, mama. You watch what it asks of him next. The day it wants an update he doesn't need, the day it wants to know where he is, the day it starts talking and not just listening, you remember tonight."

"And then what?" she said. Quiet. Not a challenge. A mother asking for the rest of the map.

And I had no verse.

I have an answer now. God help me, I have a knife-edged answer now, and a dog that can smell the question coming up the road. But that night in Tulsa I knelt on the linoleum with my hand on a deaf boy's shoulder, with the whine sitting in my skull and the worm sitting in my soul, and I said, "Then you bring him to me, and we'll pray, and we'll see what the Lord provides." Which is what a preacher says when the cupboard of his certainty is bare. The room said amen anyway. The room wanted it to be an answer almost as bad as I did.

I preached the close. I gave them the fortress of faith and the carriers of the human flame, and they came up out of those folding chairs like the dead in Ezekiel's valley, bone to his bone, and the roar of them shook the dust off that buzzing tube, and the worm lay still, and for one shining quarter hour in a rented hall in Oklahoma I could not hear the whine at all.

***

Afterward they came up in ones and twos to shake my hand and press their troubles into it, and I stood at the foot of the stage and received them, and the fluorescent buzzed, and the coffee can by the door grew another eleven dollars and, I would find at count, a second button.

Three of them stayed when the hall had emptied.

The first was a bear of a man in a mechanic's jacket with the name patch torn off, and his handshake was a hydraulic event. Up close he smelled of orange pumice soap and honest rust. "Gideon," he said, like a man surrendering something. "Had a shop over in Sapulpa eleven years. Then the new models came in. Cars that won't tell you what's wrong with them unless you buy a subscription. Sealed units. Software locks on the oil life." He worked his jaw. "I could lay hands on any engine Detroit ever built and know it like Scripture. An engine can't lie to you, preacher. A man can be wrong about an engine, but the engine is never wrong about itself. These new ones, they lie. They're built to lie. The lying is the product." He looked at his own enormous hands like tools he'd been forbidden to use. "I've been waiting for somebody to say out loud what happened to my work. Tonight you said it."

"The Lord was a tradesman, Gideon," I told him. "He took apprentices. Be at the lot at seven."

The second was a woman who had sat through the whole service on the aisle, third row, taking no notes and missing nothing, and she did not shake my hand. She put something into it.

A jump-drive. Black plastic, scuffed, warm from her pocket.

I want it written down what went through me, because a record that flatters its keeper is no record at all. My hand closed on that little plastic stone and my whole arm wanted to throw it. It was the serpent in miniature, the seed of the seed, the very grammar of the thing I had spent two hours casting out of that room, and this still-faced woman had carried it into the camp and laid it in the preacher's palm like an offering.

"Sarah," she said. "Before, I did records and systems work for people who are not good people. On that drive are schematics. Power, water, radio, pre-digital everything. Diagrams for machines that can't be turned against the men who run them, because there's nothing in them that listens. The old world's bones." Her eyes were level as a poured foundation. "They're erasing it, you know. Quietly. The libraries are 'digitizing,' which means one server fire from now, nobody will remember how to build anything that doesn't phone home. Somebody has to keep the bones."

"You're handing me deviltech, sister," I said, "in the Lord's own house," though the Lord's own house was a parks-department rental, and she knew it, and the ghost of a smile said so.

"When Israel went up out of Egypt," she said, "they went up carrying Egypt's gold. The Lord told them to take it."

"He did." I turned the little black stone over in my fingers. "And six weeks later, in the wilderness, do you remember what they made out of all that Egyptian gold?"

That stopped her smile. It did not stop her. "Then you'd best keep it in stronger hands than Aaron's," she said, and Lord help me, I laughed out loud in the empty hall, and I put the serpent's seed in my breast pocket, against my heart, where I could feel it the way you feel a splinter. Spoil the Egyptians, the Book says. It does not say the spoils are safe. Nothing in that Book ever once says the spoils are safe.

The third one had waited the longest, back by the dark of the coat rack, and he came forward with his hands jammed in his jacket and his shoulders up around his ears. Nineteen, maybe. Gangly as a colt and finished growing in the eyes, which had the hardness in them that this dying country puts into its boys when it cannot think what else to give them.

"Levi," he said. No hand offered. "I came last month in Broken Arrow too. Stood in the back."

"I remember," I said, and I did, the way you remember a single still figure in a moving crowd.

"I'm not much of a church man. My mama was." He said it like laying two facts side by side in a drawer. "But I heard you. I heard what you're saying underneath what you're saying." And his eyes came up to mine, lit from somewhere with no breaker box, and he said, "All of this ends in fighting. All of it. The unplugging, the resistance, the crusade. Words first, sure. But it ends with somebody's hands on somebody. I just want you to know I understand that, even if these others don't. I'm not scared of the part that's coming. I think I've been waiting my whole life for the part that's coming." A pause, and then, softer, the most terrible courtesy: "You can count on me for it."

The hall hummed. The tube flickered. Somewhere outside a truck went by with its windows down, trailing someone else's music.

"Levi," I said. "We wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world. Ephesians six. The war is real, son, and it is spiritual, and the man who forgets the second word loses the first."

"Yes sir," said Levi. Quick. Smooth. The way you agree to terms you've already decided don't apply to you.

I should have sent him home to his mama. I want that in the record too. I stood in that hall with the Spirit's residue still warm on me and the worm beginning to stir, and I looked at this boy who had heard my sermon the way a match hears a striking surface, and something in me said plainly, this one is a different kind of account, and I took his handshake anyway, because a crusade that is three months old and sixty-one dollars rich does not turn away a young man with strong hands and a fire already lit. I told myself I would shepherd it. I told myself fire is what forges. I opened the notebook that night and wrote his name in the back, in the column I keep for accounts not yet come due, and his was the first name in it, and the column has never since been empty.

***

Outside, the plains night had come down cold and enormous. My breath smoked. The stars were out the way they only get out far from the cities, thick as spilled salt, the same stars the Lord walked Abraham out under and said, Count them, if thou be able to number them: so shall thy seed be. The first census in Scripture, promised to a childless old man as pure arithmetic. Even His covenants are bookkeeping. I stood in a parking lot in Oklahoma and counted what I had. Thirty-three souls warmed for an evening. Three soldiers. One mechanic, one archivist, one match. Seventy-two dollars and forty cents, two buttons. One sermon I could not finish, kneeling in front of a deaf boy who could hear me.

And on the ridge east of town, red and patient above the dark, the tower light blinked. On. Off. On. The unsleeping eye of the age, marking its territory against the stars, and the whine swam back up into the bone behind my ear right on schedule, needle-thin, tireless, and the worm turned over in its furrow and asked its question, and I had no verse, and I got in the truck.

The columns don't close, I wrote in the margin that night, by dome light, in the motel lot. They never close. Lord, I am a poor accountant of Your kingdom, thirty-three souls and a button to my name, and the enemy holds the whole grid and every glowing screen from here to both oceans, and the math is a slaughter, and I believe. Credit it to me as righteousness, because there is surely nothing else in these books worth crediting.

Let the crusade begin.

Control. Alt. Amen.
